## ABSTRACT

With laser powder bed fusion (LPBF) of aluminum alloys growing in recent years, the research focus is progressively shifting from processing attempts on mature cast and wrought aluminum alloys to developing new aluminum alloy systems. This paper reports a novel Al–Li alloy specifically developed for the LPBF technology. The methodology of laser glazing on cast parts was applied to approximate solidification conditions of LPBF. Five variants of Al–Cu–Li ingots with different Sc and Zr contents were developed, and the optimized chemical composition was obtained and converted to Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr powder. This newly developed Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr alloy was successfully processed by LPBF with a high building rate and a low Li loss rate. The novel alloy shows excellent tensile properties including yield strength of  $ 482 \pm 1 $ MPa, ultimate tensile strength of  $ 539 \pm 1 $ MPa, and elongation of  $ 8.8 \pm 0.7\% $.

## 1. Introduction

Laser powder bed fusion (LPBF) of aluminum alloys is rapidly evolving in recent years [1–3]. Previous studies mainly focused on cast and wrought aluminum alloys that were originally designed for conventional processes [3,4]. The most widely studied aluminum alloys in LPBF were Al–Si casting aluminum alloys, of which AlSi10Mg attracted the greatest attention [5–7]. Owing to the narrow solidification range and low shrinkage rate, these Al–Si cast aluminum alloys have good LPBF processability. For wrought aluminum alloys commonly applied in automotive and aerospace industries such as the 2xxx, 5xxx, 6xxx, and 7xxx series alloys, attempts at these alloys to LPBF generally result in hot cracking during processing [8–11].

Conventional alloys designed for cast and wrought processes cannot take full advantage of potential benefits brought by the high solidification rate of LPBF. Thus, the research focus of LPBF aluminum alloys is gradually shifting from processing attempts on mature cast and wrought aluminum alloys to developing new aluminum alloy systems to accommodate unique thermomechanical conditions during the LPBF process. The first commercial aluminum alloy specifically designed for the LPBF is probably the Scalmalloy alloy (Al–4.60Mg–0.66Sc–0.42Zr–0.49Mn, in wt%) [12]. The Scalmalloy alloy subjected to LPBF is characterized by fine grains [13] and high tensile properties [14]. Another example of such alloy is Addalloy, an Al–Mg–Zr alloy without Sc [15] that has fine microstructure with a high yield strength and good ductility [16]. LPBF processing results of these commercial alloys point out the importance of refining grains to solve the cracking problem. It is noteworthy that contents of the inoculation elements including Sc and Zr are much higher than conventional cast and wrought aluminum alloys [17–20]. As Scalmalloy and Addalloy are proprietary, it is hard to understand how these alloys were developed.

The major challenge of developing alloys for LPBF is the high cost of producing alloy powders with various compositions and LPBF processing experiments conducted on these powders are time-consuming. One common method is to mix the current wrought aluminum alloy powders with the inoculation powder, such as 2024 +Zr [21,22], 2024 +Ti [23], 7075 +Si [24], and 7075 +ZrH₂ [9]. By controlling the amount of the inoculation powder, it is convenient to adjust the composition of the mixed powder [22,24]. But the melting points of Si, Zr, and Ti are 1414 ^\circC, 1852 ^\circC, and 1668 ^\circC, respectively, which are much higher than the melting points of aluminum alloys (463–671 ^\circC). The large differences in melting point may lead to the uneven melting of the mixed powder. Another method is using the laser of the LPBF machine to melt ingots with designed composition to approximate the solidification conditions of LPBF. This method was applied to evaluate the microstructure and the cracking of Al–Sc–Zr [25], Al–Er–Zr [25], Al–Ce [26], Al–Co [26], and Al–Mn–Sc [27] alloys after laser glazing. Among them, an Al–Mn–Mg–Sc–Zr alloy was designed specifically for LPBF and was

successfully verified by LPBF processing [28]. Thus, this design method can be applied to other alloy development for LPBF.

Al–Li alloys are gradually substituting conventional aluminum alloys in the aerospace industry because of their low density, good corrosion resistance, and combination of high strength and toughness [29,30]. LPBF offers an effective way for fabricating lightweight structures. Thus, applying Al–Li alloys to LPBF has broad application prospects for further weight reduction of aerospace structures. Similar to conventional high-strength aluminum alloys, LPBF Al–Li alloys struggle with high crack susceptibility [31–33]. Our previous study obtained crack-free and fully dense LPBF 2195 Al–Li alloy specimens through optimizing processing parameters [34]. Although high tensile properties were achieved, the narrow crack-free processing window limits further application of the LPBF 2195 alloy. Moreover, the 24% loss rate of Li may cause the main strengthening phase loss.

This study aims at dealing with the high crack susceptibility and the relatively high Li loss rate of the Al–Li alloy produced by LPBF. The casting and laser glazing methodology was applied to approximate the solidification conditions of LPBF. Five variants of Al–Li alloys with distinct Sc and Zr contents were developed and converted to ingots. Laser glazing results provided a reference of the optimized chemical composition specifically for LPBF. The newly developed Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr alloy was successfully produced by LPBF with a combination of high strength and good ductility. Comparing to the LPBF of conventional 2195 Al–Li alloy, this novel alloy performed improved building efficiency and reduced Li loss rate.

## 2. Materials and methods

The contents of Cu and Li elements were determined based on the standard chemical composition of 2195. Our previous study shows that the loss of Li after the LPBF processing is 24% [34], and thus additional Li was added. Sc and Zr were simultaneously selected as the inoculation element for the Al–Cu–Li alloy because of the low lattice misfit between the Al and Al₃(Sc, Zr) lattices [35]. The Al₃(Sc, Zr) precipitate can enhance the nucleation rate due to its core-shell structure [36,37]. The maximum equilibrium solubilities of Sc and Zr in Al are approximately 0.35 wt% [38] and 0.13 wt% [39]. As LPBF is a process with a rapid cooling rate, contents of Sc and Zr were designed to be higher than maximum equilibrium solubilities. ## 3.2. Microstructure of laser glazing samples

To study the influence of different Sc and Zr contents on crack formation, SEM and EBSD characterizations were performed at laser glazing regions and cast regions. Fig. 3 shows backscattered electron images obtained from the longitudinal section of laser glazing samples. The cast region of sample #1 showed the typical dendrite structure. White phases were distributed along the dendrite boundaries. Contrarily, these interdendritic phases were nearly absent in the laser glazing region. The laser melting enabled dissolution of these phases and solutes to go into solution. The high cooling rate of solidification froze most solutes in the Al matrix, which prevented the re-nucleation of these phases. Only a small amount of fine white phases were left in the laser glazing region. Similar phases were observed for Al–Cu alloys fabricated by LPBF and were identified as the Al₂Cu phase [41,42]. For samples inoculated with Sc and Zr, the microstructures of cast regions consisted of fine equiaxed grains. Besides phases located at the intergranular regions, some polygonal phases were inside the equiaxed grains. Similar to sample #1, these intergranular phases were also nearly absent and some fine phases were left in laser glazing regions of samples #2-#5. However, some polygonal phases were left in laser glazing regions of samples #2-#5. Regardless of cast or laser glazing regions, the amount and size of these polygonal phases tended to increase as Sc and Zr contents increase, indicating that these phases might contain Sc and Zr. To examine the element composite of the polygonal phases and intergranular phases, EDS line scanning was performed on sample #5. Fig. 4(a) and (b) present the EDS line scan of sample #5 in the laser glazing region and cast region, respectively. The EDS line scan proved that the polygonal phases were the Al₃(Sc, Zr) phase and intergranular phases were Cu-containing phases. Al₃(Sc, Zr) phases are believed to be the heterogeneous nucleation sites for the Al grains. As contents of Sc and Zr of samples #2-#5 were beyond the maximum equilibrium solubilities in Al, Al₃(Sc, Zr) phases were coarsened and left inside the Al grains in cast regions. These phases left in the laser glazing region are due to the much higher melting point of Al₃(Sc, Zr) than that of pure Al. The near absence of Cu segregation in the laser glazing region indicates that the Cu element segregated at the equiaxed grain boundaries in the cast region was nearly solutionized in the Al matrix in the laser glazing region owing to the high cooling rate during solidification froze most solutes in the Al matrix.

## 3.3. Crack inhibition mechanism

To correlate the grain size with the crack, the Rappaz-Drezet-Gremaud (RDG) criterion [44] was used for estimating the crack initiation. The RDG criterion describes the total pressure drop in the inter-dendritic liquid. If the total pressure drop exceeds a critical pressure, a cavity may form and cause a crack. The total pressure drop is contributed by mechanical deformation and solidification shrinkage, which is given by:

 $$ \begin{aligned}\Delta P_{max}&=\Delta P_{\varepsilon}+\Delta P_{sh}\\&=\frac{180}{\lambda^{2}}\frac{(1+\beta)\mu}{G}\int_{T_{c}}^{T_{l}}\frac{E(T)f_{s}(T)^{2}}{\left(1-f_{s}(T)\right)^{3}}dT+\frac{180}{\lambda^{2}}\frac{\nu\beta\mu}{G}\int_{T_{c}}^{T_{l}}\frac{f_{s}(T)^{2}}{\left(1-f_{s}(T)\right)^{2}}dT\end{aligned} $$ 

 $$ E(T)=\frac{1}{G}\int f_{s}(T)\dot{\varepsilon}(T)dT $$ 

where  $ \Delta P_{max} $ is the total pressure drop,  $ \Delta P_{\varepsilon} $ is the mechanical deformation induced pressure drop,  $ \Delta P_{sh} $ is the solidification shrinkage

induced pressure drop,  $ \beta $ is the shrinkage factor,  $ \mu $ is the dynamic viscosity,  $ \lambda $ is the characteristic microstructural length, G is the thermal gradient,  $ T_{l} $ is the liquidus temperature,  $ T_{c} $ is the coalescence temperature (the temperature near the end of solidification, beyond which the liquid feeding problem becomes trivial because the liquid film broke down into isolated drops),  $ f_{s}(T) $ is the fraction of solid, v is the solidification front velocity,  $ \dot{\varepsilon}(T) $ is the deformation rate of the solid normal to the dendrite growth direction. For the rapidly cooled aluminum alloys, the characteristic microstructural length  $ \lambda $ is fitted as follows [45]:

 $$ \lambda=43.2\dot{T}^{-0.324} $$ 

where  $ \dot{T} $ is the cooling rate and is calculated by  $ G \cdot v $. Generally, the shrinkage factor, the dynamic viscosity, and the deformation rate are assumed to be constant [46] in the RDG criterion. The solidification front velocity, the thermal gradient, and the characteristic microstructural length are related to the heat source and the nature of the materials themselves. Our previous study adopted small solidification front velocity to lower the total pressure drop [34]. In this study, the heat source was the laser and laser glazing parameters of samples for EBSD tests were the same. The physical and thermophysical properties that were calculated by the JMatPro software were very close for all samples as displayed in Table 4. Furthermore, as shown in Fig. 9, the JMatPro software gave the solidification curves of all samples. Inoculating with Sc and Zr had a negligible influence on solidification curves. So the major difference of the RDG criterion calculation was highly dependent on  $ T_{c} $, and the RDG criterion can be simplified as:

 $$ \Delta P_{max}=a\int_{T_{c}}^{T_{l}}A(T)dT+b\int_{T_{c}}^{T_{l}}B(T)dT $$ 

where a and b are constant;  $ A(T) $ and  $ B(T) $ are functions of temperature, which are positive in the range of  $ T_{c} $ to  $ T_{l} $. Increasing the coalescence temperature can decrease values of both the integral terms in Eq. (5), and thus decrease the total pressure drop. As illustrated by the dash lines in Fig. 9, the coalescence temperature can be increased by decreasing the solid fraction. It is believed that the solid fraction determining the coalescence temperature decreases with the reduction of the grain size [47]. Therefore, by refining grains in the laser glazing region, the coalescence temperature is increased, and thus the total pressure drop during the laser processing is reduced and the alloy is more resistant to cracking.

In summary, the Al–4Cu–1.25Li–1Sc–0.5Zr alloy demonstrated good laser processability with fine microstructure and outstanding crack resistance. As such, this novel alloy shows great potential to obtain crack-free and high property samples produced by LPBF.

## 4.2. Li loss rate

ICP-OES results in Table 3 show that the Li contents of powder and LPBF sample of the novel Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr alloy were 1.22% and 1.11%, which were 0.14% and 0.29% higher than AA2195 powder and LPBF sample [34]. Slight differences in Li contents had little effect on the part quality as both LPBF samples achieved the relative density of 99.9%. The Li loss rate of the LPBF Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr sample fabricated with the optimized processing parameters was 9%, which was lower than that of LPBF AA2195 sample [34]. The relatively high Li loss rate of the Al–Li alloy produced by LPBF was mitigated for the novel Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr alloy. The selective loss of Li was caused by the different vaporization rates of different alloying elements. The vaporization rate of alloying element i can be calculated by Langmuir equation [48]:

 $$ J_{i}=\frac{P_{i}}{k_{av}}\sqrt{\frac{M_{i}}{2\pi RT}} $$ 

where  $ J_i $ is the vaporization rate,  $ P_i $ is the equilibrium vapor pressure,  $ M_i $ is the molecular weight,  $ k_{av} $ is the factor used for applying Langmuir equation at atmospheric pressure and determined to be 7.5, R is the gas constant and taken as 8.31 J mol $ ^{-1} $ K $ ^{-1} $, and T is the temperature. The equilibrium vapor pressure is the product of activity and saturated vapor pressure:

 $$ P_{i}=\alpha_{i}P_{i}^{0} $$ 

where  $ \alpha_{i} $ and  $ P_{i}^{0} $ is the activity and saturated vapor pressure. Activity values of Cu, Li, Sc, and Zr were obtained from the JMatPro software. The saturated vapor pressure can be calculated according to reference:

This phenomenon was similar to that the loss rate of Zn in Mg–Zn alloy decreases with the reduced input laser energy density [50].

## 4.3. Microstructure

Fig. 12 shows the EBSD grain boundary maps obtained from the longitudinal section of the LPBF Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr alloy in the as-fabricated condition. Both the LPBF sample and the laser glazing region of sample #5 had ultrafine grains. The LPBF sample displayed a bimodal grain microstructure consisting of fine equiaxed grains and fine columnar grains. Equiaxed grains mainly located at molten pool boundaries and columnar grains located inside molten pools. Similar microstructures were observed in other Sc and Zr modified aluminum alloys fabricated by LPBF [28,51]. The equiaxed region was caused by the high number density of primary Al₃(Sc, Zr) nucleation sites. The columnar region was related to the low number density of nucleation sites associated with the high temperature gradient. Equiaxed grains and the columnar grains were characterized in Fig. 12(b) and (c) with high magnification. Statistical results of the grain size showed that the average equivalent circle diameters of equiaxed grains and columnar grains were 0.6 \mum and 2.5 \mum, respectively. Compared with the average equivalent circle diameter (37.9 \mum) of crack-free 2195 samples produced by LPBF [34], grains of LPBF Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr alloy were much finer.

## 5. Conclusions

In this study, the casting and laser glazing methodology was applied to develop a novel Al–Li alloy specifically for the LPBF technology. The newly developed Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr alloy was evaluated and validated by the LPBF processing. The main conclusions are summarized as follows:

(1) Cracks were absent in the laser glazing regions when inoculating the Al–4Cu–1Li alloy with 0.8Sc–0.4Zr and 1.0Sc–0.5Zr. Grains in the laser glazing regions were gradually finer with the increase of Sc and Zr contents, and became nearly fully equiaxed when inoculating with 1Sc–0.5Zr. Refining grains reduced the total pressure drop in the interdendritic liquid, and thus the alloy was more resistant to cracking.

(2) The optimized chemical composition was converted to powder with a chemical composition of Al–3.89Cu–1.22Li–0.98Sc–0.43Zr. The samples fabricated with the optimized processing parameters (laser power of 300 W, scanning velocity of 1200 mm/s, hatch spacing of 0.1 mm, and layer thickness of 40  $ \mu $m) achieved a densification level of 99.9% with the absence of cracks and a Li loss rate of 9%. The microstructure of the newly developed alloy sample fabricated by the LPBF consisted of fine equiaxed grains and fine columnar grains with the average equivalent circle diameters of 0.6  $ \mu $m and 2.5  $ \mu $m.

(3) The newly developed alloy LPBF sample showed a combination of high yield strength of  $ 482 \pm 1 $ MPa, high ultimate tensile strength of  $ 539 \pm 1 $ MPa, and good elongation of  $ 8.8 \pm 0.7\% $. The YS increment of the newly developed Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr alloy compared to the 2195 alloy was mainly due to the substantial grain refinement.

In summary, an Al–Cu–Li–Sc–Zr alloy was successfully developed specifically for the LPBF with high tensile properties, a high building rate, and a low Li loss rate. Future research will be carried out on the precipitation behavior and the post heat treatment response of this novel alloy.
