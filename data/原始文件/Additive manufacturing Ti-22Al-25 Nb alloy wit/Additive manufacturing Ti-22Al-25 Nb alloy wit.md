## ABSTRACT

Additive manufacturing of TiAl alloys remains challenging due to their poor high-temperature tensile properties. In this work, electron beam powder bed fusion (PBF-EB) was applied to fabricate Ti-22Al-25 Nb alloy with a high pre-heating temperature (830^\circC), achieving a breakthrough in improving the tensile properties of TiAl alloys by additive manufacturing. The microstructure and tensile properties of Ti-22Al-25 Nb fabricated by laser beam powder bed fusion (PBF-LB) and PBF-EB were investigated. Excellent tensile strength (803 MPa) at 650^\circC was obtained by PBF-EB, which was attributable to the precipitation and growth of the \sigmaphase at a suitable pre-heating temperature. An in-depth microstructure analysis of the deformation and fracture mechanisms of the as-printed samples was performed via Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), Electron Backscatter Diffraction (EBSD) and Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM). The area fraction of the \sigmaphase in the PBF-EB sample was approximately 6 times higher than that of the PBF-LB sample. The PBF-EB sample exhibited transgranular fracture at 650 ^\circC, in contrast to the PBF-LB samples. The shape and quantity of the \sigmaphase and \beta phase played an important role in improving the tensile properties of the Ti-22Al-25 Nb alloys at high temperature.

## 1. Introduction

In recent years, a variety of heat-resistant materials processed by additive manufacturing (AM) technology have been studied in aeroen-gines, rockets, satellites and other products. As lightweight structural materials, TiAl-based alloys have been used for their excellent high-temperature corrosion resistance and high-temperature tensile properties [1,2], which can be used for the last two low-pressure turbine blades of the engine below 650 ^\circC. The \beta-stabilized TNM alloy (TNM = TiAl–Nb–Mo), a 3nd or 4th generation of TiAl-alloys, is difficult to be widely applied due to its tensile strength being limited to 800 MPa at 600–750^\circC [3–5]. Therefore, researchers have developed the 4th generation of TiAl-based alloys, represented by Ti–47.5Al–5.5 Nb–0.5 W (\gamma-TiAl), to improve the material’s high-temperature strength. However, the applications of \gamma-TiAl based alloys are greatly inhibited, as their tensile strength is still approximately 800 MPa at 600–750^\circC [6]. Ti₂AlNb-based intermetallics (Ti-22Al-25 Nb, at%), a new generation of TiAl-based alloys with only 60 % of the density of Ni-based alloys, have been proposed to replace Ni-based superalloys due to the strengthening effect of the orthorhombic phase (\sigmaphase) in the temperature range of 600–750^\circC [7]. Ti-22Al-25 Nb consists of the following three phases: \beta/B_{2} (BCC), a matrix phase with good plastic deformation ability; O (orthorhombic), the main high-temperature strengthening phase; and \alpha₂ (HCP), a brittle phase [8]. The \sigma-phase grains tend to nucleate at the grain boundary of the \beta phase with Burgers orientation relationships [9–12]. As a disordered structure, a large amount of the \beta phase is beneficial to improve the plasticity of as-printed alloy due to the increase slip systems [13].

At present, Ti-22Al-25 Nb is mainly formed by casting and forging, making it difficult to produce parts with complex structures [14,15]. Therefore, the AM technique has been rapidly developed, which will change the future of material forming. Although powder metallurgy can achieve near-net-shape, the tensile properties are usually unsatisfactory due to composition segregation [16,17]. Conventional laser deposition always produces coarse grain sizes and the brittle  $ \alpha_{2} $ phase, resulting in poor tensile properties [18]. Laser beam powder bed fusion (PBF-LB) has

been used to obtain fine grains and inhibit the generation of the brittle  $ \alpha_{2} $ phase, showing excellent room-temperature (RT) tensile properties [19]. However, in one study, the Ti-22Al-25 Nb alloy exhibited poor yield strength at 650^\circC due to the low content of the strip-shaped \sigmaphase, which indicated that the area fraction of the strip-shaped \sigmaphase played an important role in improving high-temperature tensile properties [8]. Jia et al. [20] reported that the area fraction of the \sigmaphase improved from 36.7 % to 47.5 % when the sintering temperature was increased from 950^\circC to 1200^\circC, which was also concluded by Chen et al. [21]. Jia et al. [22] discovered that the area fraction of the \sigmaphase increased after solution treatment of hot extrusion samples, which indicated that the lower cooling rate contributed to the precipitation and growth of the \sigmaphase from the B_{2} phase. However, the  $ \alpha_{2} $ phase readily forms during heat treatment, which may be not conducive to the tensile properties of Ti-22Al-25 Nb [8]. The precipitation of the \sigmaphase depends on the solidification time of liquid metal at the \sigma-phase transition temperature [23]. Therefore, if the ambient temperature is set in the precipitation temperature range of the \sigmaphase during AM, it is possible to promote the precipitation and growth of a large amount of \sigmaphase.

At the same time, a large number of research results have indicated that temperature and microstructure strongly affect tensile properties. Lei et al. [24] found that coarse B_{2} phase in the weld zone exhibited quasi-cleavage fracture at RT, but the fracture strength decreased rapidly at 650^\circC, resulting in intergranular fracture. Lu et al. [25] found that samples with O + B_{2} and  $ \alpha_{2} $ + B_{2} microstructures exhibited excellent elongation at RT, whereas the elongation decreased sharply near 650^\circC, which was attributed to the precipitation of a large number of fine needle-shape O phases. Huang et al. [26] found that the elongation of a sample with a O + B_{2} microstructure increased significantly during the tensile process from 870 to 990^\circC, but the strength decreased rapidly at the same time. Therefore, it is worth studying whether the special microstructure (with the precipitation of a large number of the \sigmaphase) of electron beam powder bed fusion (PBF-EB) samples can overcome the problem of rapid decrease of strength and elongation at high temperature.

In this work, a high pre-heating temperature (830^\circC, near the initial precipitation temperature of the \sigmaphase) was designed to fully precipitate the strip-shaped \sigmaphase from the B_{2} phase during the AM of Ti-22Al-25 Nb alloy by PBF-EB. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, no research has been done in this area. The as-printed samples were manufactured with an area fraction ratio of \sigmaphase to \beta phase of approximately 1:1, resulting in an excellent high-temperature tensile strength. The corresponding microstructural and phase composition were characterized using advanced microstructural characterization techniques. This new technology allows us to achieve great improvements in the high-temperature tensile properties of TiAl-based alloys during AM.

## 3. Results and discussion

## 3.1. Porosity, phase constitution, microstructure and crystallography of PBF-LB and PBF-EB samples

The micromorphology of the Ti–22Al–25 Nb power used for PBF-LB and PBF-EB is shown in Fig. 3(a). In Fig. 3(c) and 3(d), the average particle sizes of 39.17  $ \mu $m and 59.03  $ \mu $m were used for PBF-LB and PBF-EB, respectively. As shown in Fig. 3(b), the O and  $ \beta $ phases were detected in the powders and samples processed by PBF-LB and PBF-EB without the  $ \alpha_{2} $ phase, indicating that the precipitation of the brittle  $ \alpha_{2} $ phase was inhibited during high-speed cooling. The diffraction peaks of the PBF-LB samples were consistent with those of the powders. However, the number of diffraction peaks of the \sigmaphase increased in the PBF-EB samples, indicating that a large amount of O phases precipitated and grew from the B_{2} phase, as shown in Fig. 2. Compared with the PBF-LB XRD data, the diffraction peaks of the O and  $ \beta $ phases in the PBF-EB samples shifted slightly to lower 2 $ \theta $ values, implying that the preferred growth orientation of the O and  $ \beta $ phases changed at the high preheating temperature. To investigate the pore features of the as-printed samples, micro-CT was employed, with relative densities (PBF-LB, 99.94 %; PBF-EB, 99.20 %) shown in Fig. 3(e) and 3(f). The presence of some pores may increase the potential energy barrier by hindering grain boundary migration in the PBF-EB sample, which is beneficial to controlling the growth of grain size. However, the presence of numerous pores can reduce the continuity of the as-printed sample and concentrate stress. Cracks tend to propagate along these pores, forming larger cracks. As a result, the sample breaks at lower strains, which corresponds to degraded tensile properties. Compared with horizontal direction, these fusion defects would further deteriorate the tensile properties due to the decrease of the continuity between the layers if the tensile sample was loaded along the BD.

Fig. 4 shows the EBSD, TEM, and SEM analyses of the as-printed PBF-LB and PBF-EB samples. Microstructural differences between the PBF-LB and PBF-EB samples were identified, with the \sigmaphase dispersed in the  $ \beta $ phase matrix, as seen in Fig. 4(a) and 4(b). However, the area fraction of the \sigmaphase in the PBF-EB samples (42.3 %) was approximately six times more than that of the PBF-LB samples (7.34 %), as shown in Table 2. In addition, to further confirm the existence of the O and the  $ \beta $ phases, TEM coupled with selected area electron diffraction (SAED) was also conducted as shown in Fig. 4(c)–(h). The results showed that only the O and  $ \beta $ phases were retained in the as-printed samples, which was consistent with the XRD test results. Based on the TEM images, the average grain diameter of the \sigmaphase in the PBF-EB samples was approximately 10 times more than that of the PBF-LB samples, as shown in Fig. 4(c) and 4(d), and Table 2. TEM energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) indicates that Al was enriched in the \sigmaphase, whereas Nb was enriched in the  $ \beta $ phase, as shown in Fig. 4(i)–(l). Therefore, Al element diffused from the  $ \beta $ phase to the \sigmaphase during the precipitation of the \sigmaphase from the  $ \beta $ phase. 3.2. Formation mechanism of  $ \beta $-phase and \sigma-phase grains during PBF-LB and PBF-EB

In general, the change of microstructure characterization is closely related to formation mechanism of the grains. Zhou et al. investigated the formation mechanism of O and  $ \beta $ phases in the Ti–Al–Nb system, and proposed the sequence  $ L - B_{2} - B_{2} + O - \beta + O $ [19]. Based on the pseudo-binary phase diagram for Ti-22Al-xNb (at%) in Fig. 2, the B_{2} phase first nucleates from the liquid phase via solidification during the PBF-LB process, in which the primary decomposition product of the  $ \alpha_{2} $ phase is suppressed by the high cooling rate. The \sigmaphase is mainly precipitated from the B_{2} phase at approximately 830 ^\circC, whereas the B_{2} phase is transformed into the  $ \beta $ phase by the order–disorder transition at approximately 750 ^\circC. Compared with the PBF-LB process, the \sigmaphase can be fully precipitated from the B_{2} phase and then has sufficient time to grow due to the high pre-heating temperature of 830 ^\circC used during the PBF-EB process. This is the main reason for the presence of a large amount of \sigmaphase in the PBF-EB samples (Fig. 4(b)).

The microstructure of the  $ \beta $ phase in the PBF-EB samples exhibited an equiaxed morphology in different directions, which was different from the PBF-LB samples due to the growth mode of the grains (Figs. 5(a), 5(b), 5(g), and 5(h)). in Fig. 7(a). The grain growth rate (v) and grain radius (R) around the molten pool can be expressed as [33]:

 $$ \nu=\nu_{s}\cos\theta $$ 

 $$ R=\nu(t-\tau) $$ 

where  $ v_{s} $ is the electron beam scanning speed,  $ \theta $ is the angle between the electron beam scanning direction and the direction perpendicular to the molten pool boundary, t is the time of grain growth,  $ \tau $ is the inoculation time of crystal nucleus formation ( $ t > > \tau $), and  $ \tau $ can be neglected.

In Fig. 7(b), the values of  $ \theta $ at the center line and fusion line are  $ 0^{\circ} $ and  $ 90^{\circ} $, respectively. Therefore, the growth rate at the fusion line should be zero (i.e., the minimum), whereas that at the center line can reach the maximum value. On the other hand, t at center line is much higher than that of the fusion line due to the difference of the temperature gradient (G), resulting in R being much smaller at the fusion line than at the center line. Based on previous finite element model calculations [34], the G of PBF-LB is approximately  $ 10^{7} $ K/m. After modifying the parameters of the finite element model, the G of PBF-EB is approximately  $ 10^{5} $ K/m under a pre-heating temperature of  $ 830^{\circ} $C. During the PBF-EB AM process, the G values of the grains at the fusion line decrease greatly due to the higher pre-heating temperature of the substrate than that in PBF-LB, resulting in an increase in the time of grain growth (t), which is beneficial to the growth of R for equiaxed grains (Fig. 7(c)). Although increasing beam velocity can decrease t, the effect of changing the pre-heating temperature on t is greater, as manifested by the rapid decrease in G under a high pre-heating temperature.

The grain growth along the BD depends on the solidification conditions (G and solidification velocity (V)) during AM [35–38]. The epitaxial growth of large columnar grains can be generated and pass through several molten pool boundaries during PBF-LB (Figs. 5(g) and 7(d)), which is different from that in PBF-EB (Figs. 5(h) and 7(e)). The equation of the model on the formation of the equiaxed grain through promoting the columnar to equiaxed transition (CET) is shown below [39]:

 $$ G=\frac{1}{n+1}.3\sqrt{\frac{-4\pi}{3\ln(1-\phi)}}\cdot N_{0}^{1/3}\cdot\Delta T_{actual}\cdot\left(1-\frac{\Delta T_{n}^{n+1}}{\Delta T_{actual}^{n+1}}\right) $$ 

 $$ \Delta T_{actual}=(a\cdot V)^{1/n} $$ 

where n and a are material-dependent parameters,  $ N_{0} $ is the nucleation density,  $ \Phi $ is the volume fraction of equiaxed grains,  $ T_{n} $ is the nucleation undercooling,  $ T_{actual} $ is the actual undercooling at the dendrite tip. Eq. (5) is obtained by substituting Eq. (4) into Eq. (3).

 $$ \frac{G^{n}}{V}=a\left[3\sqrt{\frac{-4\pi N_{0}}{3\ln(1-\phi)}}\cdot\frac{1}{n+1}\right]^{n} $$ 

As seen in Eq. (5),  $ \Phi $ depends on  $ G^{n}/V $. The evolution of the solidification conditions G and V during melt pool solidification is shown in Fig. 7(f) [39,40]. In a study by Gäumann et al., when the pre-heating temperature increased from 20 ^\circC to 1000 ^\circC, G decreased greatly from  $ 10^{6} $ K/m to  $ 10^{5} $ K/m, whereas V decreased only slightly and remained near  $ 10^{-2} $ m/s, indicating that the change of pre-heating temperature had a more significant effect on  $ G^{n} $ than V [40]. Compared with PBF-LB, PBF-EB greatly decreases the G of grains and increases  $ \Phi $, resulting in the equiaxed morphology of the  $ \beta $ phase grains. The above-mentioned factors explain why the PBF-EB samples exhibited an equiaxed morphology in different directions, leading to the highly non-conventional features of the Ti-22Al-25 Nb alloy.

3.4. Deformation analysis of the \sigmaphase and  $ \beta $ phase

Generally, the plastic deformation of crystalline materials depends on mobile dislocations. ## 3.5. Fracture mechanism of the as-printed samples at 650 ^\circC

To further analyze the relationship between the microstructure and the fracture behavior of the as-printed samples, EBSD, KAM, and SEM maps near the fractures of the PBF-LB and PBF-EB samples were obtained at 650 ^\circC, as seen in Fig. 10. Figs. 10(b) and 10(c) show the relationship between crystal orientation and color of the O and the \beta phases. The \beta-phase grains did not exhibit obvious deformation at 650 ^\circC, as shown in Fig. 10(d). Combined with the SEM images of the fracture surface in Fig. 10(e) and the secondary crack near the fracture surface in Fig. 10(f), cracks appeared along the grain boundary of the \beta phase in this region, exhibiting brittle intergranular fracture characteristics. This result indicated that a dramatic decline in the binding force of the grain boundaries and the decreased pinning effect of the nano–\sigmaphase in the grain boundaries lowered the tensile properties sharply at 650 ^\circC. Therefore, the grain boundary of the \beta phase broke before the slip deformation of the \beta phase, and no obvious plastic deformation occurred. Most of the fracture areas were typical intergranular brittle fracture with obvious cleavage facets, which were significantly different from the RT fracture (Figs. 7(b) and 7(c)). At 650 ^\circC, the strengthening effect of the large number of strip-shaped O phases on the \beta phase of the PBF-EB sample was slightly weakened based on the larger deformation of \beta phase compared with that at RT (Fig. 5(b)), but the sample still exhibited a high pinning effect, as shown in Fig. 10(g)–(i) and Fig. 8(a). As seen in Fig. 8(a), the elastic modulus at high temperatures was lower than that at RT according to the slope of the tensile curve, indicating that Ti-22Al-25 Nb softened at 650 ^\circC. At the same time, the slip capacity of the \beta phase was improved due to the softening of the \beta phase at high temperature (Fig. 10(g)), and the alloy exhibited good plasticity compared with PBF-LB, as shown in Fig. 8(e). A large number of dimples were generated on the fracture surface, indicating that the plastic deformation ability of the PBF-EB sample was improved (Fig. 8(e)). Therefore, the fracture mechanism of the PBF-EB sample were determined to be transgranular fracture. According to the study of the deformation mechanism of the as-printed samples, the interfacial bonding strength of the PBF-LB grains rapidly decreased due to the effect of high temperature (650 ^\circC), resulting in fracture at the \beta-phase grain boundary. However, the PBF-EB sample exhibited transgranular fracture under the strengthening effect of a large amount of \sigmaphase, proving that the increase of volume of the \sigmaphase can enhance the interfacial bonding strength of the \beta-phase grain boundary.

## 4. Conclusions

The present work attempted, for the first time, to apply a high pre-heating PBF-EB method to manufacture Ti-22Al25Nb alloy. The microstructure and the tensile properties of the Ti-22Al25Nb alloy made by PBF-EB were investigated and compared with those made by the conventional PBF-LB process. PBF-EB enabled the AM of the Ti-22Al-25 Nb alloy and indicated a unique microstructure, phase constitution, deformation behaviors, and excellent high-temperature tensile properties. The following major conclusions were obtained:

(1) The present study is the first to prepare Ti-22Al-25 Nb alloy using the PBF-EB system, which exhibited excellent high-temperature tensile strength, with a UTS of 803 MPa at 650^\circC.

(2) Microscopic characterization revealed that the as-printed samples contained the \sigmaphase and  $ \beta $ phase with an area fraction of approximately 1:1. The larger area fraction of the \sigmaphase was the crucial factor contributing to the superior high-temperature tensile properties of the sample, which was closely related to the interaction energy between the \sigmaphase and  $ \beta $ phase at high temperature.

(3) The lower cooling rate facilitated the precipitation and growth of \sigmaphase without increasing the grain size of the  $ \beta $ phase. In other words, the probability of \sigmaphase precipitating from  $ \beta $ phase in PBF-EB sample was higher than that in PBF-LB, resulting in a more uniform microstructure in PBF-EB-manufactured materials. At the same time, the high bonding strength ( $ |E_{O-\beta}| $) between the \sigmaphase and  $ \beta $ phase indicated that the as-printed sample with a large amount of \sigmaphase, exhibited high stability at  $ 650^{\circ} $C. These mechanisms resulted in a more stable structure to hinder deformation at  $ 650^{\circ} $C in PBF-EB.

(4) Two factors mainly affected the tensile properties of the PBF-LB and PBF-EB samples: the shape and quantity of the \sigmaphase and  $ \beta $ phase, and dislocation density. This study suggests the potential application of PBF-EB with a high preheating temperature for Ti-22Al-25 Nb fabrication. At the same time, the microstructural evolution and mechanical properties at different preheating temperatures were also worth studying in future work, which will change the shape and volume fraction of the \sigmaphase and  $ \beta $ phase. Based on the current research, we speculate that the precipitation of a large number of the \sigmaphase will improve the high temperature mechanical properties of the as-printed sample at a higher preheating temperature. In addition, further research is necessary to investigate other key mechanical properties. Improving the metal vapor porosity in PBF-EB samples through subsequent heat treatment and optimizing process parameters is also a worthwhile area of investigation.
