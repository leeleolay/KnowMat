## ABSTRACT

The high-temperature applications of aluminum alloys are constrained by their poor thermal stability, high creep susceptibility, and limited strength at elevated temperatures. Traditional dispersion strengthening has encountered inherent limitations in overcoming these challenges. Here, we introduce a bioinspired nanoscale confinement strategy realized by engineering a continuous three-dimensional crystalline-amorphous interpenetrating network structure, reminiscent of those found in natural biological materials. This strategy is implemented in an additively manufactured aluminum alloy, providing stringent spatial confinement that effectively impedes dislocation motion, grain-boundary migration, and atomic diffusion. In addition to good printability, the as-printed alloy achieves ultrahigh strength at room temperature to elevated temperatures, superior creep resistance, and outstanding thermal stability – a synergistic combination of properties that markedly outperforms previously reported materials. This work demonstrates the concept of strengthening materials by utilizing a continuous nanoscale amorphous network, rather than dispersed particles, through harnessing the nanoscale confinement effect inspired by Nature.

## Introduction

Owing to their high strength-to-weight ratio and excellent corrosion resistance, aluminum alloys are extensively used in fields such as aviation, aerospace, and transportation [1], playing a vital role in enhancing energy efficiency and reducing carbon emissions. However, aluminum alloys exhibit poor thermal resistance, especially when compared to steels and titanium alloys – two other widely used metallic structural materials. They suffer from a significant loss of strength with increasing temperature, are highly susceptible to creep at elevated temperatures, and possess poor microstructural thermal stability [2]. These factors present a major bottleneck, severely restricting the application of aluminum alloys in high-temperature environments. Conventional approaches to enhancing the thermal resistance of aluminum alloys, as well as other metallic materials, principally rely on introducing a high density of finely dispersed second-phase particles into the metal matrix, either through alloy design coupled with heat treatment to induce in situ precipitation [3,4], or through external addition via techniques such as powder metallurgy [5,6]. The particles improve material performance through two primary mechanisms: (i) serving as obstacles to dislocation motion, for example, by enforcing dislocation bypass through the Orowan mechanism [7]; (ii) pinning grain boundaries, i.e., via the Zener mechanism [8], to suppress grain-boundary migration and grain coarsening, thereby enhancing microstructural stability.

However, this traditional strategy faces substantial limitations. First, the enhanced mobility of dislocations and grain boundaries with increasing temperature can allow them to move through the gaps between particles [9], thereby significantly diminishing the spatial constraint imposed by these discontinuous reinforcements. In this scenario, the particles function more like a loosely constructed fence rather

than a solid wall. Second, the second phase, particularly that which precipitates in situ, is susceptible to Ostwald ripening at elevated temperatures [10]. In this process, smaller particles dissolve due to their higher surface energy, while larger particles grow, leading to an increase in particle spacing and a reduction in particle number density. This phenomenon weakens the strengthening effect of the second phase and undermines the material's thermal resistance. Moreover, the spatial discontinuity of the second-phase particles hinders direct stress transfer between them, thereby limiting the full realization of their strengthening potential. Stress concentrations are also likely to develop around the particles, promoting plastic deformation of the matrix, as well as void nucleation and microcrack initiation [11].

In contrast to the dispersion strengthening mechanisms typical of synthetic materials, biological materials that evolved in Nature, such as nacre and tooth, often consist of constituents that are not strictly segregated into a second phase and a matrix. Instead, these constituents maintain individual continuity at micro-, nano-, and even smaller scales within three-dimensional space, interwoven to form an interconnected, three-dimensional network composite structure [12]. In particular, constituents with relatively low content and small thickness in various biological materials – often referred to as interfacial phases – typically exist in an amorphous state, which affords excellent spatial continuity without internal interfacial separation [13–15]. Representative examples include the continuous amorphous proteinaceous organic phase between aragonite platelets in nacre [15], as well as the amorphous calcium phosphate found between hydroxyapatite nanofibers in mammalian tooth enamel [16]. The three-dimensional crystalline-amorphous interpenetrating network imposes stringent spatial confinement at the nanoscale and even smaller length-scales, effectively restricting the evolution and propagation of internal damage within individual constituents, while concurrently enabling efficient stress transfer both between different constituents and within each constituent. Through this mechanism, natural biological materials acquire remarkable mechanical properties and exceptional structural stability, not only during growth and metabolic processes within complex physiological environments, but also under harsh, and even extreme, service conditions. Indeed, certain biological materials, such as shells found near underwater volcanos [17], are capable of enduring severe environments by virtue of these structures.

Drawing inspiration from Nature, we propose a new strategy for high-temperature strengthening of materials, utilizing nanoscale confinement through the in situ formation of a continuous crystalline-amorphous nanoscale network composite structure. This concept is realized using a high-solute-content aluminum alloy as a demonstration platform, taking advantage of the high cooling rates afforded by 3D printing and the nanoscale solute redistribution at the solidification front, which induces substantial compositional undercooling and provides a strong thermodynamic driving force for amorphous phase formation. The aluminum alloy developed via this strategy exhibits outstanding mechanical properties at both room and elevated temperatures, achieving a room-temperature ultimate tensile strength (~676 MPa) among the highest reported for 3D-printed aluminum alloys, while demonstrating specific strengths that even surpass those of the Ti-6Al-4V titanium alloy up to 300^\circC. It also displays highly sluggish creep behavior at elevated temperatures up to 400^\circC, with creep rates reduced by one or more orders of magnitude compared to other aluminum alloys, while retaining over 80% of its initial strength after prolonged high-temperature exposure (100 h at 300^\circC). This new strategy fundamentally contrasts with conventional dispersion strengthening, offering promising avenues and opportunities for the development of advanced heat-resistant metallic materials.

## Results

To promote the formation of a nanoscale continuous amorphous network, we selected the classical heat-resistant aluminum alloy FVS0812 (Al-8.5Fe-1.3V-1.7Si, wt.%), which is characterized by a high solute content and a steep liquidus slope [18]. These attributes lead to a pronounced tendency for compositional undercooling during solidification [19], thereby providing a favorable thermodynamic driving force for amorphous phase formation. Laser powder bed fusion (LPBF), a widely used additive manufacturing technique, was implemented to fabricate the alloy due to its high cooling rate ( $ >10^{5} $ K/s), which facilitates non-equilibrium solidification. Its high-dimensional accuracy and near-net-shape forming capability also enable the integrated manufacturing of complex components. In addition, nanoscale  $ TiB_{2} $ particles (50–100 nm) were introduced as precursors for nucleating agents, which react in situ with the alloy to form lattice-matched nucleants. These nucleants facilitate heterogeneous nucleation of primary crystals and inhibit excessive columnar growth, thereby suppressing hot tearing and cracking during the forming process [20,21].

The  $ TiB_{2} $ nanoparticles were mixed with the alloy powders through mechanical stirring in anhydrous ethanol. A comparison of the morphology (Fig. 1a, b) and x-ray diffraction (XRD) spectra of the powders before and after mixing (Fig. S_{1}) indicates that the  $ TiB_{2} $ nanoparticles were successfully incorporated into the powders and uniformly decorated the powder surface. As depicted in Fig. 1c, d, the LPBF-fabricated alloy without  $ TiB_{2} $ nanoparticle incorporation displays a coarse columnar grain structure, with an average grain size of 7.3  $ \mu $m. In contrast, the alloy containing  $ TiB_{2} $ nanoparticles exhibits a microstructure dominated by equiaxed grains with subtle columnar grains, with the grain size significantly refined to approximately 1.6  $ \mu $m, about one-fifth of the original size.

The alloy primarily comprises a fusion zone (FZ) which constitutes the majority of the molten pool, accounting for approximately 80%, along with a heat-affected zone (HAZ) located near the pool boundary (Fig. 1e). The latter is formed due to the reheating of previously solidified material by subsequent laser passes (it is absent in the topmost layer that has not experienced such reheating, as shown in Fig. S_{2}). A small amount (approximately 1%) of irregularly shaped precipitates, with sizes ranging from 1 \mum to 2 \mum, is present in both regions. Compositional analysis identifies these precipitates as the  $ Al_{8}Fe_{2}Si $ phase, a common constituent in the FVS0812 alloy that typically forms in regions subjected to relatively low cooling rates [22]. Although this precipitate phase may contribute to strengthening, its low volume fraction indicates that its influence on the overall mechanical properties is limited and unlikely to constitute the dominant origin of the material’s mechanical performance. In the dominant fusion zone (Fig. 1f and Fig. S_{3}), Fe and Si are enriched along the grain boundaries between \alpha-Al grains as well as between the cellular dendrites within individual grains, forming a continuous network spanning two distinct length-scales corresponding to intergranular and intragranular regions, respectively. These boundaries are spatially interwoven with the continuous cellular structures formed by dendritic growth, creating an architecture that imparts a confinement effect analogous to that observed in natural biological materials, which will be elaborated on later. In addition, each grain center contains a nucleation core approximately 50 nm in size, enriched with Ti and V elements. Selected area electron diffraction (SAED) analysis confirms that this core corresponds to the TiV phase (Fig. S_{4}), which forms in situ during the printing process via a reaction between  $ TiB_{2} $ nanoparticles and V in the alloy. TiV possesses an orthorhombic crystal structure and exhibits good lattice matching with the \alpha-Al phase along the  $ (100)_{TiV}/(100)_{Al} $ direction, featuring a lattice mismatch of 7.94% (Fig. S_{5}). This lattice compatibility helps reduce the energy barrier for heterogeneous nucleation of primary \alpha-Al relative to homogeneous nucleation, thereby facilitating its nucleation during solidification. Moreover, during crystal growth, high concentrations of solute elements such as Fe and V are rejected from the \alpha-Al crystals and accumulate at the solid–liquid interface front, forming a solute-enriched layer that drags the interface migration [23], thereby impeding the growth of \alpha-Al crystals.

As a result, the reduced susceptibility to hot cracking endows the alloy with excellent printability, enabling it to be directly printed into large-sized ( $ \sim $50 mm, limited by the build chamber dimensions) and complex-shaped demonstrator components, such as heat-resistant parts including pistons, turbines, and blades for aerospace engines (Fig. 1g). Therefore, this approach presents a new paradigm for fabricating heat-resistant aluminum alloys, distinct from traditional casting and powder metallurgy [26,27], by integrating the advantages of mold-free processing, enhanced design flexibility, and near-net-shape manufacturing.

As shown in Fig. 2a, the as-printed alloy demonstrates outstanding tensile mechanical properties at both room and elevated temperatures. At room temperature, it yields at  $ 523 \pm 10 $ MPa, followed by pronounced work hardening, reaching an ultimate tensile strength (UTS) of  $ 676 \pm 4 $ MPa, with an elongation to fracture of approximately 5%. Its UTS surpasses that of most existing additively manufactured aluminum alloys by at least 100 MPa [28–33] (Fig. 2b). It is noteworthy that commonly used high-strength aluminum alloys, such as the 2xxx and 7xxx series, typically exhibit a high susceptibility to hot cracking during additive manufacturing, which limits their formability and mechanical performance [20,21,34]. As the temperature increases, the strength of the alloy gradually declines, as shown in Fig. 2c. Nevertheless, at temperatures below 400^\circC, its UTS exceeds that of previously reported additively manufactured aluminum alloys by at least 180 MPa [35,37–39]; indeed, its yield strength even outperforms the UTS of these reference materials. Moreover, the strength of the 3D-printed FVS0812 alloy is significantly higher than that of the same alloy produced by other processing routes, such as planar flow casting and spray deposition [26,27], thereby clearly demonstrating the advantages of additive manufacturing. Notably, within the temperature range below 300^\circC, the specific UTS (the UTS normalized by density) of the alloy surpasses that of the widely used Ti-6Al-4V titanium alloy [1]. Furthermore, the alloy demonstrates exceptional mechanical stability at elevated temperatures. As shown in Fig. 2d, it exhibits a steady-state creep rate on the order of  $ 10^{-8} $ s $ ^{-1} $ at 300^\circC under applied stresses of 100 MPa and 150 MPa, representing a reduction of one to three orders of magnitude compared to most other aluminum alloys [40–43]. Remarkably, even at 400^\circC, its steady-state creep rate under identical stress conditions is reduced by at least 50% compared to the reference materials tested at 300^\circC (see also Fig. S_{7}). The high strength from room to elevated temperatures, combined with sluggish creep behavior and ease of additive manufacturing, endows the alloy with distinct advantages and promising potential for high-temperature structural applications.

Multiscale structural characterization, from the nanoscale to the atomic level, provides critical insights into the structural origins

underlying the alloy’s exceptional properties. As shown in Fig. 3a, the dendritic cell boundaries in the fusion zone interweave through the  $ \alpha $-Al cells, forming a continuous network approximately 14 nm in width that exerts strong spatial confinement at the nanoscale. The  $ \alpha $-Al cells have an average diameter of approximately 67 nm, significantly smaller than the 200–300 nm typically observed in 3D-printed Al-Si-based alloys [24,35,44]. This refinement is attributed to the impeded redistribution of high-content solute elements and the formation of solute-enriched layers at the cell growth front, which serve to hinder cell growth. Meanwhile, the continuously interwoven cell boundaries also impose spatial constraints that restrict cell growth. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analyses of both ion-thinned and focused ion beam-milled specimens reveal a disordered atomic arrangement at the cell boundaries and the absence of periodic diffraction spots in the corresponding fast Fourier transform (FFT) patterns (Fig. 3b, c and Fig. S_{8}), indicating the amorphous structure of these boundaries. Consistently, the differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) curve exhibits a pronounced exothermic peak during heating (Fig. S_{9}), which is absent in fully crystalline aluminum alloys and can be attributed to the crystallization of the amorphous phase. Moreover, in addition to the characteristic diffraction peaks of  $ \alpha $-Al, the micro-XRD pattern of the as-printed alloy displays a distinct broad diffuse scattering hump in the 2 $ \theta $ range of 45 $ ^{\circ} $–55 $ ^{\circ} $ (Fig. S_{10}), a hallmark of the amorphous phase. Taken together, these results provide compelling evidence for the amorphous nature of the cell boundaries in the alloy. Due to the long-range atomic disorder characteristic of the amorphous structure and its seamless transition with the crystalline phase, the cell boundaries form an atomically uniform, continuous, and dense barrier free of crystalline defects such as grain boundaries or dislocations, thereby providing effective spatial confinement to the cells. The content of amorphous boundaries in the fusion zone was determined to be approximately 38% based on analysis of multiple TEM images.

Aluminum alloys are known for their inherently low propensity to form amorphous phases [45], stemming from the thermodynamic instability of the undercooled liquid and the pronounced stability of the face-centered cubic crystalline phase [46]. To date, despite extensive compositional optimization efforts, such as the addition of high concentrations of rare earth and transition metal elements, the maximum diameter of bulk aluminum-based amorphous alloys produced by rapid solidification remains limited to no more than 2.5 mm [45,47]. Here, we demonstrate the direct construction of a high-volume amorphous phase in a large-scale bulk aluminum alloy via additive manufacturing, where the amorphous phase features a distinctive nanoscale, three-dimensional continuous network interwoven with the crystalline phase.

The formation of such a structure can be interpreted from both thermodynamic and kinetic perspectives. Thermodynamically, the propensity of an alloy to form an amorphous phase is fundamentally governed by the change in Gibbs free energy of a randomly mixed atomic system, which can be evaluated in terms of the chemical mixing enthalpy ( $ \Delta H^{C} $), atomic size mismatch entropy ( $ \Delta S_{\sigma} $), and configurational entropy ( $ \Delta S_{c} $) [48,49]. A more negative  $ \Delta H^{C} $ indicates stronger atomic bonding that stabilizes local ordered structures, thereby enhancing the thermodynamic stability of the system [50]. Nevertheless, an excessively negative  $ \Delta H^{C} $ may increase the tendency for the formation of ordered crystalline phases [48], such as intermetallic compounds.  $ \Delta S_{\sigma} $ and  $ \Delta S_{c} $ denote the entropy changes in the system arising from atomic size differences and configurational disorder, respectively. Larger values of these entropy contributions indicate a higher degree of disorder within the system, leading to a reduction in free energy. Additionally, this increased structural disorder hinders atomic rearrangement from a kinetic perspective, thereby suppressing crystal nucleation and growth.

These parameters can be integrated in the following form to reflect the thermodynamic tendency of the system to form an amorphous phase [49]:

 $$ P_{a}=\Delta H^{C}(\Delta S_{\sigma}/k_{B})(\Delta S_{c}/R) $$ 

where  $ k_{B} $ is the Boltzmann’s constant and R is the gas constant. By this means, we systematically calculated the  $ P_{a} $ values for the Al-Fe-V-Si system, with Ti excluded due to its trace amount. The detailed calculation process is presented in the Supplementary Information and in Table S_{1}. The amorphous-forming ability of the Al-Fe-Si ternary system has been extensively investigated in the literature [51,52], whereas studies on the Al-Fe-V-Si quaternary system remain limited. Given that the V content in the present alloy is minimal – with most V existing in the form of TiV rather than being dissolved in the matrix – and therefore has a negligible influence on the calculated  $ P_{a} $ value, the thermodynamic propensity was then correlated with experimental observations of amorphous phase formation in Al-Fe-Si alloys. The results show that compositions that are capable of forming an amorphous phase predominantly exhibit  $ P_{a} $ values in the range of -0.6 kJ/mol to -0.2 kJ/mol (Fig. S_{12}). When  $ P_{a} $ exceeds -0.2 kJ/mol, the system exhibits poor thermodynamic stability and tends to crystallize into  $ \alpha $-Al phase. Conversely,  $ P_{a} $ below -0.6 kJ/mol indicates a strong propensity toward crystallization to form intermetallic compounds or quasicrystals. Fig. 3g presents the calculated  $ P_{a} $ values for the  $ \alpha $-Al cell and the cell boundary regions based on the measured compositions (corresponding to Fig. 3f). It is evident that the  $ P_{a} $ values of the cell exceed -0.2 kJ/mol, whereas those at the cell boundary fall precisely within the range of -0.6 kJ/mol to -0.2 kJ/mol, thermodynamically confirming their strong propensity for amorphous phase formation. On the other hand, from a kinetic perspective, the extremely high cooling rate of the LPBF process (exceeding  $ 10^{5} $ K/s) markedly suppresses the diffusion and redistribution of the solute elements which are present at high concentrations in the alloy, thereby inhibiting crystallization. The steep liquidus slope of the alloy also promotes substantial compositional undercooling in solute-enriched regions [19], thereby facilitating the retention of an amorphous structure.

Driven by the aforementioned thermodynamic and kinetic factors, the resulting continuous three-dimensional nanoscale crystalline-amorphous interpenetrating network closely parallels the structural motifs characteristic of natural biological materials [12,15]. This similarity transcends mere morphological resemblance (form similarity) and embodies a congruence in the underlying governing principles (conceptual similarity). Such a structure imparts a pronounced nanoscale spatial confinement effect, thereby endowing the alloy with excellent mechanical properties from ambient to elevated temperatures. First, the rigid amorphous network acts as a formidable barrier to dislocation motion [53], hindering dislocation transmission across interfaces while ensuring efficient stress transfer due to its spatial continuity, thereby collectively contributing to the overall strengthening of the alloy. Additionally, the spatially continuous  $ \alpha $-Al cells impart plasticity and enable work-hardening capability. Second, the amorphous network at the cell boundaries imposes stringent spatial constraints on cell growth, suppressing coarsening during solidification or subsequent heating. Consequently, the cells are confined to nanoscale dimensions, resulting in prominent strengthening effect reminiscent of the Hall-Petch relationship [54]. This simultaneously gives rise to a high density of amorphous boundary regions acting to impede deformation. Third, the amorphous boundaries, being geometrically wider than conventional sharp grain boundaries, serve as effective spatial constraints that hinder cell/grain-boundary migration and prevent cell/grain coarsening via Ostwald ripening. In addition, the solute-enriched amorphous phase forms a diffusion-impeding barrier with intrinsically low atomic diffusivity [55], inhibiting grain-boundary creep governed by the Coble mechanism [6] as well as bulk diffusion associated with the Nabarro-Herring mechanism [42]. These effects collectively enhance the alloy’s creep resistance and thermal stability.

The average grain size exhibits only marginal increase to 1.95 \mum after prolonged thermal exposure (Fig. S_{13}), compared to the initial as-printed condition (~1.6 \mum), indicating good thermal stability of the grain structure. The \alpha-Al cells in both regions retain sizes comparable to the as-printed state, averaging approximately 72 nm in diameter. The thermal stability of cells and grains fundamentally arises from the low atomic diffusivity within the nanoscale boundary network, coupled with its spatial confinement effect that restricts cell and grain growth. Notably, the amorphous phase at the cell boundaries in the fusion zone remains largely preserved, maintaining a continuous network structure. ## Discussion

The above results provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the strengthening strategy enabled by bioinspired nanoscale confinement, applicable across a wide temperature range from room temperature to elevated temperatures. This effect is achieved through the formation of a three-dimensional continuous amorphous network interwoven with a crystalline phase, giving rise to a nanoscale crystalline-amorphous interpenetrating network composite structure. Fundamentally distinct from conventional dispersion strengthening, this strategy instead embodies key structural features analogous to those found in biological materials evolved in Nature. The construction of such a structure relies on the establishment of thermodynamic conditions that favor a strong tendency toward amorphous phase formation, alongside kinetic conditions characterized by substantial compositional undercooling and suppressed crystallization. These conditions are synergistically realized through the selection of alloy compositions with high solute element content and the use of an additive manufacturing process involving high cooling rates. Additionally, good printability can be achieved by promoting heterogeneous crystal nucleation through the addition of nanoscale  $ TiB_{2} $ nucleating agents and suppressing grain and cell growth via the redistribution of high-concentration solute elements. This approach eliminates the need for highly expensive Sc element additions which are commonly employed to facilitate heterogeneous nucleation through the in situ formation of nanoscale  $ Al_{3}Sc $ nucleants [10,25].

The nanoscale crystalline-amorphous interpenetrating network enables continuous, seamless stress transfer while imposing stringent nanoscale spatial constraints on dislocation motion. This provides efficient strengthening while avoiding pronounced degradation of plasticity caused by significant stress concentrations typically associated with conventional dispersion-strengthening phases [11,56]. The three-dimensional continuous amorphous network can also form a denser barrier than dispersion-strengthening phases to impede grain-boundary and cell-boundary migration, thereby suppressing thermally driven coarsening of cells and grains. Additionally, the intrinsically low atomic diffusivity of the amorphous boundaries [55], coupled with the diffusion barriers they impose on the crystalline phase, contributes to significantly enhanced creep resistance. These mechanisms synergistically endow the printed alloy with outstanding mechanical properties from room to elevated temperatures, including a UTS exceeding that of most 3D-printed aluminum alloys by at least 100 MPa while maintaining good ductility and work-hardening capability at room temperature, a UTS surpassing that of most 3D-printed aluminum alloys by at least 180 MPa at elevated temperatures up to 400^\circC, and a specific UTS below 300^\circC exceeding that of Ti-6Al-4V alloy. This is accompanied by an extremely low high-temperature creep rate, reduced by one to three orders of magnitude compared to most aluminum alloys under identical conditions, along with excellent thermal stability that retains over 80% of its strength after prolonged thermal exposure at 300^\circC for 100 h. It is noted that a recent study has achieved room-temperature strength exceeding 800 MPa and high elevated-temperature strengths in a 3D-printed Al-Fe-V-Si-Sc alloy [10], relying on the in situ formation of multiscale heterogeneous structures comprising abundant nanoscale amorphous bands and crystalline precipitates, as well as the incorporation of costly Sc element. Nevertheless, this alloy suffers from poor ductility, with elongation below 2% at room temperature and less than 5% at elevated temperatures even at 400^\circC, alongside challenges in printability and printable dimension that require improvement. A direct comparison of the mechanical properties and reported printing dimensions of Al-Fe-V-Si-Sc alloy is provided in Table S_{2}.

The exceptional synergy between the intrinsic properties of the alloy developed in this study and its processing advantages, including good printability, facile fabrication of intricate components, and near-net-shape manufacturing, endows it with remarkable potential for high-temperature structural applications. Furthermore, the bioinspired nanoscale confinement design paradigm introduced here, realized through a nanoscale continuous amorphous network structure, paves a new avenue for enhancing material performance, particularly at elevated temperatures, distinguishing itself from conventional dispersion strengthening.

## Conclusions

In summary, we propose a bioinspired nanoscale confinement strategy for strengthening materials from room to elevated temperatures, which contrasts from conventional dispersion strengthening, and validate its effectiveness in an additively manufactured aluminum alloy. This strategy is realized by the in situ formation of a nanoscale crystalline-amorphous interpenetrating network structure resembling those found in natural biological materials, constructed by leveraging the alloy’s high solute content and the high cooling rate inherent in additive manufacturing. Such a network induces nanoscale confinement effects that effectively suppress dislocation slip, grain-boundary migration, and atomic diffusion. As a result, the alloy achieves an exceptional combination of properties surpassing those of conventional materials, including high room-temperature strength coupled with good ductility and work-hardening capability, excellent high-temperature strengths, superior creep resistance with creep rates orders of magnitude lower than those of counterparts, outstanding thermal stability, and good printability. This strategy delivers high-performance materials for high-temperature structural applications and establishes a new pathway applicable to other systems for enhancing mechanical properties across a wide temperature range.

## Methods

## Fabrication of materials

FVS0812 aluminum alloy powders were produced by gas atomization and had a chemical composition (wt.%) of Al (balance), Fe (8.43%), V (1.33%), Si (1.48%), C (0.018%), S (0.08%), O (0.048%), and N (0.06%). The powders exhibited a particle size range of 15–53 µm, with a median diameter (D₅₀) of 34.3 µm. TiB₂ nanoparticles with diameters ranging from 50 nm to 100 nm were purchased from Yinuo High-Tech Material Development Co., Ltd. The chemical composition (wt.%) was Ti (>68.2%), B (>30.8%), O (<0.40%), C (<0.15%), and Fe (<0.10%). Representative morphologies of the aluminum alloy powders and TiB₂ nanoparticles are presented in Fig. S_{17}. The alloy powders were mixed with 2 wt.% TiB₂ nanoparticles by mechanical stirring in anhydrous ethanol at 1000 rpm for 3 h. The resulting mixture was then dried in a vacuum oven at 60^\circC for 48 h prior to printing.

3D printing via laser powder bed fusion (LPBF) was performed using an EP-M150 Metal 3D Printer (Hangzhou Eplus 3d Tech, China) with both the original FVS0812 powders and those incorporating 2 wt.%  $ TiB_{2} $ nanoparticles. The process was conducted under an argon atmosphere with a laser power of 150 W, a scan speed of 1000 mm/s, a scan spacing of 0.08 mm, and a layer thickness of 0.02 mm. The build platform was preheated to 200^\circC during printing. After fabrication, the printed alloys were allowed to cool to room temperature and were subsequently ultrasonically cleaned in anhydrous ethanol to remove residual powders.

## Microstructural characterization

X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis was carried out using a D8 Discover diffractometer (Bruker, Germany) with Cu K\alpha radiation (\lambda = 0.1542

nm). Micro-XRD analysis was performed using a Bruker D8 Discover x-ray microdiffractometer (Bruker, Germany) with Co K\alpha radiation using an x-ray beam with a 0.5 mm diameter. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) measurement was conducted using a DSC 204 F_{1} Phoenix instrument (Netzsch, Germany) at a constant heating rate of 10 K/min. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) imaging was performed with an Apreo S LoVac microscope (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). Before observation, the surfaces of the printed alloys were mechanically ground with 400–2000 grit SiC papers and polished using a 250 nm SiO₂ suspension. Grain structure characterization was conducted via electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) at an accelerating voltage of 20 kV and a beam current of 13 nA.

Scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) imaging and energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) analysis were performed using an FEI Talos F200X microscope (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) operated at 200 kV and an FEI Themis Z spherical aberration-corrected transmission electron microscope (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). Thin foils of samples were first mechanically ground to a thickness of approximately 60 \mum, followed by ion milling using a Gatan 691 system (Gatan, USA). Additional specimens were prepared by focused ion beam milling using an FEI Scios 2 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) to eliminate possible preparation-induced artifacts. Visualization of the crystalline structures of the \alpha-Al and TiV phases was performed using VESTA (version 3.5.8), and their corresponding electron diffraction patterns were simulated with ReciPro (version 4.889). Lattice matching between the two phases was visualized using the iface viewer module in CryST-Box (version 1.10).

Three-dimensional atom probe tomography (3D-APT) analysis was carried out using a LEAP 5000X system (CAMECA, France) with ultraviolet laser pulsing at a laser energy of 60 pJ, a pulse repetition rate of 100 kHz, and a target evaporation rate of 0.3% per pulse at 50 K. Needle-shaped specimens were prepared by site-specific focused ion beam (FIB) milling. Data reconstruction and quantitative analysis were performed using CAMECA’s AP Suite software (version 6.3.0.90).

## Mechanical testing

Dog-bone-shaped tensile specimens with a gauge section of  $ 10 \times 2 \times 1 $ mm were prepared by precision machining and subsequently polished on both sides using 400–1200 grit SiC papers. Room-temperature tensile tests were conducted on a servo-electric AG-50kNXplus testing system (Shimadzu, Japan) at a strain rate of  $ 5 \times 10^{-4} \, s^{-1} $ until fracture. Digital image correlation (DIC) analysis was performed using VIC-2D software, with the sample surfaces coated in white primer and speckled with black dots to enable strain mapping.

High-temperature tensile testing was performed using an LE5105 mechanical testing system (Lishi Instruments, China) equipped with an environmental chamber. After sealing the chamber, the temperature was increased to the target values of 200^\circC, 250^\circC, 300^\circC, 350^\circC, and 400^\circC, respectively, and held for 20 min to ensure thermal equilibrium prior to testing. The samples were then stretched at a constant strain rate of  $ 5 \times 10^{-4} \, s^{-1} $ until fracture. The mechanical property data from room temperature to elevated temperatures are presented as mean \pm standard deviation in Table S3.

Creep testing was carried out on a GNCJ-30 testing machine (NCS Testing Technology, China) using dog-bone-shaped cylindrical specimens with a gauge diameter of 3 mm and a gauge length of 40 mm. Prior to loading, the samples were heated to the target testing temperatures of 300^\circC and 400^\circC, respectively. Creep deformation was then induced under various stress levels, with strain evolution continuously monitored using a high-temperature extensometer.

Nanoindentation testing was conducted using an FT-I04 Femto-Indenter (FemtoTools, Switzerland) with an indentation depth of 0.1  $ \mu $m and an indent spacing of 1  $ \mu $m. The loading rate was held constant at 0.05  $ \mu $m/s. Each distribution contour map of nanoindentation properties was generated from 2,000 individual data points.
