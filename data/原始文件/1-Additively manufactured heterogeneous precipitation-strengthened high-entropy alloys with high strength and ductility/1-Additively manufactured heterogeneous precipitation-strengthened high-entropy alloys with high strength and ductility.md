## ABSTRACT

Heterogeneous-structured (HS) materials have drawn particular attention due to their extraordinary strength-ductility combinations. Benefiting from the large thermal gradient and high cooling rate, additive manufacturing (AM) enables structural and compositional heterogeneity at the multiscale, which provides new routines for high-performance HS materials. Here, we successfully fabricated a cellular-structured CoNiCrFeAlTi-based high-entropy alloy (HEA) by using the directed-energy deposition (DED) technology. Subsequent thermal treatment produces a distinctly different microstructure consisting of single-phase (face-centered cubic, FCC) interior and dual-phase (FCC + L12) wall, which leads to an ultrahigh tensile strength of  $ \sim $1148 MPa combined with a large ductility of  $ \sim $28 %. We attribute the high strength to the pronounced ordering strengthening from high-density L12-type precipitates in the wall while the large ductility primarily comes from the improved plastic deformation stability via hetero-deformation-induced strain hardening and deformation-induced stacking faults (SFs). Our current work is expected to open up a new area for designing high-performance HS multiple-principal-element alloys.

## 1. Introduction

The increasing demand for highly reliable engineering structures has driven the accelerated design of high-performance metallic structural materials [1–5]. Recently, heterogeneous-structured (HS) materials, as a new class of metallic materials, have created a growing interest from material scientists and engineers [6–8]. Different from traditional strengthening strategies (i.e., the increase in strength is always accompanied by a decrease in ductility), structural heterogeneity has been proven to be effective in overcoming the strength-ductility dilemma, which thus stimulates the accelerated development of numerous HS materials with superior combinations of strength and ductility [9,10]. Of particular interest, additive manufacturing (AM), as an emerging revolutionary technology, has thoroughly changed the scenario of future metal design and manufacturing [11–13]. Several popular methods based on the AM concept have been developed, primarily including powder bed fusion (PBF) and directed energy deposition (DED) [14,15]. It is interesting to note that, among these AM technologies, the large thermal gradient and high cooling rate across a solidifying melt pool can readily produce both structural and compositional heterogeneities. Therefore, the AM technique holds tremendous potential for the design and manufacturing of high-performance HS materials [16–23].

In the past few years, although substantial strong-yet-ductile HS materials have been designed by AM, most efforts are mainly focused on the conventional alloy systems like steels, Ti alloys, and superalloys [16, 21,24–26]. More strikingly, the discovery of high-entropy alloys (HEAs)

based on multiple-principal-element strategy offers an opportunity to develop novel HS materials because of their huge compositional space, complex microstructures, and exceptional mechanical performances [27–31]. Since then, considerable studies have been made to explore the property limit by involving different types of heterogeneous structures (e.g., the multi-scale grain, hierarchical lamellar structure, and gradient-dislocation-cell structure, etc.) in the emerging HEA systems [32–35]. Besides the traditional thermomechanical processing methods, the AM has been used as an efficient alternative to incorporate the unique heterogeneous structures in the newly designed HEAs. For instance, Mu et al. [18] developed an  $ Fe_{28.0}Co_{29.5}Ni_{27.5}Al_{8.5}Ti_{6.5} $ HEA with dislocation-precipitate skeleton architectures using laser PBF (L-PBF) technology and showed a high YS of  $ 790 \pm 30 $ MPa and a large ductility of  $ 28.0 \pm 0.8\% $. Using the same technique, with the introduction of hierarchical structures in terms of grains, cellular substructures, and precipitates in a  $ (\mathrm{CoCrNi})_{94}\mathrm{Al}_{3}\mathrm{Ti}_{3} $ HEA, a high YS of  $ \sim 836.7 $ MPa and a large ductility of 27.4 % was obtained [36]. In contrast to the above PBF used, the DED is suggested to be one of the most promising AM technologies due to its fast processing, low-cost manufacturing, and high flexibility of design [37,38]. However, at the present time, it has not been explored whether such heterogeneous structures can be effectively introduced in the HEA systems via the DED approach. Also, the mechanical properties and the associated deformation mechanisms of DED-HEAs have rarely been reported.

It should be mentioned that, among currently developed HEAs, coherent L12-type precipitate-strengthened HEAs have shown significant promise due to their superb mechanical properties over a wide range of temperatures [39–41]. Based on the thermodynamic calculations (Supplementary Fig. S_{1}), Co $ _{47} $Ni $ _{28} $Cr $ _{16} $Fe $ _{3} $Al $ _{3} $Ti $ _{3} $ (at%) HEA, as a typical L12-strengthened HEA, is particularly chosen as our experimental material. In this study, we successfully printed a novel CoNiCrFeAlTi-based HEA with cellular structures using the DED approach. Subsequently, the microstructures and mechanical behaviors of the AM-fabricated HEAs were systematically investigated. The underlying deformation micro-mechanisms and strain partitioning behaviors were also carefully studied.

## 2. Material and methods

## 2.1. Powder preparation and DED processing

Elemental powders of Co, Ni, Cr, Fe, Al, and Ti were used as the raw materials. The raw materials with an atomic ratio of  $ Co_{47}Ni_{28}Cr_{16}Fe_{3}Al_{3}Ti_{3} $ were mechanically mixed via a three-dimensional mixer. The powder blends with spherical morphologies and the associated elemental distributions (Co, Ni, Cr, Fe, Al, and Ti) are provided in Fig. 1.

During the DED process, fiber laser with a wavelength of 1080 nm and a spot diameter of 1.2 mm was utilized as power source. The powder blends were fed into melt pool on surface of substrate via a coaxial powder feeding system under the protection of argon gas, as shown in Fig. 2a. ## 2.2. Microstructure characterizations

A scanning electron microscopy (SEM, FEI Scios) was employed to observe the microstructural features of the AM-fabricated HEAs. The SEM samples were firstly ground mechanically using SiC papers and then electro-polished in a mixed solution ( $ C_{2}H_{5}OH:HNO_{3} = 3:1 $) with a direct voltage of 20 V at a temperature of  $ -30^{\circ}C $. The microstructures and the associated strain partitioning behaviors were analyzed using electron backscattered diffraction (EBSD). The overall microstructures in XOY, XOZ, and YOZ planes are respectively characterized, as exhibited in Supplementary Fig. S_{2}. Also, a transmission electron microscopy (TEM, JEOL 2100F) equipped with an energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) was applied to identify the phase constituents and chemical compositions. The TEM samples were firstly ground to a thickness of  $ \sim50 \mu m $ and then punched into discs with a diameter of  $ \sim3 \ mm $, followed by ion-milling via a precision ion polishing system (PIPS, Gatan 695). X-ray diffraction (XRD) test was also conducted at a scanning speed of  $ 2^{\circ}/min $ between  $ 20-100^{\circ} $. Moreover, the atom-probe tomography (APT) experiment was conducted using a local electrode atom probe (CAMECA, LEAP 5000 XR) in a voltage-pulsing mode at 70 K with a pulse rate of 200 kHz and a pulse fraction of 20 %. The needle-shaped specimen was prepared using an FEI Scios dual-beam SEM/focused-ion-beam (FIB) instrument. Furthermore, the distribution of pores in the as-built HEA was carried out by the X-ray micro-computed tomography (micro-CT) machine (diondo d2). The densification of the AP-HEA sample is estimated to be about 99.999 % (Supplementary Fig. S_{3}), suggesting a very good processibility.

## 2.3. Mechanical testing

Flat dog-bone-shaped specimens with a gauge length of 12.5 mm and a cross-section dimension of  $ 3.2 \times 1.5 \, mm^2 $ were fabricated. The tensile tests were conducted on a Material Testing System (MTS) machine with a strain rate of  $ 1 \times 10^{-3} \, s^{-1} $ at room temperature.

## 4. Discussion

## 4.1. AM-induced cellular structure

As Fig. 3 demonstrates, the AP-HEA shows a fine cellular structure ( $ \sim $5  $ \mu $m diameter) with the FCC structure. Such a very fine microstructure is primarily ascribed to the intrinsic high cooling rate and large thermal gradient of the AM process [24]. Moreover, it is noteworthy that an evident Ti-rich zone is presented in the wall. Similar enrichment/segregation behaviors at the inter-dendritic and grain boundaries (GBs) have been often found in some other AM-fabricated metallic materials [36,43–45]. Generally, the driving force for a solute atom to segregate to GBs is controlled by the segregation enthalpy ( $ \Delta H^{seg} $). It is equivalent to the internal energy change between the solute atom occupying the GB site and a bulk (intra-grain) site, i.e.,  $ \Delta H^{seg} \approx \Delta E^{seg} = E^{solute, GB} - E^{solute} $,
Fig. 9. Deformation microstructure of the AP-HEA subjected to  $ \sim $30 % plastic strain. (a) Representative TEM image of the mechanical nanotwins. (b) DF-TEM image. (c) Associated SAED pattern along the [110] zone axis highlighting the deformation twins.

bulk [46–48]. A negative  $ \Delta E^{seg} $ contributes to GB segregation. As for our current case, Ni and Co are considered as two major elements. Here we particularly focus on the segregation energies of Ni-Ti and Co-Ti binary alloys. According to the work of Wagih et al. [46], it was revealed that the segregation energies are negative in these two binary alloys. Besides, another important factor affecting the GB segregation could be the difference in atomic radii between the host metal and solute element [49, 50]. In the case of the newly designed CoNiCrFeAlTi-based HEA, Ti element (1.47 Å) exhibits a larger atomic volume in contrast to other alloying elements, i.e., Co (1.25 Å), Ni (1.24 Å), Cr (1.27 Å), Fe (1.26), and Al (1.43 Å) [51,52]. These above critical factors lead to the enrichment of Ti element at the GBs. In addition, we also calculated the composition evolution (i.e., Co, Ni, Cr, Fe, Al, and Ti) from the Scheil model as a function of solid fraction, as shown in Supplementary Fig. S_{7}. It can be seen that the relative concentrations of Ti significantly increase as the solidification progresses, which contributes to the occurrence of Ti segregation at the inter-dendritic regions.

However, as for the HT-HEA, we found a distinctly different cellular microstructure that contains a dual-phase-structured (FCC + L1₂) wall and a single-phase-structured (FCC) interior (Fig. 4). It has been revealed that Ti element is the L1₂ phase former and can also greatly promote the precipitation of the L1₂ phase [28,53]. Moreover, the addition of Ti element can also increase the solvus temperature of L1₂ phase [54–56]. For instance, Makineni et al. [54] reported that the solvus temperature of L1₂ phase in the Co-30Ni-10Al-5Mo HEA is increased to 1066 ^\circC by the addition of 2 at% Ti. Bocchini et al. [55] revealed that the solvus temperature of L1₂ phase increases from 982 ^\circC to 1137 ^\circC as the bulk Ti content increases from 0 to 8 at% in the Co-10Ni-9Al-9 W alloy. According to the obtained chemical composition (Table 1), we calculated the phase constituents and their volume fractions of the interior and wall by using the Thermo-Calc software. As Fig. 10 demonstrates, the solvus temperature of L1₂ phase in the interior is determined to be ~797 ^\circC while that in the wall is ~948 ^\circC. This suggests that thermal treatment at 800 ^\circC will produce a single-phase (FCC) structure in the interior and a dual-phase (L1₂ + FCC) structure in the wall, in good agreement with our experiments (Fig. 4).

The above TEM observations (Figs. 8 and 9) have shown that mechanical twinning is formed in the AP-HEA while deformation-induced SFs are the dominant mechanism in the HT-HEA. Generally, the single-phase FCC alloys with low SF energy (about 10–40 mJ/m $ ^{2} $) show a high propensity of mechanical twinning [57,58]. Based on the composition information (Table 1) and the work of Xie et al. [59], the SF energy of the FCC matrix in the AP-HEA is evaluated as  $ \sim $11 mJ/m $ ^{2} $, suggesting a tendency to twin during deformation. Moreover, Rahman et al. [60] pointed out that the twin nucleation stress increased with decreasing grain size. Gutierrez-Urrutia et al. [61] further evaluated the grain size dependence on the twin stress using a Hall-Petch relationship. The critical resolved shear stress  $ \tau_{tw} $ can be given as,

 $$ \tau_{\mathrm{tw}}=\gamma_{\mathrm{SFE}}\Big/b+K_{\mathrm{tw}}^{\mathrm{H-P}}\Big/\sqrt{D} $$ 

where  $ \gamma_{SFE} $ is the SF energy, b is the Burgers vector of the Shockley partial dislocations ( $ \sim $0.148 nm) [62], D is the grain size, and  $ K_{tw}^{H-P} $ is the Hall-Petch constant for twinning. Zhang et al. [63] pointed out that the slope  $ K_{tw}^{H-P} = (3-5)K_{slip}^{H-P} $ for twinning is much higher than the slope  $ K_{slip}^{H-P} $ for slip. To simplify, an intermediate coefficient ( $ K_{tw}^{H-P} = 4K_{slip}^{H-P} = \sim652\ MPa/\mu m^{1/2} $) is used here for analysis [63,64]. The critical stress for twinning  $ \sigma_{tw} $ during the uniaxial tension can be derived as,

 $$ \sigma_{\mathrm{t w}}=M\tau_{\mathrm{t w}} $$ 

where M = 3.06 is the Taylor factor. For the AP-HEA, the grain size is estimated as  $ 93 \pm 36 \mu m $ (from EBSD data in Supplementary Fig. S_{2}). Using the above equations, the critical stress for twinning  $ \sigma_{tw} $ in the AP-HEA is calculated as 403–492 MPa, below the YS ( $ \sim $548 MPa) (Fig. 6). The theoretical calculation suggests that the mechanical twinning is readily generated in the AP-HEA, consistent with the experimental result (Fig. 9). However, given the FCC-structured interior with a diameter of  $ \sim 5 \mu m $ in the HT-HEA, the critical stress for twinning  $ \sigma_{tw} $ is determined as  $ \sim 1120 \text{ MPa} $ that is higher than the measured YS of  $ \sim 748 \text{ MPa} $ (Fig. 6), making twin nucleation more difficult and thus leading to the SF formation. Additionally, the SF-dominated deformation mode is also presented in the dual-phase wall (Fig. 8). Similar results have been frequently reported in L12-strengthened HEAs [65,66]. It has been recognized that high-density L12 precipitates play an important role in controlling the preferred deformation mode. More specifically, the high-density L12 precipitates can narrow the width of FCC matrix and significantly increase the critical twinning stress required. The mechanical twinning is hard to activated in the dual-phase wall, thus contributing to the SF formation.

Firstly, the strength increment is primarily ascribed to the high-density nanoscale L12 precipitates in the wall (Fig. 4). As previously reported [28], introducing numerous L12 nanoparticles in NiCoFeAlTi-based HEA can achieve an ultrahigh strength together with a large ductility. Those ordered L12 precipitates generally act as strong obstacles that can effectively impede the dislocation motion, thus enhancing its strength [67]. Secondly, it has been demonstrated that the heterogeneous structures with different mechanical domains can give rise to hetero-deformation-induced (HDI) strain hardening [35,68,69]. As expected, pronounced strain partitioning induced by the unique cellular structure plays a vital role in achieving the strength-ductility synergy. Our EBSD data (Fig. 7) have shown that high KAM value occurs at the interior/wall interfaces of the HT-HEA under the plastic deformation, which suggests that sufficient pile-ups of geometrically necessary dislocations (GNDs) are produced to accommodate the strain gradients between these two regions. Such unusual GND accumulations can cause high back stress near the interior/wall interfaces and contribute to achieving significant HDI strain hardening, which thereby renders an excellent ductility in the HT-HEA combined with an enhanced strength. Thirdly, dynamic formation of deformation-induced SFs and their interactions is another important factor for sustaining the strain hardening of the cellular-structured HEA. In particular, the manual interactions of the two leading partial dislocations can lead to the formation of sessile dislocations, so-called immobile Lomer-Cottrell (L-C) locks, which not only impedes the motion of those SFs, but also act as barriers to prohibit the dislocation motion, leading to a steady work hardening [70,71].

To better understand the role of the unique cellular structure, a quantitative evaluation is given here. In our present case, the hetero-deformation behaviors of the soft interior and hard wall correspond to that of the two-component composites. Accordingly, the overall strength contribution can be determined by a linear rule of mixture [35]. For the dual-phase (FCC + L12) wall, high-density L12 precipitates play a dominated role in the strength increment. Previous studies [65,66,72] have demonstrated that the strength increment is primarily attributed to the ordering strengthening, which can be expressed as [73],

 $$ \Delta\sigma_{\mathrm{o r d e r}}=0.81M(\gamma_{\mathrm{A P B}}/2b)(3\pi f/8)^{1/2} $$ 

where b is the magnitude of the Burgers vector of the matrix ( $ \sim $0.253 nm). f is the volume fraction of the L1₂ precipitates ( $ \sim $0.208, Supplementary Fig. S_{8}). \gamma_{APB} is the APB energy of L1₂ precipitates. As reported by the references [74,75], the APB energy can be evaluated by an additivity of the contributions from individual alloying elements: \gamma_{APB} = \gamma_{APB}^{0} + \sum_{i}^{n}(k_{i}x_{i}), in which \gamma_{APB}^{0} is the APB energy of Ni₃Al ( $ \sim $110 mJ/m²) [76], kₗ is the correlation coefficient, xₗ is the concentration of the alloying element i in the L1₂ precipitate, n is the number of alloying elements. According to the measured composition (Table 1), \gamma_{APB} is evaluated as  $ \sim $332.5 mJ/m². Based on the above expressions, the contribution of ordering strengthening can be calculated as  $ \sim $806.2 MPa. Aside from the precipitation hardening, the solid-solution hardening is another key factor in the strength increment. Because the solvent and solute are not detectable for HEA systems, it is challenging for estimating the solid-solution hardening [77]. Based on previous studies of Zhao et al. [65], the contribution of the solid-solution hardening in both the interior and wall of the HT-HEA matrix is roughly extrapolated as  $ \sim $334 MPa. Assuming the additivity of these two parts, the YS in the wall is calculated as  $ \sim $1140.2 MPa. Moreover, we further estimated the volume fractions of the interior and wall regions by the projected areal fraction via the ImageJ software. The volume fractions of the interior and wall were separately evaluated as  $ \sim $0.53 and  $ \sim $0.47. Thus, the overall YS is estimated as  $ \sim $712.9 MPa, which is close to the experimental value from the tensile tests ( $ \sim $748.0 MPa).

For clarification, Fig. 11 summarizes the formation and the associated deformation mechanisms of the unique cellular structures in the additively manufactured HEAs. As demonstrated, the high strength is attributed to the high-density  $ L1_{2} $ precipitation in the wall. Furthermore, such a unique cellular structure produces the HDI strain strengthening and SF-dominated deformation mechanisms in both interior and wall, which substantially improves the plastic deformation capacity, achieving the excellent ductility in high-strength heterogeneous precipitation-hardened HEAs. These findings are believed to offer an efficient pathway for the design of strong-yet-ductile HS-HEAs.

## 5. Conclusions

In summary, we successfully printed a cellular-structured CoNiCrFeAlTi-based HEA via the DED approach. Subsequent thermal treatment (800 ^\circC/4 h) leads to an ultrahigh UTS of ~1148 MPa with an excellent ductility of ~28 %. The superior synergy of strength and ductility is primarily attributed to the high-density L1₂ precipitation and improved plastic deformation capacity via HDI strain hardening and SF-dominated deformation mechanisms in both interior and wall regions. More importantly, thermal treatment-produced high-density L1₂ precipitation not only acts as strong obstacles to effectively impede the dislocation motion, but also plays a vital role in achieving HDI strain hardening and SF-dominated deformation mechanism. Our current work opens up new avenues for the design of high-performance HS multiple-principal-element alloys.
