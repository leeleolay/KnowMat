#### GENERAL

2-1/4Cr-1Mo steel is a low-carbon, low-alloy ferritic steel that has excellent creep-resistance properties at temperatures up to 1100 F. The alloy is used extensively in the utility industry for the fabrication of parts for steam-generating boilers and pressure vessels where temperatures typically range between 900 and 1100 F. The material also is used in the petrochemical industry, where thermal reforming and hydrocracking processes are carried out at high pressures and temperatures.

2-1/4Cr-1Mo steel is used primarily in the annealed and normalized-and-tempered heat-treated conditions. In these conditions the material has very good long-term creep-rupture properties between 1000 and 1100 F. For service conditions requiring lower temperatures and higher short-term tensile strengths, the alloy is given a quench-and-tempered heat treatment.

- It has been called the strongest low-alloy steel for high-temperature service, and is second only to austenitic stainless steels in terms of allowable stresses at temperatures exceeding 1050 F.

- The alloy is significantly less expensive than stainless steels.

- It has good to moderate corrosion, hydrogen attack, and oxidation resistance.

## 2-1/4Cr-1Mo steel has a number of attractive properties:

• It has high microstructural stability.

- The alloy has good formability and weldability.

- It has high bainitic hardenability, resulting in fully bainitic microstructure over a broad range of cooling rates.

- It can be heat treated to provide a range of tensile strengths.

A disadvantage of 2-1/4Cr-1Mo steel is that it is prone to temper embrittlement when exposed in the temperature range 600 to 900 F, or when subjected to a slow cool from 1100 to 600 F. A traditional guarantee of notch toughness was quarterline Charpy V-notch toughness of 40 ft-lb at 50 F, but this is being replaced by 40 ft-lb at 20 F (1). An associated effect of temper embrittlement is that the resistance to hydrogen embrittlement is reduced if the material is temper embrittled (2). Because of these implications, attempts are made to control the J*-factor [equal to (%Mn + %Si) × (%P + %Sn) × 10⁴] to less than 200.



#### CORROSION

2-1/4Cr-1Mo steel has good resistance to water corrosion and low stress corrosion susceptibility to wetting steam conditions, provided that water chemistry is properly controlled (3). The material tends to form a fairly adherent oxide scale under high-temperature steam or water exposure, with an oxidation rate that is parabolic with time. 2-1/4Cr-1Mo steel is highly resistant to stress corrosion cracking (SCC) in a chloride environment (4), and is almost immune to SCC in a water environment contaminated with 5 percent NaOH (5). Liquid sodium tends to decarburize the surface of 2-1/4Cr-1Mo steels, leading to the possibility of strain ratcheting of the soft surface layer under cyclic thermal strains (5). At ultimate strength levels less than 100 ksi, 2-1/4Cr-1Mo steel is highly resistant to hydrogen-induced loss of fracture toughness (6). At elevated temperatures the material has good resistance to hydrogen attack, and has therefore been routinely used in hydroprocessing units for a number of years. Recently there have been concerns in the petrochemical industry regarding the rise in 50 percent shear-fracture transition temperature (FATT) arising from absorption of hydrogen after extended use in hydroprocessing environments (2). More attention is now being focused on the synergistic effects of temper embrittlement and hydrogen embrittlement, since the threshold stress intensity for crack growth in a hydrogen environment is found to decrease with increase in FATT (2).

The material is used in the annealed, normalized-and-tempered, and quenched-and-tempered conditions. Typical heat treatments are:

Annealed: Austenitize in the range 1600 to 1700 F (typically 1700 F) followed by furnace cooling. A 2-hour hold at 1300 F is sometimes used (isothermal annealing).

Normalized-and-tempered: Austenitize at 1675 to 1725 F, cool in air, temper at 1075 to 1420 F. Instead of air cooling, accelerated liquid spray cooling is sometimes used, particularly for sections in excess of 4-inch thickness.

Quenched-and-tempered: Austenitize at 1725 to 1800 F, quench in oil, temper at 1050 to 1250 F.

Typical holding periods at the desired temperatures are 1 hour per inch of section thickness.

The microstructure of the annealed steel is predominantly ferrite with dispersed carbides and pearlite and possibly bainite. Molybdenum carbides, mainly of the type M2C, are responsible for imparting good creep-rupture properties to all of the heat-treated conditions. Normalized-and-tempered 2-1/4Cr-1Mo steel contains a mixture of tempered bainite and proeutectoid ferrite. Quenching has the potential to produce martensite, although in thick sections slower cooling rates lead to bainite microstructures. With regard to high-temperature properties, tempered bainite results in higher creep strength than tempered martensite or ferrite-pearlite aggregates for temperatures up to 1050 F and rupture times to 100,000 hours. For higher temperatures and times the ferrite-pearlite structure is the strongest (7).
