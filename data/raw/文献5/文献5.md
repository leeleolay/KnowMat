## Abstract

Sc/Zr modification significantly enhances the printability of high-strength Al-Cu-Li alloys, but poor ductility continues to limit the application of additive manufactured aluminum alloys as advanced component. Here, we proposed a point-by-point deposition strategy based on pulsed-laser powder bed fusion (PLPBF) to precisely control grain structures in Sc/Zr modified Al-Cu-Li alloys. This method successfully breaks the characteristic of melt pool under traditional continuous laser mode, making each ‘point’ melt pool has an independent solidification process. It facilitates the remelting of columnar grains within the melt pools while preserving the equiaxed grains at the edges. Leveraging the spatial distribution features, columnar grains are completely eliminated, resulting in a fully equiaxed grain structure in the printed sample. Meanwhile, the average grain size is reduced by 40%, accompanied by a slight increase in the volume fraction of  $ Al_{3}(Li,Sc,Zr) $ precipitates. The samples fabricated via pulsed laser exhibit a remarkable 211% improvement in elongation compared to those fabricated by continuous laser, with only a minor 7.6% decrease in tensile strength due to the shift from heterogeneous deformation induced (HDI) strengthening to grain refinement strengthening. This strategy enables tunable microstructural design in various alloy systems, offering enhanced flexibility to balance strength and ductility for advanced engineering applications.

## ARTICLE HISTORY

Aluminum alloys are favoured in the aerospace industry for the production of aircraft bodies and components due to their low density, high specific strength, and superior corrosion resistance [1–3]. The advancement of LPBF technology enables the production of complex aluminum alloy structures, fulfilling the demand for lightweight, high-performance aerospace designs [4–8]. However, significant cracking issues associated with high-strength aluminum alloys, such as Al-Cu and Al-Zn, fabricated by LPBF have historically hindered their development. Recent extensive studies indicate that heterogeneous nucleation methods, especially those involving Sc/Zr element modifications, effectively address the cracking problems while simultaneously enhancing the mechanical properties of the alloys [9–12]. ## 1. Introduction

exhibit distinct heterogeneous microstructural characteristics, which are directly related to the precipitation behaviour of primary  $ \mathrm{Al}_{3}(\mathrm{Sc},\mathrm{Zr}) $ phases under the specific solidification conditions [13]. This results in an alternating microstructure characterised by fine equiaxed grain (FG) bands at the boundaries of the melt pool and coarse columnar grain (CG) regions at its centre. The heterogeneous structure is significantly influenced by the content of Sc/Zr elements and the solidification conditions [14,15].

While the heterogeneous microstructure induced by Sc/Zr modification significantly enhances the strength of the alloy, the inherent brittleness of  $ Al_{3}(Sc,Zr) $ precipitates may constrain its plasticity, which is a common challenge in Sc/Zr modified high strength aluminum alloys. Additionally, the non-uniform distribution of the precipitates can lead to localised embrittlement, further reducing the overall plasticity. Numerous

studies have demonstrated that the fully equiaxed grain structure can effectively enhance the ductility of the alloy while simultaneously improving its strength based on the Hall-Petch effect [16].

In fact, the control of the ‘fully equiaxed grain structure’ encompasses two main aspects: first, the further refinement of grain size, and second, the continued enhancement of the volume fraction of equiaxed grains. Currently, techniques such as adjusting processing parameters and introducing nucleating sites have proven effective in controlling the grain size in FG region at the bottom of the melt pool, reducing it to sub-micron levels [17]. However, the control over the CG region in the upper melt pool remains less effective [9,18,19]. To refine the grain size in the CG region and increase the overall volume fraction of FG, current research primarily focuses on modifying the alloy composition, particularly by increasing the content of the introduced Sc/Zr elements. However, a higher solute capture rate in the upper melt pool necessitates a higher threshold for Sc/Zr content. It results in the precipitation of  $ Al_{3}(Sc,Zr) $ phases in the already enriched FG region, manifesting as large, hard, and brittle particles that significantly degrade the ductility of the alloys [20,21]. Therefore, there is an upper limit to the amount of Sc/Zr that can be added, indicating that solely relying on elemental modification presents clear limitations in regulating the comprehensive mechanical properties of the alloy.

In addition to compositional modification, solidification conditions and processing methods are currently the two primary approaches for controlling grain microstructure [22–24]. Regarding solidification conditions, adjusting process parameters, particularly reducing the solidification rate and preheating the substrate, can enhance the proportion of equiaxed grains to some extent. However, decreasing the scanning speed inevitably leads to coarser grains and precipitates, negatively impacting the mechanical properties of the alloy [25]. While substrate preheating helps mitigate grain epitaxial growth by lowering temperature gradients, its effectiveness is limited [26].

In terms of processing methods, optimising scanning strategies, adjusting overlap rates, and modifying powder layer thickness can positively influence grain refinement, primarily through ‘remelting’ [27]. The primary goal is to increase the proportion of the remelting zone, thereby enhancing the volume fraction of FG region. Yang et al. [28] achieved significantly refined columnar grains separated by sub-micron equiaxed grains in LPBF-fabricated Al-Mg-Sc-Zr alloys by adding 1.08 wt.% Sc, linking the ultrafine grain formation to  $ Al_{3}Sc $ particles in the remelting zone. By employing substrate preheating and increasing the energy density, they reduced the temperature gradient and further increased the volume fraction of the remelting zones, ultimately obtaining a nearly fully equiaxed grain microstructure. This underscores the importance of remelting methods in increasing the proportion of the FG region. However, remelting at high energy densities can also lead to overheating and microstructure coarsening, negatively impacting the mechanical properties.

LPBF is a three-dimensional (3D) manufacturing process, and the heterogeneous microstructure of Sc/Zr modified aluminum alloys also exhibits spatial distribution characteristics. Due to the challenges of modifying individual melt pool characteristics, this study explores a strategy that leverages the overlapping remelting effects of multiple melt pools in 3D space. Lasers primarily operate in two modes: continuous and pulsed. As illustrated in Figure 1(a), continuous laser forms FG at the bottom and CG at the top within a deposition track. In contrast, Figure 1(b) shows that pulsed laser breaks a large melt pool into smaller, independent pools, forming FG at the edges and CG internally. By optimising the overlap ratio of the melt pools, FG can be preserved while CG is completely remelted, resulting in a fully equiaxed grain microstructure within a single track. Furthermore, considering the spatial distribution of melt pools in three dimensions, adjusting the overlap ratio in all directions may effectively remelt the columnar zones across layers. This strategy could significantly increase the fraction of equiaxed grains and potentially result in a fully equiaxed microstructure (Figure 1(c)). The corresponding microstructure evolution from continuous to pulsed laser in bulk samples is illustrated in Figure 1(d–f). In summary, pulsed laser provides point-level control, enhancing process flexibility and benefiting from higher cooling rates, which helps prevent overheating and reduce the risk of grain coarsening, allowing regulation of microstructure size and volume fraction.

This study focuses on Al-Cu-Li-Sc-Zr alloy, characterised by its high specific strength and stiffness. Experiments were designed to fabricate samples by utilising the overlapping remelting effects of melt pools produced by pulsed lasers. The microstructure and mechanical properties were compared with those processed under continuous laser conditions. The objective is to develop a method for controlling grain size and volume fraction to improve the comprehensive mechanical properties of Al-Cu-Li alloys. Extending to other alloy systems, this method offers precise control over the heterogeneous microstructure to meet diverse performance requirements.

## 2. Materials and methods

The raw materials for the experiments were Sc/Zr modified Al-Cu-Li alloy powders produced via gas atomisation. The powder composition was Al-2.88Cu-1.79Li-1.06Sc-0.56Zr, as determined by inductively coupled plasma-optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES). The particle diameters ranged from 15 to 53  $ \mu $m, displaying good sphericity and the absence of significant impurities. Continuous laser experiments (CL) were conducted using a BLT S_{200} laser system, with a focused laser diameter of 100  $ \mu $m. Conversely, pulsed laser experiments (PL) were carried out with a Renishaw AM400 commercial laser equipped with a 400 W quasi-continuous mode laser, with a focused spot diameter of approximately 70  $ \mu $m. Figure 2 illustrates the fundamental differences between the CL and PL in this study. The key distinction lies in the continuity of energy input. In the CL mode (Figure 2(a,b)), the laser operates with a continuous and steady power output, delivering uninterrupted energy to the powder bed throughout the scanning process. In contrast, the PL mode (Figure 2(c,d)) features a time-modulated energy input, where the laser is periodically turned on and off. This pulsed delivery of energy introduces intervals of cooling between successive pulses, effectively reducing thermal accumulation and promoting a more rapid solidification process. The process parameters, presented in Table 1, were selected based on an equivalent scanning speed of 1000 mm/s, with a focus on optimising both density and microhardness. The empirical formula for the equivalent scanning speed of pulsed laser is provided below:

 $$ v_{\mathrm{e}}=\frac{D}{t+t_{0}} $$ 

where D is the point spacing, t is the exposure time, and  $ t_{0} $ is the delay time (Figure 2c). The deposition layer thickness for both techniques was established at 30  $ \mu $m, utilising a scanning strategy employed an inter-layer rotation of 67^\circ.

The substrate used for the experiments was an AlSi10Mg plate, which underwent surface polishing and was cleaned with acetone to eliminate surface oils. To prevent cracking caused by large temperature gradients, the substrate was preheated to 150^\circC prior to experimentation. All build processes were conducted in an argon atmosphere, ensuring that the oxygen concentration remained below 300 ppm during the experiments. Optimised process parameters were used to prepare metallographic samples with a size of  $ 8 \times 8 \times 8 \, mm^3 $ for microstructure observation. Industrial computerised tomography (CT, Zeiss Xradia 520 Versa) was used to detect defects and porosity in the samples, with a resolution of 3.5  $ \mu $m. To ensure consistency and improve the representativeness of the porosity analysis, both CT-scanned specimens were extracted from the same location at the mid-height of the as-built samples. ## 4. Discussion

## 4.1. Strength analysis

During the continuous-LPBF process of Sc/Zr modified Al-Cu-Li alloys, the unique precipitation and growth behaviour of the  $ Al_{3}(Li,Sc,Zr) $ phase typically results in a heterogeneous microstructure, featuring alternating equiaxed and columnar grains, which significantly contribute to the enhanced strength of the alloy [13,28]. However, in this study, a fully equiaxed grain microstructure was achieved by modulation of the laser mode and processing parameters. According to the Hall-Petch theory, FG regions are expected to exhibit significantly higher strength than CG regions [16,35]. Therefore, it was anticipated that a fully equiaxed grain microstructure would exhibit superior strength compared to a

First of all, the strengthening effect of grain refinement, induced by the nucleation of primary Al₃(Li,Sc, Zr), plays a crucial role in enhancing the strength of Sc/Zr modified Al-Cu-Li alloys. As the volume fraction of grain boundaries increases and subgrain boundaries become finer, the resistance to dislocation movement becomes more pronounced, raising the stress required for dislocation pile-up and activation, thus significantly increasing strength [39]. Based on the statistical results of grain size in Section 3.1, the computed difference in \Delta\sigma_GBS between the CL and PL samples is approximately 24.8 MPa, with values of 135.1 MPa for CL sample and 159.9 MPa for PL.

When the radius of Al₃(Li,Sc,Zr) precipitates is below the critical radius (~2 nm) for the transition from the shear to bypass mechanisms, the alloy is strengthened through dislocation shearing of the precipitates, which is known as modulus strengthening (\sigma_modulus). Coherency strengthening (\sigma_coherency) is caused by the interaction of the dislocations with strain field of Al₃(Li,Sc, Zr) precipitates, and \sigma_order is due to the APB formed when dislocations shear the precipitates. In precipitation strengthening, the \sigma_modulus and \sigma_coherency mechanisms operate prior to the dislocations shearing the precipitates, whereas the \sigma_order becomes rate-limiting during the shearing process [40]. Therefore, the strength increment due to Al₃(Li,Sc,Zr) precipitates is determined by the larger of the calculated values between \sigma_modulus + \sigma_coherency and \sigma_order. The specific calculation results based on formula (3)–(5) are shown in Table 3. It can be indicated that the \sigma_modulus and \sigma_coherency predominantly govern the strengthening contribution of shearable Al₃(Li,Sc,Zr) precipitates (278.8 MPa for CL and 354.9 MPa for PL), with a resulting difference in precipitation strengthening of 76.1 MPa.

As well known, alloy systems doped with atoms that enhance fracture energy exhibit higher theoretical stress [41]. Previous studies have shown that the fracture energies of Al–Sc and Al–Zr are greater than those of the aluminum matrix, and Sc/Zr atoms readily doped into the matrix improve its strength [24]. However, the difference in solid solution strengthening between the CL and PL samples is minimal, only 1.6 MPa (57.4 MPa for CL and 59.0 MPa for PL). Given that both samples have identical powder compositions and were processed with the same scanning speed, the solid solution strengthening effect is largely consistent across the two processes.

Calculations of the contributions from the three strengthening mechanisms suggest that the theoretical YS of the PL sample should be approximately 102.5 MPa higher than that of the CL sample. Contrarily, experimental data reveal that the YS of the CL sample exceeds that of the PL sample by 57.3 MPa. This discrepancy of 159.8 MPa is primarily attributed to a unique strengthening mechanism in the heterogeneous microstructure of the CL sample, referred to as HDI strengthening.

HDI strengthening stems from the strength disparity between the soft and hard regions, as depicted in Figure 9(a–c). The mechanical incompatibility of the non-homogeneous microstructure during deformation leas to strain redistribution, causing the softer region to yield before the harder region, thereby inducing plastic deformation [42–45]. Plastic strain at the interfaces between soft and hard regions remains negligible, peaking at dislocation sources. As strain increases, the

plastic yielding transitions from the soft to the hard region, revealing inhomogeneous deformation and creating stress and plastic strain gradients at the boundaries. To accommodate these gradients, a considerable number of geometrically necessary dislocations (GNDs) are generated [46,47]. GNDs accumulate at the boundaries of the soft zones to maintain strain continuity, generating positive stress in the hard regions and corresponding back stress in the soft zones. The HDI stress counteracts the applied shear stress, allowing the soft zones to withstand higher loads and significantly increasing the YS. In contrast, for the PL samples with a fully equiaxed microstructure, the  $ \Delta\sigma_{GBS} $, as predicted by the Hall-Petch theory, along with the increased volume fraction of fine grains and localised precipitates, contributes to the enhanced YS. The specific contributions to strength have been quantitatively analysed in the preceding sections.

To summarise, despite the theoretical expectation from Hall-Petch theory that FG regions should possess greater strength than CG regions, the heterogeneous structure – characterised by alternating fine and coarse grains – demonstrates a notable strength enhancement compared to a fully equiaxed microstructure. It can be attributed to the interplay of the vanishing HDI strengthening effect following equiaxialization, the enhanced Hall-Petch strengthening, and the increased volume fraction of FG, which leads to a higher number of localised precipitates. This underscores the pivotal role of HDI strengthening in the alternating fine and coarse grain microstructure of high strength aluminum alloys fabricated by continuous-LPBF. Interestingly, although the CL and PL samples show a certain difference in YS, their UTS remain relatively similar. This may indicate that fine grain strengthening mechanisms compensate for the diminished HDI strengthening effect, thus preserving overall tensile strength.

## 4.2. Ductility analysis

Strength and ductility are often considered as a trade-off [48,49]. While the addition of Sc/Zr elements significantly enhances the mechanical strength of the \alpha-Al matrix, the resulting Al₃(Li,Sc,Zr) precipitates, which are hard and brittle, inevitably reduce ductility. Additionally, as a typical precipitation-strengthened aluminum alloy, Al-Cu-Li alloys tend to experience a substantial sacrifice in ductility alongside strength gains after heat treatment. Therefore, improving ductility in the as-deposited state becomes particularly crucial.

Strain hardening is crucial for maintaining the ductility of metals, particularly for high strength variants [50]. In the CL sample with the unique heterogeneous structure, HDI strengthening enhances the YS, while strain hardening improved plasticity. This interplay exemplifies the intrinsic strength-ductility synergy inherent in heterogeneous structures [47]. As illustrated in Figure 9(a), GNDs accumulate at the boundaries between soft and hard regions to accommodate strain gradients, generating forward stress in the hard region which drives dislocation slip and facilitates deformation [51]. The forward and back stresses at the boundaries are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction, effectively cancelling each other out. Beyond the boundary, the HDI strain hardening effect becomes more pronounced, as shown in Figure 9(b). As the external strain increases, the accumulation of GNDs at the boundary raises the forward stress, thereby enhancing the work hardening effect and improving plasticity [47]. However, the room temperature elongation of 4.82% in the CL sample indicates that while the synergy between strength and ductility in the heterogeneous structure of the Sc/Zr modified Al-Cu-Li alloy is effective, it is insufficient to offset the reduction in plasticity caused by the substantial precipitation of hard and brittle Al₃(Li,Sc,Zr) precipitates, revealing a limitation in enhancing ductility.

Numerous studies have demonstrated that equiaxed grain microstructures generally enhance the ductility of alloys [52,53]. In this work, a remelting strategy was designed using pulsed laser to achieve fully equiaxed grain microstructures, ultimately doubling the elongation (4.82% for CL and 10.18% for PL) at the expense of a slight reduction in YS. Indeed, grain refinement proves to be an efficient method for achieving a strength-ductility synergy, offering advantages not found in other strengthening techniques. The small grains and numerous grain boundaries facilitate more uniform deformation across multiple grains, reducing dislocation accumulation and lowering the risk of crack initiation due to stress concentration. Additionally, the complex grain boundaries hinder crack propagation, enabling the material to withstand greater deformation before fracture, thereby exhibiting superior plasticity, as illustrated in Figure 9(d). In contrast, heterogeneous microstructures are prone to stress concentration and uneven deformation at CG boundaries, which can facilitate crack initiation and propagation along these boundaries and the interfaces of coarse columnar grains, ultimately leading to failure (Figure 9(e)).

Specifically, for Al-Cu-Li alloys, the coherently precipitated  $ \delta' $ ( $ Al_{3}Li $) phase can induce coplanar deformation and localised slip, increasing the likelihood of premature failure at the grain boundaries [54,55]. As localised strain intensifies, certain areas within the alloy may deform prior to macroscopic deformation. The interplay between highly localised deformation, stress

Reducing grain size is one of the simplest and most effective strategies to mitigate localised strain in the matrix. This approach improves plasticity by reducing dislocation slip lengths and local stress concentrations. It not only enhances the elongation of the sample but also alters its fracture behaviour, shifting from low-energy brittle intergranular fracture to high-energy, complex transgranular fracture.

On the other hand, from the perspective of grain orientation, the deformation of polycrystals is influenced by the obstruction of grain boundaries and the differing orientations of adjacent grains. The plastic deformation of any individual grain is constrained by its neighbours, necessitating cooperative deformation to maintain material continuity. During slip, grains with a favourable orientation are activated first. As dislocations accumulate and become impeded at the grain boundaries, other grains begin to slide. In the case of equiaxed structures, the nearly symmetric cubic configuration imparts isotropic characteristics, meaning that the physical properties of the metal are uniform in all directions. This uniformity allows for even stress distribution under load, minimising stress concentration and thereby reducing the risk of crack initiation and propagation. Moreover, the interlocking structure of equiaxed grains contributes to the toughness of the sample, enabling it to absorb energy through plastic deformation when subjected to external forces, thereby mitigating the risk of fracture. The enhancement of alloy plasticity in the as-deposited state also provides greater operational flexibility and potential for further strengthening after heat treatment.

## 5. Conclusions

This study successfully fabricated a Sc/Zr modified Al-Cu-Li alloy with a fully equiaxed microstructure based on the point-level control of pulsed laser. A comparative analysis was conducted on the microstructural characteristics and mechanical properties of heterogeneous and equiaxed microstructures, alongside an assessment of the contributing strengthening mechanisms. The key findings are summarised as follows:

(1) Sc/Zr modified Al-Cu-Li alloys with a fully equiaxed microstructure using the point-by-point deposition features of PLPBF were successfully fabricated employing a melt pool overlapping approach.

(2) The average grain size is reduced by 40%, with the refinement effect of the pulsed laser being particularly significant in the CG region. Although the difference in solidification conditions between continuous and pulsed lasers has a limited effect on the precipitation behaviour of  $ \mathrm{Al}_{3}(\mathrm{Li},\mathrm{Sc},\mathrm{Zr}) $ phases, the extensive formation of fine equiaxed grains induced by pulsed laser remelting increases the volume fraction of precipitates.

(3) The samples produced using pulsed laser demonstrate an elongation improvement of approximately

211% compared to those manufactured with continuous laser, accompanied by only a slight decrease of 7.6% in tensile strength.

(4) The strength-ductility synergy provided by HDI strengthening plays a crucial role in enhancing the strength of CL samples, but it cannot fully offset the ductility loss caused by the extensive precipitation of hard and brittle  $ Al_{3}(Li,Sc,Zr) $ phases, ultimately leading to reduced overall elongation.

(5) For PL samples, the fully equiaxed microstructure and the uniform distribution of precipitates significantly enhance the ductility of the alloy. Meanwhile, grain boundary strengthening and the increased volume of precipitates effectively compensate for the absence of HDI strengthening, resulting in a tensile strength comparable to that of CL samples.
