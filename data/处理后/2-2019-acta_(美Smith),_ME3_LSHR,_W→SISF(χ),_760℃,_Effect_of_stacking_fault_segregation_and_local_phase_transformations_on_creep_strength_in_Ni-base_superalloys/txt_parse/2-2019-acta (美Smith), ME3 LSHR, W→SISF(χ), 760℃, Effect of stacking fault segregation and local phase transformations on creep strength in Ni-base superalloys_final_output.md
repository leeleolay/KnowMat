## Abstract

In this study, two similar, commercial polycrystalline Ni-based disk superalloys (LSHR and ME3) were creep tested at 760 ^\circC and 552 MPa to approximately 0.3% plastic strain. LSHR consistently displayed superior creep properties at this stress/temperature regime even though the microstructural characteristics between the two alloys were comparable. High resolution structural and chemical analysis, however, revealed significant differences between the two alloys among active  $ \gamma' $ shearing modes involving superlattice intrinsic and extrinsic stacking faults. In ME3, Co and Cr segregation and Ni and Al depletion were observed along the intrinsic faults - revealing a  $ \gamma' $ to  $ \gamma $ phase transformation. Conversely in LSHR, an alloy with a higher W content, Co and W segregation was observed along the intrinsic faults. This observation combined with scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) simulations confirm a  $ \gamma' $-to- $ D0_{19} $  $ \chi $ phase transformation along the intrinsic faults in LSHR. Using experimental observations and density functional theory calculations, a novel local phase transformation strengthening mechanism is proposed that could be further utilized to improve the high temperature creep capabilities of Ni-base disk alloys.

## 1. Introduction

To reduce  $ CO_{2} $ emissions in power generation and propulsion systems, new high temperature capable materials are needed. Currently, Ni-base superalloys are a critical class of materials for high temperature applications such as in the hot section of jet turbine engines [1]. These metals primarily acquire their superior high temperature mechanical properties from coherent face centered cubic (FCC)  $ Ni_{3}Al $  $ \gamma' $ precipitates which can make up to 50–70% of their respective volumes [2–4]. As such, decades of research has been spent investigating the varying deformation processes that defeat these precipitates in a wide range of temperatures and stresses [5–7].

To date, the highest operating temperature that jet turbine disks can sustain is around 700 ^\circC [1]. Below this temperature the active creep deformation mechanisms primarily consist of dislocation glide in the softer \gamma channels, and anti-phase boundary (APB) shearing or dislocation looping of the \gamma' precipitates – depending on their size and volume fraction [8]. Above 700 ^\circC, different \gamma' shearing deformation mechanisms become active [9,10]. Two principal faults that form in \gamma' precipitates in association with these shearing mechanisms active between 700 and 800 ^\circC are superlattice intrinsic and extrinsic stacking faults (SISF and SESFs) [11–15]. These faults, along with APB's, can form more complex shearing modes such as microtwins and stacking fault ribbons [12,16–20]. To incorporate Ni-base superalloys into jet turbines that can operate within this higher temperature regime, these mechanisms need to be better understood.

Recent atomic-scale characterization efforts have highlighted the diffusion mechanisms that contribute to the formation of these  $ \gamma' $ fault-shearing modes. One prominent feature is the presence of solute segregation along the faults during creep at temperatures above 700 ^\circC. Initially, segregation along both SISFs and SESFs was hypothesized due to the presence of higher Z-contrast along the faults when imaged in a high angle annular dark field (HAADF) condition using a scanning transmission electron microscope (STEM) [21]. In 2015, the first direct observation of segregation along the SISFs in CMSX-4 and ME3 was confirmed by Viswanathan

et al. [22]. In the years following, numerous observations have been made to confirm the presence of segregation along stacking faults in Co and Ni-based superalloys when crept at temperatures above 700 ^\circC [23–32]. The segregation effects have only been observed in the ordered \gamma′ precipitates, not in the FCC \gamma matrix. Generally, these studies report segregation of \gamma-forming elements such as Co and Cr along superlattice stacking faults where it is believed the reduction/removal of Al–Al nearest neighbor violations helps reduce the overall stacking fault energy and may facilitate the shearing process [10,23,32].

Titus et al. first hypothesized that the observed segregation may also be driven by an atomic phase transformation along stacking faults [32]. Since then this idea has been supported by evidence of \eta phase formation along SESFs in Ni-base superalloys [31] and D0₁₉ \chi phase formation along SISFs in both Co and Ni-based superalloys [30,32,33]. In addition, some studies have attributed the solute segregation along faults to represent a localized \gamma′ to \gamma phase transformation [10,23,29]. After reviewing these segregation studies it may be concluded that slight changes in bulk chemistry between the varying superalloys results in significant differences in segregation along the faults. A study by Smith et al. was the first to explore if this difference in stacking fault segregation resulted in changes to the overall high temperature properties of the superalloys [34]. In their study, it was discovered that the nucleation of \eta phase instead of the \gamma phase along SESFs inhibited the formation of microtwins, thereby producing notably slower creep strain rates.

In a previous effort, an alloy having very similar overall composition and microstructure to ME3 but with altered refectory element levels, LSHR, was found to have a higher creep resistance [35]. However, the deformation mechanisms associated with this high creep resistance were unclear. The objective of this study is to determine if different chemical segregation processes observed along SISFs and SESFs in ME3 and LSHR may influence their overall creep properties. With support from density functional theory (DFT) calculations and HAADF-STEM simulations, it is apparent that forming the  $ Co_{3}W \chi $ phase along SISFs correlates with enhanced creep resistance, suggesting that further  $ \gamma' $ shearing in the form of stacking fault ribbons is inhibited. These observations present a new local phase transformation strengthening mechanism that can be exploited in development of future superalloys.

## 2.2. Microscopy methods

Post-mortem STEM specimens were extracted from the gauges of the tensile creep specimens oriented  $ 45^{\circ} $ away from the tensile axis to investigate deformation along crystallographic planes edge-on. This was done by removing 3 mm diameter disks through electrical discharge machining (EDM). The disks were polished down to a thickness of  $ 100 \mu m $ using SiC polishing pads and then electro-polished using a solution of 90% methanol and 10% perchloric acid at  $ -30^{\circ}C $ and 12 V. Deformation substructure analysis was performed on an FEI Talos at 200 kV utilizing a HAADF detector at high camera lengths (200–300 mm) for enhanced diffraction contrast [37]. Zone axis STEM imaging at this camera length enables large fields of view to be examined and maximizes the probability of multiple dislocation families being viewed in strong contrast, and thus is ideal for obtaining an overview of deformation substructures. Atomic resolution HAADF imaging of stacking faults was completed using a probe corrected Titan3 at 300 kV. The high resolution HAADF imaging provides atomic number (Z) contrast information on atomic columns, where heavier elements present brighter atomic columns [38,39]. High resolution energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS) was performed using Super-X detectors on both a Talos and a Titan $ ^{3} $ G2 microscope using the FEI Velox and Bruker Esprit software packages, respectively. Vertically integrated chemical line scans were quantified through Cliff-Lorimer analysis [40] using  $ K_{\alpha} $ energies for Al, Ni, Co, Cr, Ti, Nb, and Mo, while  $ M_{\alpha} $ energies were used for Ta and W as their  $ L_{\alpha} $ energies were convoluted by the Cu signal from the specimen holder.

The analysis in this section focuses exclusively on the EDS results for the stacking faults in LSHR since the segregation of  $ \gamma $ formers along SESFs [9,34] and SISFs [28,29] in ME3 has been widely reported and is in agreement with the ME3 chemical mapping performed for this study. 4.3. Local phase transformation strengthening effect of  $ \chi $ phase along SISFs

A prominent creep deformation mechanism active in Ni-base superalloys at this temperature and stress is  $ \alpha_{112} $ stacking fault ribbons. These stacking fault ribbons are suspected to be a major contribution to primary creep strain in [001] single crystal superalloys deformed in tension between 700 and 800 ^\circC [20,21], and complement the SESF/microtwinning mechanism that is prominent for [110] single crystals [45]. The structure of an  $ \alpha_{112} $ stacking fault ribbon is presented in the equation below.

 $$ \begin{array}{c}\frac{a}{3}112(SISF)+\quad\frac{a}{6}112(APB)+\quad\frac{a}{6}112(SESF)+\quad\frac{a}{3}112\quad=a112\\ (P1)\quad(P2)\quad(P3)\quad(P4)\end{array} $$ 

A stacking fault ribbon consists of four different Shockley partials (denoted throughout the paper as P_{1} through P_{4}) that shear consecutively on the same  $ \{111\} $ slip plane. Each Shockley partial leaves behind a different fault after shearing through a  $ \gamma' $ precipitate. The leading Super-Shockley partial (P_{1}) formed by the reordering of an APB and complex stacking fault, leaves behind a SISF [21,28,30]. The  $ \{111\} $ plane containing the SISF is then further sheared by an  $ \frac{a}{6} $ 112 Shockley partial (P_{2}) creating an APB. The shear of another Shockley partial (P_{3}) forms a trailing SESF. Finally, the shear of an  $ \frac{a}{3} $ 112 super-Shockley partial (P_{4}) and further reordering completes the movement of the a112 super dislocation through a  $ \gamma' $ precipitate. The movement of the stacking fault ribbon is complex and dependent on numerous diffusional processes. Therefore, the presence of  $ \chi $ phase in LSHR instead of the usual  $ \gamma $-like state along a SISF may affect the shear rate of these stacking fault ribbons by influencing the subsequent formation of the APB that must form in the middle of the ribbon configuration. It is noted that we have not observed APB faults directly in the present experimental observations, although both intrinsic and extrinsic stacking faults are present in grains such as those shown in Fig. 4. The high energy APB is a transient fault that occurs during stacking fault ribbon movement; however, capturing their presence in post-mortem observations may be difficult. In the most definitive work identifying stacking fault ribbons by Rae and co-workers [21], and in single crystal alloys with large secondary  $ \gamma' $ precipitates, it was possible to record the transition between the SISF and APB within the precipitates. This was not possible in the present study, and for the lower  $ \gamma' $ volume fraction alloys ME3 and LSHR. Nevertheless, the scenario we propose here relies on the necessity of APB fault formation during the operation of the stacking fault ribbon mechanism.

To investigate this hypothesis, density functional theory calculations were performed. 144 atom  $ Ni_{3}Al $ cells with an APB through their middle were created using CrystalMaker X. The cells were created with periodic boundary conditions. Each cell included an impurity of Cr, Mo, or W either on the APB plane or several  $ \{111\} $ planes away from the fault plane, as shown in Fig. 12.

By comparing the relaxed energies of the cell in Fig. 12(a), where an impurity is positioned away from the APB, to the cell in Fig. 12(b), with an impurity on the APB, estimates can be made as to how the different SISF segregation species might affect the formation energy of the APB. Both cells were relaxed until convergence using the DFT code VASP [46–48]. Structural relaxations were performed using functionals incorporating the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) [49] and the Projector Augmented Wave (PAW) approach. All calculations were performed using a Gamma-centered  $ 1 \times 1 \times 1 $ Monkhorst-Pack k point grid. The difference in energies between different impurity locations (away and on the APB) are presented for Cr, Mo, and W and shown in Fig. 13.

Therefore, a pre-existing SISF with  $ \chi $ phase presents a poor environment for the subsequent creation of an APB. This calculation is supported by Makineni et al. [27] who found W segregation along SISFs in a CoNi based superalloy, but a depletion of W along APBs. Therefore, either W must completely diffuse away from the fault, or a larger shear stress is required before a Shockley partial could shear the  $ \chi $ phase, creating the APB. The results by Makineni et al. suggest that W diffuses away from the fault, which in Section 4.1 was revealed to be a rate-limiting diffusion process. Conversely, when Cr segregates to the SISF, as would be the case for the  $ \gamma $ phase formation in ME3, the APB energy was found to be markedly reduced when the Cr atoms occupy Ni-sites or contributed no energetic change when on the Al-sites. As a result, the formation of a Co and Cr-rich disordered  $ \gamma $ phase along SISFs would promote the shear of a stacking ribbon. This process is summarized schematically in Fig. 14.

Fig. 14 presents the two different cases of stacking fault ribbon shear in both ME3 and LSHR. In ME3, the leading SISF of a stacking fault ribbon has sheared into a  $ \gamma' $ precipitate and the  $ \gamma $ formers Co and Cr have segregated along the fault as shown in 14(b). As the stacking fault ribbon progresses, an  $ \frac{a}{6} $112 Shockley partial (P_{2}) attempts to shear along the SISF and form an APB (Fig. 14(c)). According to the DFT calculations in Fig. 13, the segregation of Cr along the SISF promotes the formation of an APB allowing the stacking fault ribbon to advance through the precipitate as shown in 14(d). The  $ \gamma $ phase segregation along the APB is further enhanced as was discovered by Barba et al. [23]. Finally, in Fig. 14(e) the Shockley partials (P_{3}), which forms the SESF, and (P_{4}), which completes the  $ \alpha $112 dislocation, can easily shear through the  $ \gamma $-rich APB resulting in a faster macro-scale primary creep strain rate in ME3.

For LSHR, the leading Super-Shockley partial (P_{1}) enters the precipitate; but, instead the  $ Co_{3}W \chi $ phase nucleates along the fault, as shown in Fig. 14(f). Again, a Shockley partial (P_{2}) attempts to shear along the SISF to create an APB (Fig. 14(g)). However, according to the DFT calculations the presence of W on the Al-sites significantly inhibits this shear resulting in a more stable SISF as shown in Fig. 14(h). The more stable SISF results in a much slower shear rate of the  $ \alpha_{112} $ dislocation contributing to a lower creep strain rate as compared to ME3. Indeed, Fig. 14 presents a novel local “phase transformation strengthening” mechanism that can be utilized and optimized to improve the high temperature creep properties of Ni-base disk superalloys. From these results, the authors suggest the promotion of slow diffusers (especially W) to segregate along stacking faults as well as the formation of  $ \eta $ phase along SESFs and  $ \chi $ phase along SISFs for optimal creep properties in future disk superalloys.

## 5. Conclusion

This study identifies distinctly different stacking fault segregation and local phase transformation phenomena active following creep properties of two similar, polycrystalline Ni-base disk superalloys, ME3 and LSHR. From the atomic-scale STEM EDS and HAADF-STEM characterization of active  $ \gamma' $ shearing modes following creep at 760 ^\circC and 552 MPa, several notable conclusions can be drawn.

1. Significant differences between ME3 and LSHR creep strain rates exist even though their microstructures (grain size and precipitate structure), solute strengthening contributions, and  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ misfit values are all very similar.

2. Slight differences in overall chemistry between the two alloys resulted in notable differences in stacking fault segregation. LSHR presented the formation of  $ \eta $ phase and/or W-rich  $ \gamma $ phase along SESFs and the  $ Co_{3}W $  $ \chi $ phase along SISFs. In contrast, ME3 presented the formation of a Co and Cr-rich  $ \gamma $ phase along both stacking fault types.

## 3. The observed diffusion of W along SESFs presents a new rate limiting process during creep, by slowing the formation and extension of twins in LSHR and contributing to its superior creep strength.

4. DFT VASP calculations found W and Mo segregation to  $ \gamma' $ Al-sites significantly raised APB energy while Cr segregation reduced or had little effect on the APB energy depending on which lattice site it segregated to.

5. The formation of  $ \chi $ phase along SISFs presents a novel local phase transformation strengthening mechanism by inhibiting the shear of a112 stacking fault ribbons.

All of these conclusions combine to strongly imply that segregation and/or phase transformations along stacking faults have a considerable effect on creep properties in Ni-base superalloys above 700 ^\circC. Further work is needed to improve the understanding of these new potential creep strengthening mechanisms.
