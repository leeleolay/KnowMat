## Abstract

The tensile properties, mode I fracture toughness ( $ K_{Ic} $), fatigue crack growth behavior, and unnotched fatigue strength of additively manufactured Ti-6Al-4V (Ti_{64}) alloy using selective laser melting (SLM) technique were investigated. Four different combinations of layer thickness (t) - scan rotation between successive layers ( $ \phi $), which resulted in mesostructures that range from through-thickness columnar prior  $ \beta $ grains with square cross-sections, whose side lengths equal to the scan spacing, to near-equiaxed mesostructures in both build and transverse directions, were explored. Possible anisotropy in mechanical properties was investigated by conducting tests on samples whose loading axis is either parallel or perpendicular to the build directions. In all cases, the microstructure consisted of fine  $ \alpha/\alpha' $ lath structure, where  $ \alpha' $ is the metastable martensitic Ti phase that is acicular in shape, within the prior  $ \beta $ grains. Experimental results show that the process parameter combinations of  $ t = 60 \mu m $ and  $ \phi = 67^\circ $ results in an alloy that exhibits high yield strength (>1100 MPa) and ductility (>12%) simultaneously,  $ K_{Ic} $ of 58 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $, and unnotched fatigue strength, which is similar to that of the same alloy but manufactured using conventional techniques. The anisotropy in properties, overall, was found to be not substantial, even in the case where columnar growth of prior  $ \beta $ grains occurs in the build direction. The values of the Paris exponents for steady state fatigue crack growth (FCG) are much lower than those reported for conventionally manufactured Ti_{64}, suggesting higher FCG resistance in SLM Ti_{64}. Analysis of the effective microstructural length scale that controls the near-threshold FCG rate suggests that it is the colony size that dominates this behavior. Overall, the results of this study indicate directions for process parameter optimization that would lead to SLM Ti_{64} that is not only has high strength, but also is damage tolerant.

## 1. Introduction

Additive manufacturing (AM) of metallic components through selective laser melting (SLM) of layered powder beds offers the ability to build near-net shape parts with complex geometries using a minimum number of processing steps. Furthermore, it offers a number of other advantages such as high fly-to-buy ratio, ability to rapidly prototype, and flexibility for on-demand manufacturing. As a result of these, considerable current excitement exists on AM of structural metallic parts with different alloys. However, a comprehensive understanding of the structure-property correlations in them is essential before they are certified fit for many critical applications, especially for parts that experience fluctuating loads in the aerospace structures.

Of the many structural alloys that are being evaluated for their suitability for AM, Ti-6Al-4V (Ti_{64} here afterwards), which is the most widely used Ti alloy in the aerospace industry in particular, is a prominent one. The microstructure of this alloy, when conventionally manufactured, contains both hexagonal close packed (HCP)  $ \alpha $ and body centered cubic (BCC)  $ \beta $ phases, whose arrangement depends on the heat treatment conditions employed. Typically, it contains reasonably coarse  $ \alpha $ lamellae in either colony or basket-weave arrangements, while the prior  $ \beta $ grain boundaries (GBs) are often decorated with primary  $ \alpha $ [1]. Consequently, the overall mechanical response of Ti_{64} can be influenced by several different microstructural length scales such as the grain, colony, and lath sizes, along with the volume fractions of  $ \alpha $ and  $ \beta $. The presence of a meso-structure in parts that are built using SLM, which is a

consequence of the line-by-line, layer-by-layer AM process, would be an added variable in the context of alloy design. While significant anisotropy in properties is a distinct possibility as a result of the mesostructures, it can also have a pronounced influence on the crack growth behavior, both under quasi-static and cyclic loads. The latter is of particular relevance for damage tolerant design of components that are prone to fatigue loading–an aspect that is of paramount importance in the aerospace industry. In some alloys, such as Al-12 wt.% Si, it was already shown that such a meso-structure can be exploited for enhancing the fracture toughness via increased crack tip tortuosity [2]. Consequently, 'what role does the mesostructure play in determining the mechanical behavior of SLM Ti_{64}?' is a question that requires detailed examination.

There are additional microstructural aspects that require close scientific scrutiny. Both the melting of the powder by laser and the subsequent cooling of it occur rapidly, which are primarily due to the high heat gradients inherent to the SLM process [3]. This results in a fine, and often a metastable, microstructure in almost all the SLM alloys. In the context of Ti_{64}, the rapid quenching can lead to the formation martensitic  $ \alpha' $ phase that typically occurs as fine acicular laths. While the presence of  $ \alpha' $ in the microstructure can impart high yield strength ( $ \sigma_{Y} $) to Ti_{64}, it can be detrimental from the overall mechanical performance point of view due to its low ductility [4,5]. The extent to which the presence of  $ \alpha' $ in SLM Ti_{64} affects its crack resistance is another aspect that requires close examination.

Due to the fact that metal powders are used for producing the parts via SLM, it can be considered—in essence—as a powder metallurgy (PM) technique. Then, as in all the PM produced parts, porosity is an inevitable consequence. Immediately, the unnotched fatigue life of such parts becomes a serious concern, especially given that the high value SLM components primary/first deployment is likely to be in fatigue critical structures. Thus, an experimental evaluation of the fatigue strength of SLM Ti_{64} and possible correlation of it to the porosity (size and distribution) is essential.

Keeping the above issues in view, the present study was initiated with the purpose of developing a comprehensive understanding of (a) the effect of the process parameters such as scan spacing and rotation on the development of the micro- and mesostructures in SLM Ti_{64}, and consequently (b) the effect of those structures on the mechanical performance of the alloy. Four different combinations of scan spacing (30 and 60  $ \mu $m) and rotation (67^\circ and 90^\circ) were utilized to produce alloy coupons with mesostructures, which range from square columnar prior  $ \beta $ grains that span the entire height of the built part to near equiaxed grains both build and transverse directions. Uniaxial tensile properties, mode I fracture toughness, fatigue crack growth (FCG) characteristics, and unnotched fatigue strength of these materials, all at room temperature, were evaluated, so as to develop structure-property relations for SLM Ti_{64}.

## 2. Background

Given the amount of current interest in AM of metal alloys, considerable literature on SLM Ti_{64} already exists. A brief review of it is presented here, with the primary purpose of putting the present work in a proper perspective. The tensile properties of SLM Ti_{64} with acicular  $ \alpha' $ were reported in a number of papers, which are summarized in Table S_{1} of the supplementary information (SI). As seen, the ranges in both  $ \sigma_{Y} $ and elongation to failure,  $ e_{f} $, reported are considerable. The high strengths in most cases are due to the acicular  $ \alpha' $ microstructure with small colony sizes, which delays the onset of plastic deformation. Low  $ \sigma_{Y} $, observed in some instances, is attributed to the high porosity present in the SLM coupons [6]. Importantly, the ductilities of SLM Ti_{64} with acicular  $ \alpha' $ are relatively low. As a consequence, a majority of the prior work was focused on modifying the metastable  $ \alpha' $, so as to obtain microstructures with either ultrafine lath or coarse lamellar  $ \alpha/\beta $ structures. Towards this end, Xu et al. [4,5] attempted at in-situ decomposition of  $ \alpha' $ into an  $ \alpha/\beta $ lamellar structure by varying the focal offset distance (FOD) of the laser beam and were able to improve the ductility to ~12%. However, FOD is rarely used as a variable in the SLM, as the temperature profile generated by FOD is sample size dependent [5]. Significant anisotropy in properties of SLM Ti_{64} was also reported, which was attributed to either porosity or columnar prior  $ \beta $ grain structure along the build direction [7].

Cain et al. [8] reported the mode I fracture toughness ( $ K_{Ic} $) of SLM Ti_{64} to be 28 and 23 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ when tested in parallel and perpendicular directions to the build direction, respectively. The latter improves to 30 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ upon a stress relieving anneal; yet these  $ K_{Ic} $ are much smaller than  $ K_{Ic} $ of conventionally manufactured Ti_{64}, which ranges between 44 and 66 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ for annealed sample according to data provided in Ref. [9]. The low  $ K_{Ic} $ of SLM alloy and anisotropy in it were attributed to porosity and the columnar prior  $ \beta $ grain structure, respectively [8]. In contrast, Hooreweder et al. [10] report a substantially higher  $ K_{Ic} $ of 50.2 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ for SLM Ti_{64} in parallel to build direction, which was further improved to 84 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ by Mohamed et al. [11] through a duplex heat treatment below the  $ \beta $-transus temperature.

A few studies also were conducted on FCG behavior of SLM Ti_{64}. While Cain et al. [8] report that the alignment of prior  $ \beta $ boundaries along the build direction increases the FCG rate, Jiao et al. [12] report the contrary, i.e., the steady state FCG rate is unaffected by the columnar grains. Cain et al. [8] and Hooreweder et al. [10] report widely ranging Paris exponents (m) of 3.2–5.8 and 3.4 respectively. It appears that the resistance to FCG of SLM Ti_{64} is better in the Paris regime as compared to that of the conventionally manufactured Ti_{64} alloys, but it is poor in the threshold region, which was attributed to the microstructure with brittle acicular  $ \alpha' $ [12]. In contrast, Edward et al. [13] suggest that prior  $ \beta $ provides a weak path for the crack propagation.

Turning to the high cycle fatigue behavior, Rafi et al. [14] reported a high fatigue strength (550 MPa) under tension-tension fatigue loading (load ratio, R=0.1) for SLM Ti_{64} as compared to cast and annealed Ti_{64} (430 MPa). They attribute this to low porosity and acicular  $ \alpha' $ which impedes the dislocation motion necessary for the crack nucleation. In contrast, Mower et al. [15] report the fatigue strength to be quite low (<100 MPa) using rotating bending fatigue (RBF) experiments, which was attributed to low ductility and high porosity in the as-SLM specimens. The pores that lead to crack initiation in Gong et al. [1]'s study were found to be non-spherical in shape with smooth internal surface; this observation suggests that they are a consequence of entrapped gas bubbles, which, in turn, indicate that high laser energy is applied to the melt pool [16]. It is also possible that the marked differences in the fatigue strengths measured by Rafi et al. [14] and Gong et al. [1] could be, in part, due to the different testing strategies. Typically, RBF, which imposes fully reversed loading during cycling, i.e., R=-1, is more severe as compared to tension-tension fatigue.

## 3. Materials and experiments

## 3.1. Materials

All the samples tested in this study were manufactured with the ELI grade Ti64 powder in an argon gas environment in an EOSINT M 280 SLM unit equipped with a Yb:YAG fiber laser. The combinations of process parameters, laser power (w), scan speed (v), spacing (l), rotation between successive layers ( $ \phi $), and layer thickness (t),

utilized for producing the test coupons are listed in Table 1. The resulting energy density,  $ J (=w/lvt) $, for each combination of the parameters is also listed. In all, coupons with four different combinations of process parameters–two different t (30 and 60  $ \mu $m), each with two different  $ \phi $ (67 and 90^\circ) – were produced. Here afterwards, we use an abbreviated nomenclature for the SLM coupons wherein the first two digits denote t and the last two  $ \phi $. For example, 6067 refers to a SLM alloy coupon produced with  $ t = 60 \mu $m and  $ \phi = 67^\circ $. In all four cases, samples with the following two geometries were prepared:  $ 64 \times 64 mm^2 $ square cross-section coupons each with 14 mm thickness for fracture toughness and FCG experiments, and 9 mm diameter cylindrical samples that are 64 mm long for RBF tests. Subsequent to manufacturing, all the samples were stress relieved by annealing them at 650 ^\circC for 3 h. The purpose of rotating the scan direction between successive layers is to impart some degree of isotropy to the produced part as well to reduce porosity [17,18]. The 67^\circ scan rotation is conventionally used in the SLM to ensure the highest number of scan rotations between the laser tracks with similar orientation.

## 3.2. Experiments

Microstructural analyses on mirror polished and etched (with the Kroll's reagent: 100 ml distilled water, 5 ml nitric acid, and 2 ml HF) samples were conducted by using an optical microscope (OM) as well as a scanning electron microscope (SEM). In the latter, both back scattered imaging and the compositional analyses were carried out. Additionally, a transmission electron microscope (TEM) operating at 300 KV was utilized for high magnification microstructural analysis and selected area electron diffraction (SAED). For this purpose, 3 mm diameter disks were first polished up to 60  $ \mu $m thickness. Their thickness was then further reduced, so as to make some portions electron transparent, to below 40 nm using a focused ion beam in the precision ion polishing system (PIPS) that uses Argon ion beam at incident angles  $ < 5^{\circ} $ from top and bottom of the sample. The samples were cooled during polishing to reduce heat induced damage ( $ -80^{\circ} $C). For qualitative analyses of phases and their orientation, X-ray diffraction (XRD) was conducted on surfaces parallel and perpendicular to build direction, using a diffractometer with a Cu anode (wavelength  $ K_{\alpha} = 1.54184 \AA $). The generated XRD data was analyzed with the aid of a commercial software. The lath size of the samples was measured quantitatively by using the electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) technique in a SEM. An area of  $ \sim 400 \times 400 \mu m^{2} $ of the electropolished samples were scanned with step size of 0.3  $ \mu $m.

Both the image analysis method (IAM) and X-ray tomography were used for the density measurement. For the former, parallel and perpendicular surfaces of all the samples were mirror polished. To measure the porosity, at least five images each, obtained at 200X and collected from different areas of both the surfaces of the samples, were utilized and analyzed by a commercial image analysis software. Additionally, X-ray tomography was utilized for 3D porosity analysis; a voxel size of  $ 0.96 \times 0.96 \times 0.96 \mu m^{3} $ was utilized and data was collected on  $ 1.5 \times 1.5 \times 1.5 mm^{3} $ specimens. The data collected was analyzed using a commercial image analysis software package. Uniaxial tensile tests were performed on dog-bone shaped specimens with 6 mm gage length and 2 (width) by 0.5 (thickness)  $ mm^{2} $ cross-section. These tests were performed under displacement control with a rate of 0.006 mm/s, which corresponds to a nominal strain rate of  $ 0.001 s^{-1} $. These specimens were machined such that the loading axis is either parallel or perpendicular to the build direction. They are referred to as 'B' and 'S' directions here afterwards. Both fracture toughness and the FCG tests were performed on mirror-polished, Chevron notch and half-sized compact-tension, C(T) specimens that are 12 mm thick and 24 mm wide in a servo-hydraulic testing machine. Room temperature fracture toughness and FCG tests were conducted in both B and S directions, i.e., the normal to the crack plane is along build or transverse-to-build directions respectively. These are according to ASTM standards E399-12 [19] and ASTM E647-15 [20] respectively.

For the FCG measurements, the tensile load was applied on the sample in sinusoidal wave form at a frequency of 10 Hz at constant R (0.1), and the crack growth was monitored with the aid of a traveling microscope with a 0.01 mm resolution (10X magnification). For the fracture toughness measurements, the specimens were precracked such that the a/W ratio is  $ \sim 0.45–0.55 $ (a - crack length, W - width of the specimen), before being fractured at a displacement rate of 0.01 mm/s, and  $ K_{Ic} $ was estimated using the equation given in ASTM E399-12. For the FCG measurements also the samples were precracked up to a/W  $ \sim 0.45–0.55 $, before the load shedding method prescribed in ASTM E647-15 was employed to determine the threshold stress intensity factor range for fatigue crack growth,  $ \Delta K_{0} $. In this procedure, the stress intensity factor range,  $ \Delta K $, was decreased by 10% after each 0.2 mm crack growth until a crack propagation rate (da/dN) of  $ 2 \times 10^{-9} $ m/cycle was achieved; the corresponding  $ \Delta K $ was designated  $ \Delta K_{0} $. Once  $ \Delta K_{0} $ is obtained,  $ \Delta K $ was increased by 10% for each 0.2 mm crack growth, so as to determine the steady state FCG characteristics, until the specimen fractures.

For the unnotched fatigue strength (FS) determination using RBF tests, the cylindrical blocks were machined into hourglass shaped specimens with gauge length of 22 mm and minimum diameter ~ 5 mm, and then samples were mirror polished before testing. The first set of tests were conducted at a stress amplitude,  $ S_{a} $, which corresponds to approximately half the tensile strength. For the next set,  $ S_{a} $ is reduced by ~50 MPa. This was continued until reaching a stress level, where all the three tested samples survive a minimum of  $ 10^{7} $ cycles; this  $ S_{a} $ is designated as FS. Note that the unnotched fatigue tests could be performed on specimens in only orientation (specimen axis coinciding with the build direction) due to the fact that the SLM produced cylindrical blocks only allow for such testing. Post-mortem fractographic analyses of all the samples was performed in a SEM.

The schematic displayed in Fig. 1b illustrates that the laser tracks of successive layers overlap because the high  $ J(55.6\,\mathrm{J}/\mathrm{mm}^{3}) $ employed, which results in a large melt pool. As a result, they form a mesostructure with a square cross section in B-plane and long columns grains that span the entire height of the coupon in S-plane. The side length of the square prior  $ \beta $ grains seen in B plane,  $ \sim140 \mu m $, is approximately equal to the scan spacing between the laser tracks. These columnar prior  $ \beta $ grains contain fine and acicular  $ \alpha/\alpha' $ laths, while the grain boundaries are decorated with the  $ \alpha $ phase. The high cooling rate associated with the SLM process and the resulting steep thermal gradients are the reasons for the formation of the  $ \alpha/\alpha' $ laths [21]. The martensitic  $ \alpha' $ phase has HCP crystal structure with a c/a ratio of 1.589, which is slightly lower than that of the  $ \alpha $-Ti (c/a = 1.59–1.6) [4]. The orientation of the  $ \alpha/\alpha' $ laths depends on the prior  $ \beta $ boundaries as the laths nucleate and grow from them on the favorable variant plane amongst the possible 12 variants [22].

Higher magnification images of the microstructures of the S-plane of 3090 case, displayed in Fig. 1d, shows that most of the laths are oriented at  $ \sim45^{\circ} $ to B direction. This is because the cubic prior  $ \beta $ and the hexagonal  $ \alpha' $ are related through Burger's orientation relationships [23]. Of the 12 possible relationships,  $ \{110\}_{\beta}//\{0001\}_{\alpha'} $ and  $ <111>\}_{\beta}//<1120>\}_{\alpha} $ are the most favorable ones, which results in the growth of the laths along  $ \pm45^{\circ} $ [22,24]. Further, both Fig. 1a and d indicate to an epitaxial growth, which results in columnar prior  $ \beta $ grains that grow in the  $ <001>\}_{\beta} $ directions that are normal to the deposited powder layers. The observation of a mesostructure with the columnar grains implies that heterogeneous grain nucleation sites from within the melt pools are absent and the growth of the underlying grains during solidification of the melt pools dominates. The high energy density utilized for the SLM process, which melts all the powder particles in the melt pool at any given instance, and hence eliminates the possibility of any of them acting as heterogeneous nucleation sites, is a process parameter that contributes to such columnar  $ \beta $ grain growth [4]. Consequently, the columnar grains span the entire thickness of the Ti_{64} blocks that are built via SLM using this particular combination of process parameters.

When the coupons were fabricated with 30  $ \mu $m–67^\circ combination, the melt pool from one layer does not exactly overlap with the next layer due to the 67^\circ scan rotation, which results in an irregular arrangement of the prior  $ \beta $ grain boundaries, as schematically illustrated in Fig. 2b that shows the mismatch between the laser tracks in both B- and S-planes. Because of this mismatch, prior  $ \beta $ grains appear equiaxed in the B-plane, as seen in Fig. 2c, with an average grain diameter of 140  $ \mu $m, which again is equal to S that was employed during SLM. The microstructure of the S-plane, displayed in Fig. 2d, also shows columnar prior  $ \beta $ grains. However, the walls of these grains are jagged and discontinuous because of the mismatch between the successive laser tracks.

Representative mesostructure of the sample with  $ t = 60 \mu m $ and  $ \phi = 90^{\circ} $, displayed in Fig. 3a, shows that the square-shaped grains were present in it too, which are similar to that seen in 3090 case, because of the overlapping of the laser tracks; 3D schematic in Fig. 3b shows the formation of the square grains in the B-plane. However, the side lengths of these grains are now  $ \sim 120 \mu m $, reflecting the reduced l of  $ 120 \mu m $ that was utilized. This observation illustrates that the size of prior  $ \beta $ grain depends on the spacing between the laser tracks. Qualitative analyses of the different phases and their orientation within the SLM samples were performed by recourse to the XRD experiments. Since the growth of the laths in Ti_{64} depends on the direction of the heat conduction from the melt zone, and hence can be controlled using the scan strategy [25], XRD scans on both of BD and SD planes were recorded (Fig. 5) so as to examine the anisotropy in the microstructure as well. While the presence of hexagonal  $ \alpha/\alpha' $ phases was confirmed through these, diffraction peaks corresponding to  $ \beta $ phase were absent in the scans suggesting the absence it in as-built SLM Ti_{64} coupons. The XRD scans from the samples B_{6090} and S_{6067} show a low-intensity peak at  $ 2\theta = 27^{\circ} $, which may correspond to Ti3Al phase. The presence of Ti3Al along the dark bands in the microstructure of SLM Ti_{64} was reported by Thijs et al. [25]. It is likely that this intermetallic phase forms due to segregation of Al to the melt zone boundaries, which is a result of relatively fast cooling rate, as the Ti3Al peak was missing from the scans of 3090 and 3067 samples that are manufactured with higher J. The observation of the strongest intensity peak for the (110) plane in the scan obtained from B_{3090}, displayed in Fig. 5a, as compared to peak intensities in other samples, indicates that the laths are relatively more randomly orientated in this sample as compared to others. Furthermore, the intensity peaks corresponding to (102), (110), (103), (200), (112), and (201) in scans of S_{3090} and S_{6090} are relatively smaller than the corresponding ones in S_{3067} and S_{6067}. A possible explanation for this that the scan rotation of  $ 67^{\circ} $ results in shorter prior  $ \beta $ grains.

Some of the prior studies on SLM Ti_{64} indicate that the microstructural phase within the grains is fully martensitic  $ \alpha' $ with  $ \alpha $ phase being present only at prior  $ \beta $ boundaries [3,25,26]. Some other studies, in contrast, report that the microstructure within the grains contains both  $ \alpha $ and  $ \alpha' $ [27,28]. It is important to note that the former set of studies have utilized XRD for their characterization, which is incapable distinguishing  $ \alpha $ from  $ \alpha' $, both of which have HCP crystal structure and the difference in their c/a ratios is small. Backscattered electron (or Z contrast) imaging in SEM can resolve this as  $ \alpha' $, being a non-equilibrium phase, contains higher amount of V, which is a  $ \beta $ stabilizer, as compared to  $ \alpha $ [4,29]. A representative optical image of the pores in sample S_{6067} is displayed in Fig. 8a. The calculated apparent densities,  $ \rho $, of 3090, 3067, 6090, 6067 samples are  $ 99.76 \pm 0.16\% $,  $ 99.83 \pm 0.08\% $,  $ 99.83 \pm 0.07\% $ and  $ 99.77 \pm 0.15\% $, respectively. The corresponding porosities, measured using X-ray tomography, are 0.37%, 0.39%, 0.29%, and 0.17% respectively, which are approximately similar to those estimated using image analysis technique. Importantly,  $ \rho $ is >99.5% in all the cases, which implies that the coupons produced via SLM for the present set of studies can be considered as near-full dense. Furthermore, these  $ \rho $ are either better or at the least comparable to those reported in literature for SLM Ti_{64} with  $ \alpha' $ microstructure [6].

## 4.2.2. Fracture toughness

The measured  $ K_{Ic} $ values, listed in Table 2, range from 48 to 58 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $. In all the cases, the combinations of  $ K_{Ic} $ and  $ \sigma_{Y} $ confirm the dominance of plane strain conditions as prescribed by the standard: ASTM E399. The present  $ K_{Ic} $ for SLM Ti_{64} are substantially higher than 28 and 31 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ reported by Cain et al. [8] for SLM Ti_{64}, which they attribute to high levels of porosity in their samples, whereas the porosity levels in the present set of SLM specimens are substantially smaller. In fact,  $ K_{Ic} $ of the present SLM samples are similar to those of conventionally manufactured and annealed Ti_{64} alloy with a similar grain size and fine colony structure, whose  $ K_{Ic} $, measured under plane strain conditions, are reported to range between 44 and 66 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ [35]. This similarity leads to conclude that an optimized SLM process neither adversely affects the toughness of Ti_{64} nor substantially enhances it. The latter point is important as the meso-structure, imparted by the line-by-line and layer-by-layer nature of the SLM process, was shown to enhance  $ K_{Ic} $ substantially in Al-Si alloys [2]. The reason behind the toughness enhancement in that case, in spite of the marked reductions in tensile ductility, were suggested to be the crack path tortuosity that was facilitated by the brittle Si networks decorating the hatch lines. The relatively strong metallurgical bonding between the hatch lines in the SLM alloys of the present study prevents any such pronounced crack path tortuosity and hence  $ K_{Ic} $ is not enhanced by the mesostructure. Similar conclusions were drawn by Suryawanshi et al. [36,37] who measured  $ K_{Ic} $ of 18Ni (300) maraging steel as well as 316L stainless steel samples made using SLM and found their  $ K_{Ic} $ to be similar to respective values reported on their counterparts that are manufactured using conventional techniques.

A strong metallurgical bonding between the successive layers also leads to the absence of anisotropy in  $ K_{Ic} $ of three alloys whereas only 3067 exhibits some anisotropy –that too not a significant one– in its toughness. This, again, is in contrast to an earlier report on fracture toughness of SLM Ti_{64} [8], wherein  $ K_{Ic} $ was reported to be highly anisotropic (28 and 16 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ in S and B directions) that was attributed to the brittleness of martensitic  $ \alpha' $ and pronounced texture because of columnar prior  $ \beta $ grains. Since these microstructural parameters are also common to the SLM Ti_{64} of the current study, we infer that it is neither of these features are the reasons behind the anisotropy observed by Hooreweder et al. [10]. Instead, it again may be due to the high levels of porosity. Coupons of Ti_{64} manufactured by the other AM processes such as electron beam melting (EBM) also show near isotropic tensile and fracture properties [38].

## 4.2.3. Fatigue crack growth

Representative FCG data for 3090, measured in two directions so as to evaluate the anisotropy, is displayed in Fig. 11 (FCG plots for the other three alloys are given in Fig. S_{2} of SI.). Through this data, the empirical Paris equation,  $ da/dN = C(\Delta K)^m $ that relates the crack growth rate per cycle ( $ da/dN $) to  $ \Delta K $, where C and m are material constants that are often referred to as Paris constant and exponent respectively, were fitted. These values and  $ \Delta K_0 $ for all the alloy-direction combinations examined in this study are listed in Table 2. It indicates that  $ \Delta K_0 $, which ranges between 5.3 and 5.8 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ overall, is relatively insensitive to the meso- and/or micro-structures of the SLM Ti_{64}. Furthermore, it is similar to 5.4 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ that was reported for conventionally manufactured Ti_{64} with an  $ \alpha/\beta $ duplex microstructure [40]. Also, the anisotropy in  $ \Delta K_0 $ is insignificant. In contrast, the estimated values of m for 3090 and 3067 suggest that the rates at which fatigue cracks grow under steady state conditions, as indicated by m, are significantly anisotropic (for 3090 in particular) whereas near-identical m values are obtained for both directions for the other two cases. On average, samples with 67^\circ scan rotation show higher m, and hence lower resistance to FCG, as compared to samples with 90^\circ scan rotation. The overall range of m values, 2.5–3.5, is smaller and lower than the range of 3.2–5.8 reported for SLM Ti-64 by Cain et al. in an earlier study [8] and even the best of conventionally manufactured Ti_{64} [41]. In this context, it is worth noting that the FCG rates in coupons produced with the other AM processes, namely wire + arc additive manufacturing [42] and EBM [38], which impart lamellar microstructure that is similar to that seen in the present study, are also lower as compared to those in the conventionally manufactured Ti_{64}.

## 5. Discussion

## 5.1. Strength, ductility and toughness of SLM Ti_{64}

Columnar grain structure in SLM Ti_{64}, which was reported in earlier studies as well, is considered as one of the important drawbacks of SLM Ti_{64}, as such a structure was construed to be detrimental for the mechanical performance (anisotropy in properties as well as provision of paths that are less resistant to crack growth) of the Ti_{64} components built via SLM [7,26]. Therefore, considerable efforts to find ways to mitigate such structures are underway. The focus of such efforts is to use alloy design strategies, through minor alloying additions that facilitate homogeneous nucleation sites within the melt pool, so as to break down the columnar structure and impart an equiaxed microstructure to the built part. Results presented in the preceding section are interesting from this aspect for two specific reasons. (a) They demonstrate that it is possible to break the columnar structure down by a judicious selection of the process parameters. This obviates the need for minor additions of inoculants that could lead to additional consequences in terms of microstructures and mechanical properties that may not always be desirable. Furthermore, the observation that prior  $ \beta $ grain sizes in the B plane scale approximately with the scan spacing suggests that it offers an additional process parameter for tailoring the microstructures.

Importantly, the comprehensive mechanical property evaluation carried out in this study shows that the presence of a columnar structure may not lead to a serious penalty in terms of either strength or anisotropy; as discussed in section 4.2.1, all the alloys

show  $ \sigma_{Y} $ in excess of 1000 MPa. Such a high strength is possibly due to the presence of fine acicular  $ \alpha'/\alpha $ lath structure within the microstructure of all alloys. Then, it is reasonable to expect the microstructural length scale of relevance from the strength point of view to be  $ \alpha' $ lath size. (Recall that the plastic deformation in Ti_{64} can be affected by several different length scales, with one of them being dominant.) To ascertain this, we have utilized EBSD data, which are displayed in Fig. 12. From it, we note that the average lath sizes as well as their distributions in all the four cases are similar. Another factor that could affect  $ \sigma_{Y} $ is the relative orientation of the laths with respect to the tensile loading direction, especially since some of the alloys have columnar grain structure. However, the laths are oriented at  $ \sim45^{\circ} $ to BD in all cases (see figures c and d in Figs. 1–4). As a result, the resolved shear stresses should be similar for sample tested in B and S directions. This is possibly the reason for broadly similar  $ \sigma_{Y} $ of all alloys, in spite of the significant differences in the mesostructure. As already mentioned, the absence of anisotropy in any considerable level is due to the fact the metallurgical bonding between grains/mesostructural constituent elements within the material are strong.

While the lowest  $ e_{f} $ was observed for B_{3090} case may raise concern, it has to be kept in mind that ductility per se is not of major concern in the context of AM parts as long as the fracture toughness levels are satisfactory. In this context, it is important to note that ductility is an important metric of merit for conventionally manufactured alloys for the following two reasons. A material with low ductility is (i) often non-amenable to forming and shaping operations such as rolling, extrusion, and forging, and (ii) likely to exhibit low  $ K_{Ic} $. In the case of SLM components, since near-net shaped parts are made directly, i.e., no further mechanical operations are required, the first reason becomes inconsequential. The presence of a mesostructure in SLM alloys could make ductility and  $ K_{Ic} $ uncorrelated. An example of this is SLM Al-Si alloy, which shows high fracture initiation toughness despite having low ductility. In the current context of SLM Ti_{64}, while no pronounced effect of the mesostructure on the fracture toughness could be seen, the measured  $ K_{Ic} $ are within the same range of those reported for conventionally manufactured Ti_{64}. Here, it must be mentioned that the  $ K_{Ic} $ of Ti_{64} could be highly sensitive to the characteristic (or dominant) microstructural length scale ( $ l^{*} $) with a square root

As already mentioned, the microstructural length scales within the SLM Ti_{64} examined in the present study, whether it is the prior  $ \beta $ grain size or the lath sizes, are approximately similar irrespective of the processing conditions. This is possibly the reason for the broadly similar  $ K_{IC} $ values exhibited by all the four SLM Ti_{64} alloys examined in this work.

In the above context, it is to be noted that the microstructures and (hence) the mechanical properties of the SLM alloys can be influenced by post-SLM heat treatments. In the present study, the coupons were subjected to a post-SLM process annealing (at 650 ^\circC for 3 h), whose sole purpose was to relieve the internal residual stresses that build-up during the SLM process. Since 650 ^\circC is much lower than the \beta-transus temperature of Ti_{64} (~960 ^\circC), it does not alter the microstructure in any significant manner. However, any heat treatment at temperatures that are close to the \beta-Transus temperature can alter the microstructure in a significant manner. Therefore, such processes would offer opportunities to tailor the mechanical performance of the SLM Ti_{64}. We are currently exploring these possibilities, especially for enhancing the fracture and fatigue resistances.

## 5.2. Fatigue crack growth behavior

While near-threshold FCG behavior is expected to be highly sensitive to the microstructure, the steady state crack growth rate is not [44]. Thus, the similarity in microstructural length scales across the four different SLM Ti_{64} examined in this study could also be the reason for the small overall range of  $ \Delta K_{0} $ (5.3–5.8 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $) that was experimentally measured in them. Here, it is important to recognize that m is a global indicator of FCG resistance offered by a material. Hence, it does not accurately reflect the information about the local effect of the microstructural features on FCG; features such as grain boundaries or lamellae size can indeed locally retard or accelerate crack growth, especially near the threshold regime. For example, in alloys that contain ductile and brittle constituent phases, the cracks can propagate faster through the latter whereas the ductile phase can retard crack growth due to the relatively large plastic zone that forms ahead of the crack tip, which dissipates more energy in the form of plastic work [44].

For understanding the local effects on the near-threshold FCG, both cyclic and dynamic plastic zones that occur ahead of the crack tip need to be considered. The sizes of these are controlled by  $ \Delta K $ and the maximum stress intensity factor of the fatigue cycle,  $ K_{\text{max}} (= \Delta K / (1 - R)) $, respectively. While the cyclic plastic zone is compressive in nature for mode I loading, the monotonic one is tensile in nature, whose size,  $ r_p $, under plane strain conditions is given by Ref. [38]:

 $$ r_{\mathrm{p}}=\frac{1}{3\pi}\left(\frac{K_{\max}}{\sigma_{\mathrm{Y}}}\right)^{2} $$ 

Sadananda and Vasudevan, who examined the near-threshold FCG behavior of Ti alloys [45] in detail, conclude that  $ K_{max} $ is the dominating factor than  $ \Delta K $, especially when the plastic zone size is similar to  $ l^{*} $. In a separate study, Sadananda et al. [46] suggest that  $ r_{p} $ for effective interaction, to be  $ 1.5 \times l^{*} $. Thus, when  $ r_{p} \sim 1.5 \times l^{*} $, the microstructure can have pronounced influence on FCG.

The SLM Ti_{64} alloys examined in this study have three microstructural length scales, viz., lath size, t, and l. (As noted already, the latter corresponds to the prior  $ \beta $ grain size in the S plane). Since these are 5–30, 30 and 60, and 120 and 140  $ \mu $m respectively, effective interaction between the cracks and these length scales can be expected for  $ r_{p} \leq 210 \mu $m. Rewriting the Paris equation in terms of  $ r_{p} $ gives:

$$ \frac{d a}{d N}=((1-R)\sigma_{\mathrm{Y}})^{m}\times\left(3\pi r_{\mathrm{p}}\right)^{m/2} $$ 

Thus, a log-log plot of  $ da/dN $ vs.  $ r_{p} $ should yield a linear trend in experimental data, with significant deviations from linearity highlighting the effect of microstructural features on the near-threshold FCG.

Log-log plots  $ da/dN $ vs.  $ r_p $ for all the eight cases whose FCG behavior was examined in this study are displayed in Fig. 13. The following are some general features observed from these plots. (i) In all the cases, significant deviations from linearity was observed between  $ \sim $40 and 90  $ \mu $m. This implies that both the lath size, especially the larger laths, and t play an important role in deflecting fatigue cracks. (ii) Such deviation in the FCG rates become muted for  $ r_p \geq 180-210 $  $ \mu $m. Furthermore, note that in the samples with  $ \phi = 90^\circ $ scan rotation (Fig. 13a and b), marked deviations can be noted specifically at either 180 or 210  $ \mu $m, due to the fixed size of the microstructural feature, namely the prior  $ \beta $ size. In samples with  $ \phi = 67^\circ $, in contrast, the deviations occur at random  $ r_p $, as the prior  $ \beta $ grain size does not have a specific value (Fig. 13c and d). An estimate of  $ r_p $ for the experimentally measured  $ \Delta K_0 $, which ranges between 5.3 and 5.8 MPa  $ \sqrt{m} $ (see Table 2), yields 28–40  $ \mu $m, which suggests that the prior  $ \beta $ grain sizes, which range between 120 and 140  $ \mu $m in B plane, and their morphology (as in columnar vs. equiaxed) may not affect the threshold. Likewise, the lath thickness' are too small (up to 2  $ \mu $m) than these estimated  $ r_p $; thus, this length scale may not also be the determining factor for  $ \Delta K_0 $. On the other hand, the average colony sizes of  $ \sim $10–15  $ \mu $m are in the similar range of interaction. Hence, it may the colony size that determines  $ \Delta K_0 $. The similarity in the colony sizes of all the SLM Ti_{64} samples examined in this study, and their independence from the orientation of testing, coupled with the fact that  $ \Delta K_0 $ is itself independent of orientation and or processing condition, supports such a hypothesis. Further work is needed, however, wherein the colony size is systematically varied and its influence on  $ \Delta K_0 $ is examined experimentally, for ascertaining this hypothesis.

These interactions between the near-threshold fatigue crack and the microstructure are illustrated through representative micrographs captured from the side surfaces of the FCG specimens. Fig. 14a shows the fatigue crack deflection from a prior  $ \beta $ grain boundary in the S_{6067} whereas 14b shows the crack growth along it in S_{3090}. Crack growth along the prior  $ \beta $ boundary in the sample S_{3090} makes it more anisotropic compared to the other samples. However, deviation of the crack by the melt zone boundary in 6090 as indicated in Fig. 14c makes it less anisotropic. The other effective crack deflectors in the microstructure are thick laths, as shown in Fig. 14d.

## 6. Summary and conclusions

With the objective of establishing structure-property correlations in SLM Ti64, detailed microstructural and mechanical characterizations were performed on coupons produced using four different combinations of SLM processing parameters. The followings are the key conclusions that can be drawn from this study.

The columnar prior  $ \beta $ grain structure, which appears common in most of the SLM Ti_{64} alloys, can be circumvented by the choice of an appropriate combination of layer thickness and scan rotation. An equiaxed microstructure, thus obtained, exhibits the optimum combination of high strength, ductility, and fracture toughness. Furthermore, suggestions of prior studies on SLM Ti_{64} that the presence of  $ \alpha' $ in the as-built alloys' microstructure is detrimental to the ductility do not appear be valid, as excellent combinations of strength and ductility could be obtained in this study without having to resort to additions processing steps that would aid in the decomposition of  $ \alpha' $ into  $ \alpha $ and  $ \beta $.

2. Both the unnotched fatigue strength and the fatigue crack growth characteristics of SLM T64 were found to be comparable to the respective properties of the conventionally manufactured Ti_{64}, although it is possible to further enhance the fatigue strength via further optimization of SLM process parameters so as to reduce the porosity. While the Paris exponent, m, does not provide any new insights into the microstructural effects on the FCG characteristics, examination of the plastic zone sizes near the threshold regime indicate that the colony size may be the microstructural length scale that has a direct bearing on  $ \Delta K_{0} $.
