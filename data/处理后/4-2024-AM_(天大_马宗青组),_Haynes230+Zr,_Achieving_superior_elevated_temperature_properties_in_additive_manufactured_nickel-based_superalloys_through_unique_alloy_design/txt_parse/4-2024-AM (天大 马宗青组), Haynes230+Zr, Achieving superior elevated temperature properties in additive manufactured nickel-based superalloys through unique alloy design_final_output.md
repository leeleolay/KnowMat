## Abstract

Additive manufacturing (AM) offers a new solution for the integrated fabrication of complex components, supplementing the shortcomings of conventional subtractive manufacturing. However, the forming defects owing to alloy unsuitability and the lack of long-term service stability of the components seriously limit its widespread engineering applications. Here we developed a nickel-based superalloy, Haynes 230AM, suitable for AM by using Zirconium (Zr) alloying strategy in Haynes 230 alloy, which is crack-free and has outstanding elevated temperature properties. Compared with commercial Haynes 230 alloy fabricated by rolling and AM, the printed Haynes 230AM in our work achieves about 700 % improvement in creep lifetime, breaking the status quo that the creep properties of AM fabricated alloys are generally inferior to the corresponding conventional manufactured alloys. Our results reveal that the introduction of Zr element optimized the microstructure of the alloy. The superior creep resistance of Haynes 230AM stems from the uniform distribution of fine ZrC carbide, the precipitation of  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phase along close-packed planes and the formation of unique matrix distortion stripes, which impede dislocation motion and delay the formation of creep defects. Moreover, the Zr element can also improve internal oxide scales, reduce oxidation weight gain by 20 % and does not deteriorate the mechanical properties after long-term thermal exposure. These results can provide guidance for the design of high-performance AM-specific superalloys.

## 1. Introduction

Nickel-based superalloys are widely used as critical materials for hot-end components in aerospace and energy production areas owing to their excellent mechanical properties, thermal stability and oxidation resistance [1,2]. Generally, the nickel-based superalloy parts are fabricated by casting, forging and powder metallurgy. However, conventional manufacturing processes are increasingly difficult to meet the demand for lightweight and integrated fabrication of intricate components [3]. Additive manufacturing (AM), also known as 3D printing, exhibits outstanding advantages in the manufacture of geometrically complex components due to its unique layer-by-layer fabrication technology [4–6]. To date, there are still bottlenecks to the widespread engineering application of AM technology in the fabrication of nickel-based superalloys. On the one hand, most of the existing commercial alloys are unable to accommodate the spatially variable temperature fields and ultrafast cooling rates during AM fabrication, and thus exhibit significant cracking [7–9]. On the other hand, the heterogeneous microstructure and the defects generated by non-equilibrium solidification make it difficult for alloys to achieve the performance levels obtained by conventional manufacturing methods, especially under long-term service conditions [5,10–12].

Optimizing the composition of existing alloys to improve their printability has recently attracted extensive attention in the AM field. It has been shown that low melting liquid films generated by local segregation of solute elements at grain boundaries widen the solidification interval of grains bridging and can lead to significant cracking of printed alloys under thermal stress [13–15]. Substantial researches utilizing atomic probe technology, non-equilibrium Scheil model, and hot crack sensitivity index theory have confirmed that the segregation of Zr, B, Si, Mn, etc. elements increase the cracking tendency of nickel-based superalloys and that the reduction or removal of these elements can effectively inhibit cracking [15–17]. For instance, Després et al. [13] achieved crack-free fabrication of IN738LC alloy by removing Zr from

the alloy composition. Tomus et al. [18] effectively mitigated cracking in Hastelloy X alloy by reducing the Si and C content. However, it is well accepted that the existence of trace alloying elements such as Zr, B in nickel-based superalloys improves grain boundary strength and reduces the rate of grain boundary diffusion, thereby greatly improving creep resistance and notch sensitivity [19–22]. Therefore, the contradiction between the improved printability of AM-fabricated nickel-based superalloys and the degradation of long-term service mechanical properties caused by the reduction in the content of grain boundary-acting elements needs to be overcome for composition optimization.

Designing alloy compositions based on the unique microstructural features generated by the AM process can provide an opportunity to develop alloys with advanced mechanical properties [23,24]. Recently, we reported that the continuous network of segregated phases along cellular boundaries can effectively suppress cracking by relieving stress concentrations when the Zr content exceeds a certain value in nickel-based superalloys fabricated by AM [25]. Although the addition of 1 wt% Zr eliminated cracking in nickel-based superalloys and enabled outstanding room temperature mechanical properties, Zr is usually present as a trace alloying element (generally < 0.2 wt%) in existing nickel-based superalloys. Excessive Zr content can lead to increased solubility of carbon in the matrix, resulting in lamellar carbide precipitation, which can serve a favorable position for creep voids and crack propagation [26]. Moreover, it has also been reported that the Ni-Zr phase can reduce the stress-rupture life of cast nickel-based superalloys [27]. However, extraordinary properties have been reported by modulation of elements beyond the constant amount in previous work, such as Zhang et al. added 8.5 wt% of Cu to pure Ti and achieved the transformation of columnar to equiaxial grains in AM-fabricated samples and increased the strength by 270 MPa [23]. Choudhuri et al. achieved a 600 % improvement in the creep life of Mg-Nd alloy by introducing 0.4 at% zinc [28]. Therefore, it remains to be further clarified that Zr additions beyond the norm may have a negative/beneficial effect on the creep lifetime, oxidation resistance and microstructural stability of AM-fabricated nickel-based superalloys in long-term service at elevated temperatures.

Here we verified the performance of nickel-based superalloys fabricated by laser powder bed fusion (LPBF, one of AM technology) under elevated temperature long-term service conditions utilizing creep testing, tensile testing, and isothermal oxidation. Two types of the nickel-based superalloys Haynes 230 and Haynes 230AM (1 wt% Zr added) were used for comparative verification of the above-mentioned properties changes. Benefitting from the addition of Zr, the Haynes 230AM alloy exhibits excellent creep and oxidation resistance, and still maintains stable mechanical properties after long-term thermal exposure. These results provide important guidance for facilitating the compositional design and engineering application of similar alloys suitable for AM with excellent properties and printability.

## 2. Methods

## 2.1. Sample fabrication and heat treatment

Two composition nickel-based superalloys were used as comparative experimental alloys in this work. Compared with Haynes 230 (H230) alloy, Haynes 230AM (H230AM) alloy added 1 wt% Zr. The alloy powders were prepared by argon atomization and the detailed compositions are listed in Table 1. The LPBF fabricated samples were produced on a Concept Laser M2 machine with the laser power of 300 W, the scanning speed of 1100 mm/s, the layer thickness of 50  $ \mu $m and the hatch spacing of 100  $ \mu $m. The strip scanning strategy was employed in the fabrication process and the new layer was rotated 67^\circ in the scanning direction relative to the previously deposited layer. All fabrication processes were carried out in an argon atmosphere.

The H230AM samples were heat treated in the muffle furnace at 1200 ^\circC for 1 h, then air cooled to room temperature. In order to close the cracks and eliminate their interference with the mechanical properties, the H230 samples were subjected to hot isostatic pressing (HIP) heat treatment. The HIP treatment was carried out at 1175 ^\circC for 4 h under 160 MPa of isostatic pressure, followed by argon cooled to room temperature. No cracks were observed in the H230 and H230AM samples after heat treatment (Suppl. Fig. 1).

## 2.2. Mechanical testing

Tension creep tests were conducted at 900 ^\circC with stresses of 45 MP, 65 MPa and 85 MPa according to ASTM E139–11 standard. Wherein, the creep test of H230AM at 45 MPa was artificially interrupted when the creep life reached 1500 h. Creep samples were machined from heat treated samples with a diameter of 5 mm and a gage length of 25 mm. The loading axis of the force is perpendicular to the building direction. To investigate the stability of the mechanical properties of H230 and H230AM after long-term thermal exposure, tensile tests were carried out on samples after heat treatment as well as after 200 h and 500 h of oxidization at 900 ^\circC. Dog-bone-shaped tensile sample with a gauge dimension of  $ 5 \times 1.5 \times 1.5 $ mm was used for testing. The uniaxial tensile test was carried out at 900 ^\circC with a strain rate of  $ 10^{-3} \, s^{-1} $ along the sample perpendicular to the building direction.

## 2.3. Oxidation testing

H230 and H230AM samples were cut from heat-treated samples. The surface of the samples was polished using the 1.5  $ \mu $m diamond paster and then ultrasonically cleaned in ethanol. Isothermal oxidation tests were carried out in a muffle furnace at 900  $ ^{\circ} $C in air, and the weight gains at different exposure times were measured using an electronic balance with an accuracy of 0.1 mg.

## 2.4. Microstructural characterization

Macroscopic and microstructure of the alloys were characterized by optical microscopy (OM, Olympus) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM, S-4800). The sample was electrolytically etched in a solution of 20 % sulfuric acid at a voltage of 5 V for 10 s. Electron backscattered diffraction (EBSD) and energy dispersive spectrometer (EDS) testing were performed on a JSM-7800 F SEM with a step size of 1 µm. The phase analysis of the oxidized samples was performed using a Bruker D8 Advanced X-ray diffractometer (XRD) with a step size of 0.02^\circ. All XRD scans were carried out in the plane of the alloy perpendicular to the building direction. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) observations were performed using a 200 kV Talos F200X microscope to determine the detailed phases and structures. Atomic scale imaging was acquired by spherical aberration corrected scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM, JEM-ARM200F). The TEM samples were first mechanically ground to 30 µm and then thinned by ion-milling using a Gatan PIPS II 695 instrument.

## 3. Results and discussion

## 3.1. Microstructures of the as-printed and heat-treated alloys

Fig. 1 provides the microstructural characterization of the H230 and H230AM samples. The H230 sample exhibits severe cracking with a crack density of 2.81 mm/mm² (Fig. 1a), while the cracking is completely eliminated in the H230AM sample. Further characterization reveals that the H230AM has a significantly higher fraction of segregation phases at cell boundaries compared to the H230 (Fig. 1b, f). It has been indicated that the network segregation is the Ni-Zr phase in our previous study [25]. Benefiting from the continuous and uniform intercell liquid film introduced by Zr element segregation, it plays the role of backfilling and alleviating grain boundary stress concentration, thereby suppressing the formation of cracks during the solidification [25,29]. After HIP heat treatment, the cracks in H230 are completely annihilated (Suppl. Fig. 1a), and granular precipitated phases with an average size of 1.3 \pm 0.2 \mum and 0.45 \pm 0.05 \mum, are distributed at grain boundaries and grains interior (Fig. 1c), respectively. Based on the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern of TEM and EDS results, it is further confirmed that the phases distributed at intragranular and intergranular are both W-rich M₆C carbides (Suppl. Fig. 2a, b). Therein, the intragranular M₆C precipitated along the cell boundaries (Fig. 1d), which is consistent with the previous study [30]. Specifically, the precipitate free zone occurs on both sides of the grain boundary, as indicated in Fig. 1c, because the coarsening of intergranular M₆C consumes the carbon in the surrounding area. As a comparison, the network segregation phases are completely dissolved in the H230AM after normal atmospheric heat treatment, and extensive precipitated phases with sizes of 166 \pm 18 nm are uniformly distributed in the matrix, as shown in Fig. 1g. According to the SAED pattern and EDS results, the precipitated phase in the H230AM is identified as ZrC carbide (Suppl. Fig. 2c), which is distributed along cell and grain boundaries (Fig. 1h). It can be seen that the introduction of 1 wt% Zr element in H230 alloy transforms the carbides from M₆C to ZrC in the heat-treated microstructure. Comparing Fig. 1c and g, it can be noticed that the coarse carbides along the grain boundaries in the H230 sample are obviously suppressed in the H230AM sample. Moreover, the number densities of carbides in H230AM and H230 were counted to be 1.71 \mum⁻² and 0.52 \mum⁻², respectively. The transformation of carbide characteristics stems from the high carbon affinity of the Zr element and its inhibition of the diffusion of the M₆C forming element towards grain boundaries [21,31]. The extensive cell boundaries in the microstructure are preserved after heat treatment which allows ZrC particles to be uniformly distributed in the matrix. It is well known that the coarse particles can generate the stress concentration at grain boundaries and then facilitate priority failure of the components in service [32,33]. According to the Ashby-Orowan relationship [1], the high-density of precipitated particles contributes to the improvement of strength. Therefore, the uniform distribution of high-density fine ZrC particles in H230AM may be beneficial for long-term service properties at elevated temperatures.

## 3.2. The creep properties

Tensile creep tests were performed at 900 ^\circC with the applied stresses from 45 to 85 MPa. Fig. 2a shows the creep curves of the H230 and H230AM samples. It can be seen that the creep curves are mainly in the steady-state and tertiary creep stages, and the creep rupture time of H230AM is significantly longer than that of H230 at the same applied stress. Specifically, the creep lifetime of H230AM is improved by 672 %, 756 % and >787 % compared to H230 at stresses of 45 MPa, 65 MPa and 85 MPa, respectively. The creep life of H230 after HIP treatment is at the same level as its conventionally manufactured counterparts, as shown in Fig. 2b. Remarkably, the creep lifetime of H230AM is also considerably better than the creep lifetimes of similar nickel-based superalloys under the same test conditions [34–38], breaking the status quo that the creep property of the alloys fabricated by AM is generally inferior to that of the conventional processes (forging, rolling, etc.). It is known that the improvement of creep lifetime mainly depends on the extension of the steady-state creep stage, which is controlled by the minimum creep rate [39]. As shown in Fig. 2c, the steady-state creep rate of H230AM is about an order of magnitude lower than the H230 under the same applied stress. In addition, the steady-state creep rate ( $ \dot{\varepsilon} $) is generally related to the applied stress ( $ \sigma $), and the creep behavior can be evaluated according to the Arrhenius equation [40]:

The decreased steady-state creep rate and increased stress exponent imply that H230AM has a higher creep resistance and a different creep deformation mechanism from H230. To understand the reasons for the extraordinary creep lifetime of the H230AM, a detailed comparative analysis of the microstructure of the H230 and H230AM after the creep test at 900 ^\circC with applied 65 MPa stress was conducted. Apparently, the creep cracks in both samples are mainly distributed along grain boundaries perpendicular to the load direction (Fig. 3a, b), which is consistent with the creep failure mode of nickel-based alloys along the grain boundaries at elevated temperatures [41]. Generally, the coarse grains imply that there are fewer grain boundaries available as cavity nucleation sites, which is conductive to lower creep rates and longer creep lifetime [42]. The average grain sizes of H230 and H230AM after heat treatment were 13.2  $ \mu $m and 10.9  $ \mu $m, respectively (Suppl. Fig. 3). The grain size of H230AM (10  $ \mu $m) and H230 (12.4  $ \mu $m) remains essentially unchanged after creep testing, as shown in Fig. 3e and f. It can be seen that the grain size of H230AM is slightly lower than that of H230, and much smaller than that of the corresponding conventional cast H230 counterpart [36]. However, the H230AM exhibits much better creep properties than them, implying that the grain size is not the main cause for the significant difference in the creep lifetime between H230AM and H230 samples. The fracture cross-section microstructures of two samples show that micropores and cracks nucleated along the intergranular large-sized carbides (Fig. 4a, c). Moreover, the content of precipitates in the microstructure of H230AM increased obviously after creep deformation (Fig. 4c). The EDS mapping and SAED patterns confirmed the presence of three types of precipitation phases in H230AM sample, as shown in Fig. 5. Most of the precipitates are ZrC and still maintain their original size and morphology, and M6C carbides with a size of about 380 nm are precipitated along the grain boundaries. In addition, the plate-like Ni_{7}Zr_{2} phase with an average size of about 300 nm, is precipitated within the grain interior (Fig. 4d), and it has an orientation relationship with the \gamma matrix of  $ (001)_{Ni_{7}Zr_{2}} \mathbin{//}(111)_\gamma $ (Fig. 5c). As indicated by arrows in Figs. 4c, M6C carbides precipitated along the grain boundaries in the H230AM sample during the creep deformation, but their size and content are still lower than that in the H230 sample. As revealed by the XRD patterns in Fig. 6a, there are distinct M6C diffraction peaks in the H230 samples, whereas no apparent diffraction peaks of the precipitates are observed in the H230AM samples. In addition, the peaks of the H230AM sample are shifted to the right compared to H230 sample, as shown in Fig. 6b. According to the Bragg formula, the solid solution of W and Zr with large atomic radii can increase the crystal plane spacing of the matrix, while excessive precipitation of M6C carbides can consume the solid solution elements in the matrix that are unfavorable for creep resistance [43]. Although a high number density of grain boundary carbides can provide better resistance to grain boundary sliding and thus reduce the creep rate, large scale incoherent carbides will accelerate the formation of creep cavities thereby shortening the creep lifetime [42]. Moreover, the precipitation-free zone near the grain boundary, as indicated in Fig. 1c, cannot provide a sufficient constraint on the growth of grain boundary cavities [44]. Therefore, the dispersed fine grain boundary ZrC carbides in the microstructure are beneficial for improving the creep lifetime of H230AM.

It has been reported that the distribution of precipitates along the close-packed planes can limit the activation of dislocation movement in the dominant slip system and thus enhance creep resistance [28]. Moreover, uniformly dispersed fine strengthening phases can maintain uniform plastic deformation and alleviate stress concentration at grain boundaries to delay the formation of voids and cracks caused by intergranular cavitation [33,45,46]. Evidently, the volume fraction of ZrC and  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phases within the grain interior of H230AM is much higher than that of the  $ M_{6}C $ in H230, as shown in Fig. 4a, c. Hence, the high-density smaller size of the intragranular ZrC carbide and the  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ precipitates along the close-packed planes (face-centered cubic close-packed planes  $ \{111\} $) contribute to precipitation strengthening, which is favorable for promoting the creep lifetime of H230AM.

Unusually, in high magnification scanning TEM (STEM) image (Fig. 4e), it can be observed that there are lots of parallel stripes in most of the grains. These stripes are likely to have an orientation relationship with the (022) plane of the  $ \gamma $ matrix, combining the direction of the inserted line and the SAED pattern. It is noteworthy that the dislocations undergo periodic bending with the stripes in the matrix, as indicated by the arrows in Fig. 4e, which implies the strong interaction between the dislocations and stripes. Fig. 4f shows the high magnification high annular angle dark field (HAADF) STEM image of the stripes along [011] zone axis, and also displays the Fast-Fourier-transform (FFT) patterns with stripe (Fig. 4f $ _{1} $) and stripe-free (Fig. 4f $ _{2} $) regions. The image shows no new phase formation in the stripe region, and the FFT pattern of the stripe region does not show the distinct superlattice diffraction spots in the (022) plane compared to the stripe-free region, indicating a severe distortion of the {011} planes in this region. The geometric phase analysis (GPA) is used to further analyze the nanoscale strain field in microstructure, as indicated in Fig. 4g. Clearly, the strip region has a more drastic strain float than the other regions. Moreover, the dominant XRD diffraction peak of H230 was transformed from (220) to (200) and the intensity ratio of  $ I_{(200)} / I_{(220)} $ increased by 190 %. However, the dominant XRD diffraction peak of H230AM remains to be (220), and the intensity ratio of  $ I_{(200)} / I_{(220)} $ only increased by 72 % after creep test, as shown in Fig. 6a, c. The increase of the  $ I_{(200)} / I_{(220)} $ value indicates that the crystal orientation tends to change from {011} to {001} during creep. The EBSD-IPF maps also show the crystal orientation intensity is concentrated from [101] to [001] for both samples after creep testing (Suppl. Fig. 3c). Crystal orientation rotation still exists in other alloys and the slow crystal rotation process is beneficial for extending the creep lifetime [47,48]. The crystal rotation during creep is usually accomplished with the assistance of dislocation slip, stacking faults or twinning. Therefore, it is reasonable to speculate that the rotation of the crystal orientation in H230 is mainly accomplished by dislocation long-range slip and climb, whereas in H230AM it is achieved by micro distortions of the crystal. Compared to H230, the H230AM has a slower crystal rotation during creep testing, which can delay the strain localization creep damage caused by crystal orientation rotation, thus increasing the creep life of the alloy [47,49].

Creep is known to be a dynamic competitive process between work hardening and recovery softening. The fact that no cell boundaries are observed in the microstructure after creep proves that significant recovery occurred (Fig. 4b, d). Compared with H230, the delaying effect of these additional matrix distortion stripes on dislocation motion as well as the high resistance to transformation of crystal orientation are both beneficial to maintain the work hardening, thereby reducing creep rate and prolonging steady-state creep stage of H230AM. As discussed above, the dominant mechanisms for the increased creep resistance of H230AM are precipitates-dislocation and matrix distortion-dislocation interaction. Similar matrix distortion stripes in creep testing of nickel-based superalloys have not been reported to the best of our knowledge. We speculate that this may be due to the addition of excess Zr, which acts as a grain-boundary element to promote the annihilation of vacancies and inhibit grain-boundary diffusion [21,50], thus making it difficult to achieve a change in crystal orientation by dislocation long-range slip or climb, and strain is released in the form of localized distortion. As a result, regularly distributed matrix distortion stripes appear in the microstructure.

## 3.3. Stability of microstructure and mechanical properties

As mentioned above, the massive plate-like  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phase precipitated in the microstructure of H230AM sample during creep testing. It has been reported that the precipitation of topologically close-packed (TCP) phases and the coarsening of precipitation phases can cause deterioration of mechanical properties and priority failure of the material [51, 52]. To validate whether the precipitation of the  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phase deteriorates the mechanical properties of the alloy in service, we further investigated the variations in the microstructure stability and mechanical properties after long-term thermal exposure. The average size of  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phase is  $ 293 \pm 58 $ nm and  $ 289 \pm 51 $ nm after thermal exposure at  $ 900\ ^{\circ}C $ for 50 h and 500 h, respectively, and they always maintain uniform distribution in the  $ \gamma $ matrix, as indicated in Fig. 7a and Suppl. Fig. 4, which indicates that the  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phase can precipitate rapidly in the  $ \gamma $ matrix and remain stable during subsequent thermal exposure. This also proves that the  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phase observed in the creep fracture in Fig. 4c is already precipitated at the early stage of creep. The occurrence of phase coarsening requires the diffusion of solutes through the matrix from small to large precipitated phases. Therefore, the low solubility of Zr atoms in the  $ \gamma $ matrix contributes to the rapid precipitation of  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phase from the supersaturated solid solution, and provides thermodynamic barriers for coarsening during long-term thermal exposure [27, 53]. The HAADF-STEM image shows a coherent interface between the  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phase and  $ \gamma $ matrix, and the  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phase precipitates along the (111) plane of the  $ \gamma $ matrix exhibiting the (111)  $ \gamma $ // (001)  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ orientation relationship (Fig. 7b), consistent with SAED pattern in Fig. 5c. The coherent interface favors the reduction of the interface energy and improves the stability of the  $ Ni_{7}Zr_{2} $ phase, which can reduce the cavitation damage during creep deformation [44].

We further investigated the elevated temperature tensile properties of samples after long-term thermal exposure (Table 2 and Suppl. Fig. 4). After heat treatment (HT), the strength and plasticity of H230AM at elevated temperature are higher than that of H230. This is because the high-density fine ZrC particles in the microstructure plays a strengthening role and avoids the premature failure caused by concentration of stress of coarse carbides at grain boundaries. Both H230AM and H230 samples showed a gradual decrease in the strength, and initially increases and then decreases in the ductility with increasing thermal exposure time. The strength of H230AM samples is always higher than that of H230 under the same conditions, and the discrepancy in elongation between these two samples is within 5 %. The Ni-Zr phase has been reported to cause premature failure in cast nickel-based superalloys [27,31], but it is generally distributed at grain boundaries with large dimensions in cast nickel-based superalloys, which is obviously different from the uniformly distributed Ni $ _7 $Zr $ _2 $ phase within the grain interior of LPBF fabricated H230AM in our work. This is attributed to the presence of broad cell boundaries in the LPBF fabricated nickel-based superalloys that provide a large number of sites for the nucleation of Ni $ _7 $Zr $ _2 $ phase. In addition, the H230AM exhibits excellent creep resistance as mentioned above. These results confirm that the precipitation of Ni $ _7 $Zr $ _2 $ phase during long-term thermal exposure does not cause a serious deterioration of the elevated temperature mechanical properties of H230AM.

## 3.4. Long-term isothermal oxidation behavior

Oxidation is also an important factor limiting the long-term service of the alloys at elevated temperature. Fig. 8a presents the mass gain of the samples at 900 ^\circC in the air atmosphere. The results show that the mass gain of H230 fabricated by LPBF is slightly above to that of rolled H230 [34]. The values of H230AM sample are 20 % lower than that of H230 sample, which suggests that the introduction of Zr element enhances the oxidation resistance of the alloy at elevated temperature. The X-ray diffraction (XRD) results show no distinct difference between H230 and H230AM samples in surface oxides, which are mainly (Mn, Ni)Cr₂O₄, Mn₂O₃, and Cr₂O₃ (Fig. 8b). It has been reported that the elements of Zr and Al have similar oxygen affinity (Zr = −923 kJ/mol, Al = −924 kJ/mol) [54]. This indicates that Zr can play a similar role as Al in the oxidation of nickel-based superalloys.

According to the results above, it can be concluded that the enhancement of the oxidation resistance of H230AM is due to the optimization of the inner oxide layer and intergranular oxidation by Zr element addition. The formation of oxides strongly depends on the inward diffusion of  $ O^{2-} $ and outward diffusion of metal cations [55]. The Al and Zr-rich oxides in the internal layers of the H230AM sample indicate that the high activity  $ Al^{3+} $ and  $ Zr^{4+} $ diffuse to the surface to form an oxide layer, which can hinder the further inward diffusion of  $ O^{2-} $. As highlighted in Fig. 9b, the presence of Zr-rich particles free zone on the surface of H230AM indicates that the diffusion of Zr element is significant during the oxidation. The added Zr can further promote the densification of the surface oxide layer and improve its adhesion [13,56,57]. In addition, grain boundaries are also act as fast diffusion channels for  $ O^{2-} $, thus triggering internal oxidation [12]. Owing to the limited Al content in the H230 alloy,  $ O^{2-} $ penetrates further into the grains along the grain boundaries thereby leading to the formation of  $ Cr_{2}O_{3} $ at the periphery of  $ Al_{2}O_{3} $ in H230 (Suppl. 6a). Hence, the Zr with similar activity to Al in H230AM can further promote the densification of intergranular oxide layer, thereby reducing the channels for  $ O^{2-} $ transport.

## 4. Conclusion

In conclusion, we reported a defect-free H230AM alloy fabricated by AM suitable for long-term service at elevated temperatures. Our results indicate that it has an extraordinary creep lifetime and oxidation resistance superior to previously reported similar nickel-based superalloys fabricated by AM, forging or rolling. The introduction of the Zr element effectively suppresses the cracking, allowing H230AM to achieve excellent mechanical properties without the need for HIP treatment. The fine ZrC carbides instead of the coarse grain boundary  $ M_{6}C $ carbides in the H230AM is beneficial for suppressing the formation of grain boundary cavities.
