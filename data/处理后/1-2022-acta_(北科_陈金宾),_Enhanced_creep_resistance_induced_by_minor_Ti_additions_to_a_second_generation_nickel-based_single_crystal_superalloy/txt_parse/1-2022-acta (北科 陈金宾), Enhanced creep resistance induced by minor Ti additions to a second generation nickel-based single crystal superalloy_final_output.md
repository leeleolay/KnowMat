## Abstract

Over the past decades, the evolution of high-performance nickel-based single crystal (SX) superalloys has advanced along the pathway of continuous increment in the Re content, which leads to microstructure instability, as well as increased density and cost. This paper proposes an alternative approach to enhance the creep resistance by slight composition modification instead of increasing the Re content. We systematically investigated the effects of minor Ti additions on the microstructure, lattice misfit, and elemental partitioning behavior of a novel second generation nickel-based SX superalloy, whose creep performance exceeded that of René N_{5}. The addition of 0.5 wt.% Ti resulted in more and finer  $ \gamma' $ precipitates, narrower  $ \gamma $ channels, and a higher magnitude of the  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ lattice misfit. In addition, all partitioning coefficients of Re, Mo, W, and Cr elements in the  $ \gamma $ matrix were increased. Thicker  $ \gamma' $ and thinner  $ \gamma $ lamellae were formed in the raft structure. The creep stain rate decreased significantly, and the creep life extended twice that of the base alloy at 1030 ^\circC and 230 MPa. The temperature capacity of the Ti-containing alloy was superior to that of some commercial second generation SX superalloys and similar to that of René N_{6}. Denser dislocation networks accumulated at the  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ interface, confirming the dislocation strengthening mechanism during the creep process. Stronger solid solution strengthening, impeded cross-slip, and more sluggish diffusion dynamics were considered to contribute to the enhanced creep resistance of the Ti-containing superalloy.

## 1. Introduction

Nickel-based superalloys, which exhibit excellent comprehensive mechanical properties, are critical materials for the hottest sections of advanced aero-engines and industrial gas turbines [1]. However, the requirement of extreme service performance places very strict requirements on the temperature capacity of superalloys [2,3]. The dominating strengthening mechanisms for nickel-based superalloys to resist the degradation caused by thermal and mechanical stress are the solid solution strengthening of the FCC-structure  $ \gamma $ matrix [4] and the precipitation strengthening of the coherently embedded L12-structure  $ \gamma' $ phase [5]. The mechanical properties of these superalloys depend strongly upon their microstructure, which is controlled by their chemical composition and processing conditions [6,7]. On the other hand, the evolution of superalloys, from polycrystalline to single crystal (SX), with completely eliminated grain boundaries, yields further improved temperature capacity.

Compositional optimization is a basic route for developing high-performance superalloys [8–12]. The first, second and third generation SX superalloys have been developed and classified in terms of their Re contents (0 wt.%, ~3 wt.%, and ~6 wt.%) and different contents of Al, Ti, and refractory elements, including Mo, W, and Ta [13–15]. As a crucial element, Re is strongly partitioned in the  $ \gamma $ matrix, and has a very low diffusion coefficient. Hence, Re provides the most effective solid solution strengthening effect and slows down the alloys' diffusion kinetics, which is beneficial to creep resistance [16,17]. However, the increase in Re content is not only detrimental for lightweight component design but also causes microstructural instabilities, including the formation of topologically close-packed (TCP) phases and secondary reaction zones, which are detrimental to long-term service performance at elevated temperatures [18,19]. The solid solution strengthening effect might deteriorate if refractory elements are extracted from the  $ \gamma $ matrix, and microcracks might be formed at the brittle interface of rigid TCP phases. Recently, much attention has been paid to the development of advanced SX superalloys with lower Re or no Re content. For instance, the René N_{515} alloy [20], developed by GE Aviation, contains 1.5 wt.% Re and exhibits creep rupture capability similar to that of René N_{5}. QTSX alloy [21], developed by QuesTek Innovations, contains only 1 wt.% Re and exhibits superior castability and similar or better high-temperature mechanical properties compared to the widely used SX superalloys, such as PWA 1484 and René N_{5}. Cannon-Muskegon reported the Re-free CMSX-7 alloy and low-Re CMSX-8 alloy [22] (1.5 wt.% Re) as alternatives to the first and second generation SX superalloys for applications, respectively.

Ti is a major  $ \gamma' $ forming element. The additions of Ti to Ni-based superalloys can effectively increase the volume fraction and the solvus temperature of the  $ \gamma' $ phase, facilitating the retention of high strength of the alloys at elevated temperatures. In certain Re-free Ni-based superalloys, the distribution of the refractory element W between the  $ \gamma $ and  $ \gamma' $ phases can be influenced by Ti additions [13,23,24], and then, the alloy creep strengths at elevated temperatures can be enhanced due to the higher solid solution strengthening effect. However, Ti additions do not always improve the creep performance of superalloys. For example, the creep strain rate of the ERBO/36-Ti alloy (1 at.% Ti) was increased compared to that of Astra100 (Ti-free) at 1100 ^\circC [13]. In addition, the addition of 2 wt.% Ti to a Re-containing alloy resulted in a similar creep property at 980 ^\circC/248 MPa [25]. Therefore, the actual effects of Ti on the creep performance of diverse superalloys remain uncertain. In contrast, previous studies have tended to eliminate the Ti element in the modern SX superalloys due to its negative effects on oxidation resistance and the retention of thermal barrier coatings (TBCs) [1,26,27]. For example, Tawancy et al. [28] confirmed that the presence of Ti in superalloy substrates can significantly influence the useful life of TBCs, because the Ti-rich oxide particles formed near the bond coat surface degrade the adherence of the thermally grown oxide. Abbasi et al. [29] found that oxide thickness and defect density increased with the Ti content. In addition, the  $ \eta $-Ni $ _{3} $Ti phase can be formed in some Ni-based superalloys when the Ti content exceeds a critical value [30,31]. However, Guan et al. [32] indicated that the increase in Ti content improves the hot corrosion resistance. Thus, the Ti concentration should be limited in Ni-based SX superalloys, considering the potential benefits to alloy strength and the adverse effects on environmental resistance. However, the substantial effects and the intrinsic mechanism of Ti in Ni-based superalloys, especially in SX alloys, remain unclear. In this study, a minor amount of Ti (0.5 wt.%) was added to a novel second generation Ni-based SX superalloy, and its effects on the microstructure, lattice misfit, elemental partitioning behavior, and phase stability were investigated. The Ti-containing alloy exhibited significantly enhanced creep performance. The creep mechanism and strengthening effects are discussed in details. We believe that the study results will provide a new approach to further improve the temperature capacity of nickel-based SX superalloys and reduce alloy cost and density.

## 2. Experimental

Two novel second generation Ni-based SX superalloys (named as T0 and T5 in this paper) were designed as comparative experimental alloys. Their nominal and measured chemical compositions are listed in Table 1. For alloy T5, 0.5 wt.% Ti was added at the expense of Ni compared to the T0 alloy. The nominal contents of all other alloying elements in the two alloys remained the same. The measured compositions corresponded well with the nominal levels, with a maximum deviation of \pm0.2 wt.%.

The as-cast cylindrical SX bars 160 mm in length and 14 mm in diameter were prepared at the Beijing Institute of Aeronautical Materials by a high-rate solidification (HRS) process using spiral grain selectors. The withdrawal rate of directional solidification was 3 mm/min. The crystallographic orientation of the SX bars was measured using the Laue back-reflection X-ray technique, and only the bars whose misorientation was within 6^\circ away from [001] were selected for subsequent creep tests. The primary dendrite arm spacings of alloys T0 and T5 were measured as 345 and 364  $ \mu $m, respectively. High temperature solution treatments for T0 and T5 were performed at 1290 ^\circC and 1300 ^\circC, respectively, for 4 h, followed by air cooling. Then, a two-stage aging treatment, involving first treatment at 1120 ^\circC for 4 h followed by air cooling and the second treatment at 900 ^\circC for 16 h followed by air cooling, was conducted to obtain fine cubical  $ \gamma' $ precipitates. Here, we defined the specimens obtained after the complete heat treatment processing as the alloys in the initial state. The initial SX bars were then machined into tensile creep specimens with a gage length and diameter of 25 and 5 mm, respectively. A set of high temperature creep rupture tests were performed for both alloys at 1030 ^\circC/230 MPa, 1050 ^\circC/190 MPa, 1100 ^\circC/137 MPa, 1100 ^\circC/158 MPa and 980 ^\circC/248 MPa. To analyze the corresponding microstructure and dislocation configuration evolution, the creep test conducted at 1030 ^\circC/230 MPa was interrupted at 1% creep strain. Additionally, cubical samples with an edge length of 10 mm were aged at the creep test temperature of 1030 ^\circC for 0.5 h and 2 h to compare the new microstructure with that available at the beginning of the creep tests.

The metallographic samples in the initial state and those aged at 1030 ^\circC were cut from only the transverse sections of alloy bars, while those for interrupted creep tests were cut from both transverse and longitudinal sections, which were perpendicular and parallel to the applied tensile stress direction in the gage length, respectively. All metallographic samples were manually polished and then electrolytically etched in a solution of H₃PO₄, HNO₃, and H₂SO₄ at 3 V for 3–5 s to expose the \gamma′ precipitates, or chemically etched with a solution of HF, CH₃COOH, HNO₃, and H₂O to expose the \gamma channel. The microstructure observations were then conducted using a ZEISS SUPRA 55 field-emission scanning electron microscope (FE-SEM). The microstructure was quantitatively characterized using the image analysis software Image-Pro Plus 6.0. The area fraction of the \gamma′ phase was measured by image thresholding according to the gray values. In this study, the measured area fractions were adopted to approximately represent the volume fractions. For obtaining statistical results, at least three representative

The dislocation configurations were observed on an FEI Talos F200X transmission electron microscope (TEM) operated at 300 kV, and the thin foils for the TEM were prepared by manually grinding disks of 3 mm diameter to 60  $ \mu $m thickness, and then electrochemically thinned in a twin-jet polisher with a solution of  $ C_{2}H_{5}OH $ and  $ HClO_{4} $ at  $ -25\ ^{\circ}C $ and 30 V.

The lattice constants of the  $ \gamma $ and  $ \gamma' $ phases in alloys in the initial state at room temperature were determined by high-energy synchrotron X-ray diffraction at beamline BL14B1 in the Shanghai Synchrotron Radiation Facility (SSRF) of China, with a beam energy of  $ E = 18 \, \text{keV} \, (\lambda = 0.6887 \, \text{nm}) $. The beam size was  $ 3 \times 3 \, \text{mm}^2 $ and the dimensions of the alloy sample were  $ 10 \, \text{mm} \times 10 \, \text{mm} \times 1 \, \text{mm} $. The (004) reflections were recorded and analyzed by PeakFit software, and then, the  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ lattice misfit ( $ \delta $) was calculated as follows:

 $$ \delta=\frac{2\big(a_{\gamma^{\prime}}-a_{\gamma}\big)}{\big(a_{\gamma^{\prime}}+a_{\gamma}\big)} $$ 

where  $ a_{\gamma'} $ and  $ a_{\gamma} $ are the lattice constants of the  $ \gamma' $ and  $ \gamma $ phases, respectively.

For determining the lattice misfits in the creep-deformed state, cylindrical specimens were cut from the gage segment of the creep samples interrupted at 1% strain. Neutron diffraction was performed on the General Purpose Powder Diffractometer (GPPD) with a 360^\circ rotation stage at the China Spallation Neutron Source. The neutron wavelengths were in the range of 0.1–4.9 \times 10−10 m, and the time-of-flight (TOF) method was used to collect the data. The (004) reflection of the overlapped \gamma and \gamma′ phase peaks in the neutron diffraction spectrum were analyzed by using PeakFit software, and the lattice constants and misfit were calculated.

To analyze the alloying elements’ partitioning behavior in the initial state, needle-shaped specimens for atom probe tomography (APT) were prepared by the lift-out method using an FEI Helios nanolab 600i focused ion beam (FIB) system. For maintaining consistency, the APT specimens for both alloys were lifted from the dendrite cores for consistency and mounted on silicon microtips. The APT experiments were conducted using a CAMECA LEAP-5000XR tomograph operated in the high-voltage pulsing mode, using an energy of 80 pJ of energy per pulse and a pulse repetition rate of 200 kHz. The microtip temperature was maintained at 50 K, and the evaporation rate was 0.5%. Then, the data analysis was performed using the CAMECA IVAS 3.8.0 software. For each alloy, one specimen was measured, and the compositions of the  $ \gamma $ and  $ \gamma' $ phases were derived from an average of multiple proxigrams.

## 3.2. Alloying element partitioning behavior

Fig. 2 shows the alloying element partitioning behavior in the alloys measured by APT. The APT reconstructions in Fig. 2(a) reveal the distribution of alloying element atoms in the  $ \gamma $ and  $ \gamma' $ phases of alloys in the initial state. The color density indicates the relative concentration of the corresponding element atoms. In both alloys, Co, Cr, Mo, and Re are evidently enriched in the  $ \gamma $ matrix, while Al and Ta are partitioned to  $ \gamma' $ precipitates. Meanwhile, W shows no evident preference for either phase. Ti additions in alloy T5 mostly exist in  $ \gamma' $ precipitates. Fig. 2(b) displays the proximity histogram plots of the concentration profiles through the  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ interfaces of alloys T0 and T5. The left and right sides in the plots indicate the compositions of the  $ \gamma $ and  $ \gamma' $ phases, respectively. The transition region within the range of several nanometers is the  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ interface in which the element concentrations change significantly. The width of the interface in alloy T5 is slightly narrower than that in alloy T0. The  $ \gamma $ and  $ \gamma' $ phase compositions are obtained by averaging the concentration along the plateau regions of the proximity histogram away from the  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ interfaces. For a quantitative assessment of the element partitioning behavior, the partitioning coefficients (k) of the alloying elements are calculated as follows:

 $$ k_{i}^{\gamma^{^{\prime}}/\gamma}=\frac{C_{i}^{\gamma^{^{\prime}}}}{C_{i}^{\gamma}} $$ 

where i represents each of the alloying elements, and  $ C_{i}^{\gamma} $ and  $ C_{i}^{\gamma} $ correspond to the atomic concentration of element i in the  $ \gamma $ and  $ \gamma' $ phases, respectively. ## 3.3. High-temperature creep properties

Fig. 3 illustrates the strain–time curves, strain rate–time curves, and strain rate–strain curves of the creep tests for alloys T0 and T5 at 1030 ^\circC and 230 MPa. ## 3.5. Microstructure evolution after creep deformation

Fig. 5 shows the microstructures in the transverse and longitudinal sections of the creep specimens interrupted at 1% strain at 1030 ^\circC and 230 MPa. In both sections, some changes are observed in the morphologies of the  $ \gamma $ and  $ \gamma' $ phases compared to those in the initial state. In the transverse section, considerable coarsening and coalescence of the  $ \gamma' $ phase occurs in a wide range, and only a few individual  $ \gamma' $ precipitates are left. Additionally, the  $ \gamma' $ volume fraction is apparently decreased, indicating that considerable  $ \gamma' $ precipitates are dissolved under the applied stress at elevated temperatures. The volume fractions of  $ \gamma' $ are measured as 44.4% and 46.8% in the interrupted alloys T0 and T5, respectively. In the longitudinal section, an N-type raft structure is formed perpendicular to the applied stress due to the negative misfit in both alloys. The degree of orientation  $ \Omega $, which is commonly used to quantify the perfection degree of the raft structure lamellar in superalloys [35,36], was first suggested by Underwood et al. [37] and can be defined as follows:

 $$ \Omega=\frac{P_{\perp}-P_{\parallel}}{P_{\perp}+P_{\parallel}} $$ 

where P is the number of line intersections with  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ interfaces per unit length in the perpendicular ( $ \perp $) and parallel ( $ \parallel $) scan directions to the lamella, respectively. The  $ \Omega $ values of the interrupted microstructures in alloys T0 and T5 are calculated as 0.67 and 0.73, respectively, indicating that the raft structure in alloy T5 is better than that in alloy T0. The thickness values of the rafted  $ \gamma $ and  $ \gamma' $ lamellae in alloy T0 are measured as 0.215 and 0.223  $ \mu $m, while they are 0.192 and 0.231  $ \mu $m in alloy T5, respectively.

Furthermore, the configuration of dislocations in the interrupted creep test specimens is analyzed. Fig. 6(a) and (b) show the morphologies of dislocation networks accumulated at the  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ phase interface in the [001] and [110] orientations, respectively. The interfacial dislocation networks in alloy T5 are observed to be much denser than those in alloy T0. The mean dislocation spacings in the dislocation networks are measured as 60.5 and 45.1 nm in alloys T0 and T5, respectively.

## 4. Discussion

As seen in Section 3.3, the creep resistance is improved in alloy T5 through minor Ti additions to the base alloy. The increase in creep rupture life is mainly attributed to the extension of the steady creep stage, which is controlled by a rather low minimum creep rate. The creep test results under the five individual conditions of alloys T5 and T0 are indicated as the red stars and black circles, respectively, in Fig. 7. For comparison, certain commercial SX superalloys are also plotted in this figure. The data points of creep rupture life at high temperature and low stress for alloy T5 are located significantly above the second generation SX alloys, such as René N_{5} [38], PWA1484 [21], and CMSX-4 [39], but are close to and even slightly above the curves of René N_{6} [38] and TMS-75 [40] (more clearly displayed in the inset graph of Fig. 7). The Larson–Miller parameters of alloy T5 approach those of CMSX-10 [41] at 1100 ^\circC. These results indicate that the temperature capacity of alloy T5 is superior to those of typical second generation SX superalloys and is similar to or even better than those of some third generation SX superalloys. This superiority is particularly true in the high temperature and low stress creep regime. A significant reduction in alloy density and cost is realized by the proposed low-Re strategy. 4.2. Stability of  $ \gamma' $ phase at elevated temperatures

In terms of the characteristic parameters of the  $ \gamma $ matrix and  $ \gamma' $ precipitates in the initial state listed in Table 2, the volume fractions of  $ \gamma' $ precipitates in the dendrite core and interdendritic region of alloy T5 are both larger than those in alloy T0. In addition, the mean size of  $ \gamma' $ precipitate in the dendrite core and interdendritic region decreases from 332 and 344 nm in alloy T0 to 302 and 322 nm in alloy T5, respectively. Based on the compositions of the  $ \gamma' $ and  $ \gamma' $ phases derived from the APT analysis, the volume fraction of  $ \gamma' $ can be obtained using the lever rule and expressed as

 $$ f_{\gamma^{\prime}}=\left(C_{i}^{n}\mathrm{~-~}C_{i}^{\gamma}\right)/\left(C_{i}^{\gamma^{\prime}}-C_{i}^{\gamma}\right) $$ 

where  $ C_{i}^{n} $ is the atomic concentration of element i in the measured bulk composition of the alloy. Fig. 8 shows the lever rule plots of alloys T0 and T5, and the calculated volume fractions of the  $ \gamma' $ phase in alloys T0 and T5 are 69.7% and 74.0%, respectively. The values calculated based on the lever rule coincide well with those obtained from the SEM image analysis. These results demonstrate that minor Ti additions promote the precipitation of the  $ \gamma' $ phase. This is acceptable because of the increased total concentration of  $ \gamma' $ forming elements. The volume fraction of  $ \gamma' $ in the interdentritic region is larger than that in the dendrite core, as there are more  $ \gamma' $ formers Al, Ta, and Ti. In addition, with the addition of 0.5 wt.% Ti, the widths of the  $ \gamma $ channel in the dendrite cores and interdendritic regions narrow down from 50.2 and 51.4 nm to 42.9 and 47.5 nm. The geometrical relationship clearly indicates that the higher volume fraction and smaller size of  $ \gamma' $ precipitate lead to smaller spacing (i.e., a narrower  $ \gamma $ channel). The Orowan force resistant to dislocation motion through the  $ \gamma $ matrix is negatively correlated with the channel width [42]. Therefore, the initial microstructure with finer  $ \gamma' $ precipitates and thinner  $ \gamma $ channels in alloy T5 is more desirable for high temperature strengthening.

The volume fractions of  $ \gamma' $ precipitates in the dendrite cores and interdendritic regions of both alloys (Fig. 1) decrease after they are subjected to aging at 1030 ^\circC, as shown in Fig. 9, which is caused by the dissolution of the  $ \gamma' $ phase at high temperature toward a new equilibrium. In addition, continuous coarsening of  $ \gamma' $ precipitates and broadening of  $ \gamma $ matrix channels occur. The dissolution of the  $ \gamma' $ phase and the compositional changes in the  $ \gamma' $ and  $ \gamma $ phases with increasing temperature weaken the resistance to deformation. The decrease in  $ \gamma' $ volume fraction in the dendrite core and interdendritic region of alloy T0 is faster than that in alloy T5. The difference in  $ \gamma' $ volume fraction between the two alloys further increases after aging at 1030 ^\circC for 0.5 h, which is regarded as the start point of creep deformation. The amount of  $ \gamma' $ phase present at elevated temperatures is important to creep resistance. A minor addition of Ti in alloy T5 is conducive to the preservation of the  $ \gamma' $ phase and accordingly enhances the creep strength.

## 4.4. Mechanisms for enhanced creep resistance

To understand the creep deformation mechanism in detail, the creep behaviors of alloys T0 and T5 at 1030 ^\circC and 230 MPa are analyzed. The time required to reach the minimum creep rate is increased for alloy T5 compared to alloy T0, as shown in Fig. 3(b). The accumulated strain for the minimum creep rate of T5 extends from 0.6% to 0.8%, as shown in Fig. 3(c). The lower minimum creep strain rate for alloy T5 is evidently responsible for the extended creep rupture life. In addition, more dislocations are required to relieve the coherency stress with a higher  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ misfit, and the primary creep is consequently extended. This also contributes to the extended overall creep rupture life.

The volume fraction of the  $ \gamma' $ phase in both alloys decreases under the applied tensile stress at elevated temperatures, and an N-type raft structure is formed. Although T5 experiences a much longer period of creep deformation process, the remaining content of  $ \gamma' $ phase in the interrupted specimen of alloy T5 is still more than that in that of alloy T0. This result verifies that Ti partitioning in the  $ \gamma' $ phase stabilizes a larger  $ \gamma' $ volume fraction at elevated temperatures, which is consistent with the case shown in Fig. 9.

The rafting degree in the longitudinal section of alloy T5 after 1% creep deformation is more severe than that in alloy T0. The raft structure is conducive to creep strengthening in the high temperature and low stress regime, as the thermally activated climbing of dislocations to bypass the  $ \gamma $ phase is retarded for the extended climb distance in the raft structure. The rafted  $ \gamma' $ lamella in alloy T5 is thicker than that in alloy T0 and, correspondingly, the width of the rafted  $ \gamma $ channel in alloy T5 is less than that in alloy T0. Tian et al. [51] manifested that the creep strain rate during the steady stage reduces as the thickness of the rafted  $ \gamma' $ phase increases in consideration of the dislocation climbing process. Kondo et al. [52] found that the minimum creep rate is linearly correlated to the thickness of the  $ \gamma $ channel in the creep test because the dislocation curvature would increase in the thicker  $ \gamma $ channel, resulting in a decrease in creep resistance. Therefore, the thinner  $ \gamma $ lamellae and thicker rafted  $ \gamma' $ lamellae, as well as the higher rafting degree in alloy T5, all account for the sluggish dislocation motion; that is, the duration of the steady creep stage is effectively extended.

The higher lattice misfit in alloy T5 leads to a denser  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ interfacial dislocation network, which significantly contributes to the superior creep strength. In the early stage of creep deformation, the coherency featured by the misfit of the  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ interface decreases and reaches saturation when rafts are formed [53]. According to the result of neutron diffraction, together with the estimation by dislocation spacings, the magnitude of the unconstrained lattice misfit in alloy T5 interrupted at 1% strain is larger than that in alloy T0. The higher magnitude of lattice misfit in alloy T5 can yield a higher coherency stress. Under the application of an external tensile stress, the net stress in the  $ \gamma $ channels is lower, resulting in slower dislocation activity in the  $ \gamma $ channels, which is beneficial to creep resistance. In the case of the raft structure, the accumulated interfacial dislocations can also provide resistance to the dislocation motion in the  $ \gamma $ channels and prevent the slip dislocation from moving to the  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ interface and shearing the rafted  $ \gamma' $ phase [54].

## 5. Conclusions

Based on the above results and analysis, the following main conclusions can be drawn:

The addition of 0.5 wt.% Ti to the second generation Ni-based SX superalloy results in increased  $ \gamma' $ volume fraction, finer  $ \gamma' $ precipitates, thinner  $ \gamma $ matrix channels, and higher magnitude of  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ lattice misfit. The partitioning coefficients of the solid solution strengthening elements Re, Mo, W, and Cr are increased as a result of minor Ti additions.

A raft structure with thicker  $ \gamma' $ and thinner  $ \gamma $ lamellae is formed in the Ti-containing alloy after creep at 1030 ^\circC. The stability of the  $ \gamma' $ phase in this alloy is enhanced, and minor Ti additions stabilize a larger  $ \gamma' $ volume fraction at elevated temperatures.

In the high temperature creep regime, the minimum creep strain rate of the Ti-containing alloy is decreased to one-third that of the Ti-free alloy, which extends the creep rupture life by a factor of approximately two. As evaluated by the Larson–Miller parameter, the Ti-containing alloy shows superior temperature capacity to typical second generation SX superalloys and in the range of the third generation SX superalloys, which contain twice the Re content as that contained by second generation SX superalloys.

Denser dislocation networks are accumulated at the  $ \gamma/\gamma' $ interface in the Ti-containing alloy, confirming the higher magnitude of the unconstrained lattice misfit. The calculation results show that the Ti-containing alloy exhibits comparable solid solution strengthening and cross-slip-induced hardening effect and lower effective diffusivity than René N_{6}. All solid solution strengthening, precipitation strengthening, and sluggish diffusion dynamics contribute to enhanced creep resistance at elevated temperatures.

Two novel second generation Ni-based SX superalloys (named as T0 and T5 in this paper) were designed as comparative experimental alloys. Their nominal and measured chemical compositions are listed in Table 1. For alloy T5, 0.5 wt.% Ti was added at the expense of Ni compared to the T0 alloy. The nominal contents of all other alloying elements in the two alloys remained the same. The measured compositions corresponded well with the nominal levels, with a maximum deviation of \pm0.2 wt.%.

## 3.1. Microstructures in the initial state and after aging at 1030 ^\circC

A raft structure with thicker  $ \gamma' $ and thinner  $ \gamma $ lamellae is formed in the Ti-containing alloy after creep at 1030 ^\circC. The stability of the  $ \gamma' $ phase in this alloy is enhanced, and minor Ti additions stabilize a larger  $ \gamma' $ volume fraction at elevated temperatures.
